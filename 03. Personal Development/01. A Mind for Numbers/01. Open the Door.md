# Chapter 1: Open the Door

## The Story: From Math-Phobe to Engineering Professor

The author of this book — now an engineering professor — grew up hating math and science. She flunked her way through high school math, and didn't start studying trigonometry (remedial trigonometry) until she was twenty-six.

As a kid, she couldn't even make sense of a clock face — why did the *little* hand point to the hour, when the hour seemed like the more important number? She was constantly confused by things like this, and concluded the simplest explanation: she just wasn't smart, at least not in that "technical" way.

**What she didn't realize at the time:** that self-image — "I'm not a math person" — was actively shaping her life and choices. She'd come to treat numbers and equations like something to avoid entirely. She didn't yet know there were simple mental strategies that could have made math click — strategies that help people who are bad at math *and* people who are already good at it.

Her real problem, she'd later understand, was that she'd only ever learned to use *one* mode of thinking to approach a subject like math — and that mode wasn't the right one for it.

### Why This Happens So Easily

Math, as it's typically taught, builds in a strict logical sequence — addition, subtraction, multiplication, division, and upward from there. Miss one step along the way, and everything built on top of it becomes shaky. And missing a step is *easy*: a rough patch at home, a burned-out teacher, even a couple of weeks of illness at the wrong moment can throw the whole sequence off.

For the author, it was a mix of things: a family crisis in seventh grade (her father lost his job after a back injury), a new school with an unhelpful math teacher who offered zero explanation, and a chemistry teacher who — during her very first experiment — quietly gave her lab group different materials than everyone else, then mocked them when their results didn't match the rest of the class. By that point, she didn't just dislike math and science — she actively resented them. When her parents encouraged her to get extra help, she refused, convinced the subjects were pointless anyway. Her way of "winning" against required coursework was simply refusing to engage with it at all, and failing every test on principle.

### What Actually Kept Her Afloat

She did have real strengths: history, social studies, culture, and especially language. Straight out of high school, she enlisted in the Army specifically because they'd pay her to learn a new language. She picked Russian on a whim — and turned out to be genuinely excellent at it, good enough that people sometimes mistook her for a native speaker. That earned her an ROTC scholarship, and she went on to graduate with honors in Slavic languages and literature from the University of Washington.

**A pattern worth noticing here:** the better she got at Russian, the more she enjoyed it — and the more she enjoyed it, the more time she spent on it. Success fed motivation, and motivation fed more success. It became a self-reinforcing loop.

### The Turning Point

Then came a twist nobody would have predicted: she was commissioned as a second lieutenant in the U.S. Army Signal Corps — a role that suddenly demanded real expertise in radio, cable, and telephone switching systems. Overnight, she went from being a confident expert in her field to a near-total beginner in a highly technical one. She finished dead last in her required electronics training, then shipped out to West Germany as a distinctly unimpressive communications platoon leader.

But she noticed something important while there: the officers and enlisted members who *were* technically skilled were genuinely in demand. They solved real problems, and their skill helped everyone else succeed.

Reflecting on her own career path, she realized she'd only ever followed the interests she already had — without ever staying open to developing new ones. That had quietly boxed her in. Staying in the Army meant her weak technical skills would always hold her back. Leaving meant competing for entry-level jobs against a sea of other people with a generic liberal arts degree — there simply aren't many openings specifically for Russian linguists.

### The Decision to Retrain Her Brain

She had one more option: GI Bill benefits she could put toward more schooling. So she asked herself a genuinely radical question — could she deliberately retrain her own brain? Go from someone who feared math to someone who genuinely loved it?

She'd never heard of anyone doing this before, especially starting from as deep a hole as she was in. But she'd seen firsthand, through her colleagues, exactly what kind of doors real technical skill could open. It became a genuine challenge she couldn't resist — so she committed to it.

**It was not easy at first.** Her early semesters were genuinely frustrating — she felt like she was fumbling in the dark while classmates around her seemed to just *see* the answers naturally. But slowly, she started figuring out what she'd been doing wrong.

### The Real Lessons She Learned Along the Way

A few concrete things shifted for her as she relearned how to learn:

- **She'd been putting in effort the wrong way** — she compares it to trying to lift a piece of lumber while standing on top of it. Effort alone wasn't the issue; the *method* was.
- **She learned not just how to study, but when to actually stop** — recognizing when to step back from a problem, rather than grinding at it indefinitely.
- **Internalizing key concepts and techniques** — really absorbing them, not just memorizing them — turned out to be a genuinely powerful tool.
- **She learned not to overload herself.** She deliberately took fewer courses per semester than her classmates, giving herself real time to actually practice — even though it meant graduating later than the people around her.

As she gradually got better at *how* to learn math and science, the subjects themselves got easier. And — just like with Russian — the better she got, the more she genuinely enjoyed it.

### Where She Ended Up

She went on to earn a bachelor's degree in electrical engineering, then a master's in electrical and computer engineering, and eventually a doctorate in systems engineering — covering thermodynamics, electromagnetics, acoustics, and physical chemistry along the way. The further she went, the better she performed — by her doctoral studies, she was earning consistently strong grades. (Not effortlessly — the work still took real effort. But by then, she knew exactly *what* work needed doing.)

## Why This Book Exists

As an engineering professor, the author became genuinely interested in how the brain actually works — partly because engineering underlies the medical imaging technology used to study the brain in the first place. That gave her real insight into *how* and *why* she'd been able to change her own thinking — and how she could help others learn the same way, without the years of unnecessary struggle she went through.

**A core idea worth sitting with:** the brain is already capable of extraordinary calculation — you do it every time you catch a ball, sway to a beat, or steer around a pothole. We solve genuinely complex problems unconsciously, all the time, often arriving at a solution before we've consciously worked out how we got there. Everyone has a natural aptitude for math and science in this sense — what's actually needed is learning the specific "language and culture" of these fields, not some innate gift most people simply lack.

**Where the book's techniques actually come from:** while researching it, the author interviewed hundreds of leading professors across math, physics, chemistry, biology, and engineering, as well as education, psychology, neuroscience, and fields like business and health sciences. A striking pattern emerged — many of these top experts had personally used the exact techniques covered in this book while learning their own fields, and had passed the same techniques on to their own students. But because these methods can seem counterintuitive (sometimes even irrational-sounding), many instructors have struggled to explain them clearly — and some admitted to sharing their own "secret" methods almost sheepishly, not realizing how many other top teachers relied on the very same approaches. This book gathers those insights in one place, alongside input from ordinary students facing the same time constraints and struggles as most readers.

## Who This Book Is For

This book is written for math experts and math-phobes alike — regardless of your past grades, or how good or bad you currently believe you are at these subjects. Its aim is to make your own thought process visible: how your mind actually learns, and importantly, how it can sometimes trick you into *feeling* like you're learning when you're not. It includes hands-on exercises you can apply directly to whatever you're currently studying.

- If you're already strong in math or science, these insights can sharpen you further — more enjoyment, more creativity, more elegant problem-solving.
- If you're convinced you simply don't have "the knack" for numbers or science, there's genuine reason for hope. Following concrete, evidence-based tips on how learning actually works can produce real, sometimes surprising change.

And the broader payoff extends well past math and science — these same tools tend to make you more effective and more creative in nearly everything you do.
