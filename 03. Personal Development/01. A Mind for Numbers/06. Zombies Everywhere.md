# Chapter 6: Zombies Everywhere — Digging Deeper into the Habit of Procrastination

## Lisa Allen's Turnaround

In *The Power of Habit*, Charles Duhigg tells the story of Lisa Allen — a woman who'd struggled with her weight for years, started drinking and smoking at sixteen, watched her husband leave her for someone else, never held a job longer than a year, and sank deep into debt.

In just four years, Lisa completely turned her life around: lost sixty pounds, worked toward a master's degree, quit drinking and smoking, and got fit enough to run a marathon.

Understanding *how* she did that starts with understanding habit itself.

## What Habit Actually Is

Habit is simply your brain switching into a preprogrammed "zombie" mode — and it's directly related to **chunking**, the automatic neural pattern that forms from repeated practice. Habit is an energy-saver: it frees your conscious mind for other things. The first time you backed a car out of a driveway, it felt overwhelmingly complex — a flood of information demanding full attention. Now, you just think "let's go," and it happens almost automatically. Your brain has chunked the whole process, and slipped into zombie mode — no longer consciously tracking every detail.

You enter this habitual zombie mode far more than you'd think — that's genuinely the point of a habit: it lets you *not* consciously focus on what you're doing, saving real mental energy. Habits can be brief (a passing smile at a stranger) or extended (a run, or a few hours of TV after work).

**Every habit has four parts:**

1. **The Cue** — the trigger that launches zombie mode. Could be as simple as seeing the first item on your to-do list, or a text from a friend. A cue itself is neutral — neither good nor bad. What matters is the *routine* you fall into in response.
2. **The Routine** — your actual zombie-mode response to the cue. Can be harmless, useful, or, at worst, genuinely destructive.
3. **The Reward** — habits persist because they pay off with some small dose of pleasure. Procrastination is an easy habit to build precisely because the reward (shifting attention to something more pleasant) arrives so quickly. Good habits can be rewarded too — and finding real rewards for good study habits is key to actually beating procrastination.
4. **The Belief** — a habit's real power comes from your belief in it. If you believe you'll *never* stop putting off studying until late in the day, that belief itself becomes part of what keeps the habit alive. Changing the habit means changing that underlying belief.

> **Katherine Folk, freshman, industrial and systems engineering:**
> "When I can't bring myself to start something, going for a quick run or doing something active first makes it much easier to actually start once I come back to it."

## Harnessing Your Zombies to Work For You

The goal here isn't a full personality overhaul — just overwriting *parts* of existing habits, and building a few new ones, while using as little willpower as possible. **The real trick is finding the pressure point: your reaction to the cue.** That reaction is the *only* place you actually need to apply willpower.

### 1. The Cue — Recognize What Triggers You

Cues generally fall into a few categories: **location, time, how you feel, other people's reactions, or something that just happened.** Do you look something up online and end up web surfing without meaning to? Does one stray text derail your focus for ten minutes, even when you're trying to stay on task? Because procrastination is automatic, you're often not even aware it's started.

Building *new* cues genuinely helps — starting homework the moment you get home, or right after your first class break. As procrastination researcher Piers Steel puts it: **"If you protect your routine, eventually it will protect you."**

**Practical ways to blunt the most damaging cues:** turn off your phone, or step away from the internet, during focused work blocks (a 25-minute Pomodoro session, for instance). One student, Yusra Hasan, hands her phone and laptop to her sister to "watch over" while she studies — a clever double move, since publicly announcing your intent to study *while* removing the temptation reinforces the commitment. Enlist friends and family — they can genuinely help here.

### 2. The Routine — Have a Plan for the Pressure Point

If your default routine is diverting attention to something less unpleasant the moment a cue hits, your brain will default there automatically unless you deliberately rewire it. **Rewiring needs a real plan.** Some students leave their phone in the car before class specifically to remove that distraction. Others find real value in a quiet library spot, or simply sitting in a favorite chair at a set time with the internet disconnected.

Your plan won't work perfectly right away — adjust it as needed, and genuinely enjoy the wins when it does work. Don't try to overhaul everything at once. The **Pomodoro technique** (a 25-minute timer, covered in more depth below) is especially useful for shifting your reaction to a cue.

**A small physical tip:** have something in your stomach before tackling a genuinely hard task — it ensures you have the mental energy for that initial burst of willpower, and removes "I'll just go grab a snack" as a convenient escape hatch.

### 3. The Reward — Find What Actually Motivates You

This one sometimes takes real investigation. Why are you actually procrastinating? Could you substitute in a different emotional payoff — pride in completing something small, a sense of satisfaction, winning a little personal bet you've turned into a game? Let yourself genuinely enjoy a coffee, a favorite website, or a guilt-free evening of mindless TV as an earned reward. For bigger achievements, consider a bigger reward — movie tickets, something you've genuinely wanted.

> **Charlene Brisson, psychology major, accelerated second-degree nursing program:**
> "My boyfriend and I love movies, so as a reward for completing certain tasks on schedule, he takes me to a movie. It's not just motivating — it's actually helped me build new studying habits by reinforcing the whole cue/routine/reward loop."

**Habits are powerful because they create genuine neurological cravings.** Adding a new reward helps override an old craving — but the real rewiring only happens once your brain genuinely starts *expecting* that new reward. Even a small internal "attaboy" or "attagirl" jump-starts this rewiring — a process sometimes called **learned industriousness**, which gradually makes previously boring tasks feel genuinely more interesting. Simply getting into a flow of work can become its own reward — often producing a sense of productivity you wouldn't have predicted when you first sat down. Setting a reward tied to a specific time (lunch with a friend at noon, stopping the main task at 5 p.m.) creates a useful mini-deadline that helps spur real work.

**Don't worry if flow doesn't come easily at first.** It can genuinely take a few days of drudgery, cycling through several Pomodoros, before real flow starts to unfold in a new area — and remember, competence tends to breed enjoyment, not the other way around.

### 4. The Belief — You Have to Actually Believe It Will Work

The single most important part of breaking a procrastination habit is genuinely believing you *can*. When things get stressful, the pull back toward old, comfortable habits gets strong — belief in your new system is what actually carries you through. Part of building that belief comes from your surroundings — spend time with classmates who genuinely embody the "can-do" attitude you're trying to build. A supportive circle of like-minded people helps you hold onto values you might otherwise forget in a weak moment.

**A genuinely powerful technique here: mental contrasting.** Picture where you want to be — say, working as a doctor, taking a vacation you can actually afford — then deliberately contrast that image with your actual current reality: the beat-up car, the instant mac and cheese, the mountain of student debt. **It's specifically the contrast** between the vision and the current reality that creates the motivating power. Placing images around your workspace that represent where you want to be can help prime this — just be sure to genuinely hold the contrast with your current, more mundane reality alongside it. You genuinely can change that reality.

> **Mike Orrell, junior, electrical engineering:**
> "Mental contrasting is great — I've used it since I was a kid, and I think anyone can apply it to different situations. I once spent months stuck working at a chicken supplier factory in Maryland during a brutal summer, and right there, I decided I was going to school for my degree. That experience became my mental contrast ever since. Sometimes it just takes one bad day to spark an important realization — after that, staying focused on the way out gets a lot easier."

### Try It: Zombie Wrangling

Do you check email or social media the moment you wake up? Try setting a timer for ten minutes of real work first *instead* — then reward yourself with that online time afterward. This small exercise in self-control genuinely builds power over your zombies for the rest of the day.

**Warning:** the first time you try this, some part of you will scream to check your phone "just this once." Ignore it — and try to genuinely laugh at your own zombies' predictable protests instead of taking them seriously.

## Focus on Process, Not Product

If you're avoiding a task because it makes you uncomfortable, here's a powerful reframe: **focus on process, not product.**

**Process** is simply the flow of time and the habits attached to it — "I'm going to spend twenty minutes working." **Product** is the outcome — the finished homework assignment itself.

**Avoiding procrastination means avoiding a focus on the product.** Instead, put your attention on building the *process* — the habit — that happens to let you get the unpleasant task done along the way. Say you dislike math homework, and keep putting it off, telling yourself "it's only five problems, how hard can it be" — while deep down, the whole task genuinely feels daunting, which is exactly why it's easier to imagine finishing it at the last minute instead.

**The trick is avoiding a focus on the product** — the finished problem set — since that's exactly what triggers the pain that fuels procrastination in the first place. Instead, focus on the process: the small, regular chunks of time spent over days or weeks. It genuinely doesn't matter whether you finish the homework, or fully grasp a concept, in any single sitting — the entire point is simply showing up and putting in your best calm effort for a short stretch. That's the process.

**Why this works:** the habitual, zombie part of your brain genuinely prefers a process it can march through mindlessly — it's far easier to recruit a helpful zombie habit around a *process* than around a distant, intimidating *product*.

> **Forrest Newman, Professor of Astronomy and Physics, Sacramento City College:**
> "It's a good idea to mark your daily reading goal with a bookmark or Post-it note. This gives you immediate visual feedback on progress — you're more motivated when you can actually see the finish line."

## Break Work into Bite-Sized Pieces: The Pomodoro Technique

The **Pomodoro technique** — named after the tomato-shaped kitchen timer its creator, Francesco Cirillo, originally used in the 1980s — is built specifically for short bursts of focused attention. Set a timer for **25 minutes**. Once it starts, you're on the clock — no sneaking off to browse, chat, or text. A nice side benefit: if you're working around friends or family, you can simply tell them you're "doing a Pomodoro" — a friendly, socially acceptable reason for them to leave you alone.

**Working under a timer might feel stressful — but research suggests this is actually useful.** Learning under mild stress makes you noticeably better at handling *larger* stress later. In *Choke*, researcher Sian Beilock describes how golfers who practice putting in front of an audience don't choke later during real competition — the mild pressure builds real resilience. The same holds for studying under a modest time crunch: it makes you far less likely to freeze up during a genuinely high-pressure exam. Top performers across wildly different fields — surgery, computer programming — often deliberately seek out coaches who put them under real pressure specifically to build this resilience.

**When you first try Pomodoro, expect a strong urge to peek at something unrelated** — but you'll also likely be pleasantly surprised how easily you can catch yourself and redirect. Twenty-five minutes is short enough that almost anyone can sustain focus for it. When the timer ends, genuinely enjoy that small sense of accomplishment.

> **Joseph Coyne, junior, history:**
> "One helpful tip: just get started. Simple advice, but once you're off to a good start, everything else gets easier. I like the quiet floor of the library — seeing other people in the same boat helps me visualize doing the same thing myself."

**When distraction inevitably rises mid-Pomodoro, train yourself to ignore it.** This is genuinely one of the single most important pieces of procrastination advice: **ignore distractions when they arise, rather than trying to prevent yourself from ever feeling the pull toward them.** That said, minimizing distraction in the first place still helps — many students find a quiet space, noise-canceling headphones, or both, genuinely invaluable for real concentration.

> **Bill Zettler, Professor of Biology, University of Florida:**
> "I was born without auditory canals, so I'm deaf. When I study, off comes the hearing aid, and I can *really* focus — I genuinely love this about my situation. I took an IQ test at the end of first grade and scored 90, well below average — my mom was dismayed, but I was thrilled, since I thought I'd gotten an 'A.' No idea what my IQ actually is now — probably dropped a bit since I can hear again. Thank God for on/off switches."

**How soon should you start another Pomodoro?** Depends on the situation. If a deadline is weeks away, reward yourself with a genuine half hour of guilt-free browsing. Under real time pressure with a lot due, a two-to-five-minute breather might have to suffice. You can alternate timed Pomodoro sessions with regular, untimed work sessions — and if you notice yourself drifting, just put the timer back on.

**Why timer-based systems work so well:** they push simple, focused effort to the forefront, letting you disconnect from fixating on any single item, and settle into a kind of automatic flow without worrying about finishing. This seems to actually make diffuse-mode thinking more accessible. Focusing on process instead of product also lets you stop constantly judging yourself ("am I getting closer to done?") and just relax into the actual work — genuinely useful for procrastination in writing-heavy classes too, not just math and science.

## Why Multitasking Actively Hurts You

**Multitasking is a bit like constantly pulling up a plant to check its roots** — constantly shifting your attention means new ideas never get the chance to actually take root. Multitasking while studying makes you tired faster; every tiny attention-shift, however small it feels, quietly drains real energy. The cumulative effect: you accomplish noticeably less for the same effort, remember material worse, make more mistakes, and struggle more to apply what little you did retain to new situations. Unsurprisingly, students who habitually multitask while studying or in class consistently earn lower grades on average.

**Procrastination often hides inside seemingly small, unimportant tasks** — sharpening a pencil, tidying a desk — partly because these still deliver a small, genuine hit of "accomplishment," even though they don't actually move the real work forward. Your mind is quietly tricking you here — which is exactly why keeping an experiment notebook (covered soon) is so useful for catching the pattern.

### Try It: Ignorance Is Bliss

Next time the urge to check messages hits, pause, actually notice the feeling, acknowledge it — and then simply ignore it. Practicing *ignoring* a distraction is a genuinely far more powerful skill than trying to will yourself into never feeling the pull in the first place.

## Summing It Up

- A little work on something that feels genuinely painful can pay off enormously.
- Habits like procrastination have four parts: **cue, routine, reward,** and **belief**.
- Change a habit by altering your response to its cue, or removing the cue entirely — and lock the change in with a genuine reward and real belief that it works.
- Focus on **process** (how you spend your time) rather than **product** (what you're trying to finish).
- Use the **25-minute Pomodoro** to sustain focus in short bursts, rewarding yourself after each successful stretch.
- Schedule genuine free time to nurture diffuse mode.
- **Mental contrasting** — vividly comparing your current reality against your desired future — is a genuinely powerful motivator.
- **Multitasking prevents your brain from making rich, lasting connections**, since the connection-building process keeps getting interrupted before it can properly settle.

## Key Takeaways, Tips, and Tricks

- **Every habit — including procrastination — has four parts:** cue, routine, reward, belief. You only need willpower at one point: changing your *reaction* to the cue.
- **Identify your personal procrastination cues** — location, time of day, mood, other people, or a specific trigger event — and design around them (turning off your phone, handing devices to someone else, changing your environment).
- **Build a specific plan for the pressure point**, not a vague intention — a set spot, a set time, devices physically out of reach.
- **Eat something before tackling a hard task**, so you have the mental energy for that initial push, and no excuse to wander off for a snack.
- **Choose a genuine reward** tied to finishing a task — small for small wins, bigger for bigger ones — and let yourself actually enjoy it guilt-free.
- **Believe the new system will work**, and surround yourself with people who reinforce that belief — it matters as much as the mechanics.
- **Use mental contrasting**: vividly picture your desired future, then vividly contrast it against your current reality — the gap itself is the motivator.
- **Focus on process, not product.** Commit to a block of time, not a finished outcome — this is far easier for your habitual brain to actually latch onto.
- **Use the Pomodoro technique**: 25 minutes of focused work, no distractions, then a real reward. Tell people around you you're "on the clock" so they leave you alone.
- **When distraction hits, don't fight the feeling — just ignore it and keep going.** Learning to ignore distraction is more powerful than trying not to feel it at all.
- **Avoid multitasking.** Constant attention-switching drains energy, weakens memory, increases mistakes, and lowers grades — even though each switch feels harmless in the moment.
- **Watch for "productive-feeling" busywork** (tidying, small unrelated tasks) that quietly substitutes for the real task while still giving a false sense of accomplishment.
- **If you feel foggy while studying, get up and move** — a short burst of physical activity can noticeably sharpen recall and understanding.
