# Chapter 16: Avoiding Overconfidence — The Power of Teamwork

## Fred's Story

Fred couldn't move his left hand. Not surprising, given that a month earlier — while singing in the shower — he'd suffered a near-fatal stroke in the right hemisphere of his brain, the side that controls the *left* side of the body.

**Fred's real problem was worse: despite genuinely being unable to move his left hand, he insisted — and truly believed — that he could.** He'd blame the stillness on being "too tired to lift a finger," or insist his hand *had* moved and people simply hadn't been watching. He'd even covertly move it with his right hand, then loudly announce it had moved on its own.

His left hand did gradually recover over the following months, and he eventually laughed with his doctor about how thoroughly he'd fooled himself. But real, lasting changes remained. Fred, once caring and considerate, became dogmatic and self-righteous. Once a sharp practical joker himself, he now just nodded blankly at other people's punch lines, missing them entirely. His once-cautious investing instincts gave way to naive overconfidence. Worse, he became genuinely emotionally tone-deaf — trying to sell his wife's car without asking her first, then genuinely surprised by her anger; sitting placidly eating popcorn while his family cried over their dying dog, as if watching it happen in a movie.

**What made this genuinely puzzling: Fred's raw intelligence stayed intact** — he could still rapidly draw up a profit-and-loss statement, or solve complex algebra. But if a calculation produced something genuinely nonsensical — a hot dog stand somehow losing nearly a billion dollars — it simply didn't bother him. There was no internal "click" of *wait, that can't be right.*

**Fred turned out to be a classic case of "broad-perspective perceptual disorder of the right hemisphere."** His stroke had disabled large parts of his brain's right hemisphere — leaving him able to function, but only partially. (Simplistic "left brain vs. right brain" claims deserve real skepticism — but that doesn't mean genuinely useful research into hemispheric differences should be thrown out entirely.) Fred is a genuine, cautionary reminder of what happens when we fail to use our full range of cognitive ability — not nearly as devastating for most of us as it was for him, but even subtle underuse of certain mental capacities can genuinely hurt our work.

## Avoiding Overconfidence

**Real evidence suggests the right hemisphere is what helps us step back and see the big picture.** People with right-hemisphere damage often can't experience genuine "aha!" insight — exactly why Fred couldn't catch a joke's punch line. The right hemisphere appears to play a genuinely vital role in course-correcting and running honest "reality checks" on our own thinking.

**In a real sense, breezing through a homework or test problem without going back to check your work means refusing to use part of your own brain.** You're skipping the pause where you'd step back with the bigger picture in mind, and honestly ask whether the answer actually makes sense. As leading neuroscientist V.S. Ramachandran put it, the right hemisphere acts almost like a built-in "Devil's Advocate," questioning the status quo and hunting for global inconsistencies — while "the left hemisphere always tries to cling tenaciously to the way things were." This echoes psychologist Michael Gazzaniga's earlier research, which found the left hemisphere essentially *interprets* the world for us, and will go to genuinely great lengths to keep that interpretation unchanged, even in the face of contradicting evidence.

**Working in focused mode, small errors in assumptions or calculation are genuinely easy to make.** Go off track early, and it genuinely doesn't matter how flawless the rest of the work is — the final answer is still wrong, sometimes laughably so (calculating Earth's circumference at two and a half feet, say). But these nonsensical results often just don't register as *wrong* — because the focused, left-leaning mode carries a real bias toward clinging to whatever's already been done. Focused analysis is genuinely valuable and upbeat — but real research consistently points to a real risk of rigidity, dogmatism, and self-centeredness alongside it.

**When you feel absolutely, completely certain your homework or test answer is correct, be aware that confidence might genuinely be an artifact of left-hemisphere overconfidence, not actual correctness.** Deliberately stepping back to recheck your work invites more genuine interaction *between* hemispheres — drawing on the distinct strengths of each.

**Students uncomfortable with math often fall into what might be called "equation sheet bingo"** — desperately hunting for a pattern that matches what the teacher or textbook did, and forcing their own numbers to fit it. **Genuinely good learners instead vet their own work for whether it actually makes sense** — asking themselves what the equations genuinely mean, and where they actually come from.

> "The first principle is that you must not fool yourself — and you are the easiest person to fool."
> — Physicist Richard Feynman, on avoiding pseudoscience masquerading as real science

## The Value of Brainstorming with Others

**Niels Bohr, deeply involved in the Manhattan Project, was also one of the greatest physicists who ever lived — which, ironically, made it genuinely harder for him to think clearly about physics.** He was so revered as the genius who'd intuited quantum theory that his own ideas had become essentially unassailable — nobody would push back on even his weakest suggestions, since everyone else simply "ooh"-ed and "ahh"-ed reverently.

**Richard Feynman, still a relative youngster among hundreds of prominent physicists at Los Alamos, turned out to be Bohr's real secret weapon** — genuinely unintimidated by anyone, willing to tell Bohr flatly when an idea seemed foolish. Bohr deliberately sought Feynman out for private brainstorming *before* meeting with the wider physics team, precisely because Feynman was the one person genuinely willing to push back.

**As Bohr understood, genuinely brainstorming with others — as long as they know the material — can be enormously helpful.** Your own neural horsepower alone, both modes and both hemispheres, still has real blind spots, and your own naively upbeat focused mode can easily skip right past an error, especially one you made yourself. Worse, you can sometimes feel completely confident you've mastered something you genuinely haven't — exactly the shock of discovering you flunked a test you were sure you'd aced.

**Studying with friends genuinely helps you catch when your own thinking has gone astray.** Friends and study partners essentially act as an external, ever-questioning "diffuse mode" outside your own head, catching things you personally missed or genuinely couldn't see. And as covered earlier, explaining something to a friend also genuinely deepens your own understanding of it in the process.

**Working with others matters beyond problem-solving — it genuinely shapes careers too.** A single tip from a teammate — take Professor Passionate's class, check out this job opening — can meaningfully redirect the course of your life. One of sociology's most-cited papers, Mark Granovetter's "The Strength of Weak Ties," found that your *acquaintances*, not your close friends, predict your access to genuinely new ideas and job opportunities. Close friends tend to run in the same social circles you already do — but acquaintances (classmates, teammates) circulate in genuinely different ones, dramatically expanding your effective access to an "outside your own brain" diffuse network.

**Study partners should have at least some genuine willingness to critique.** Research on team creativity shows that purely agreeable, nonjudgmental collaboration is actually *less* productive than sessions where honest criticism is genuinely welcomed and even actively invited. If you or a study partner spots something genuinely wrong in the group's understanding, say so plainly, and hash it out without worrying too much about hurt feelings. That's not license for gratuitous cruelty — but excessive concern with maintaining a "safe" environment for criticism can genuinely kill constructive, creative thinking, since attention shifts toward managing feelings rather than the material itself. Like Feynman, remember that criticism — given or received — genuinely isn't about *you*; it's about the material you're actually trying to understand. Related: competition, often unfairly maligned, is genuinely a form of intense collaboration that can bring out real best effort.

**Study partners help in one more genuinely underrated way: you generally don't mind looking a little foolish in front of friends — but you don't want to look *too* foolish, too often.** Studying together functions a bit like practicing in front of a small audience — research shows this kind of public practice genuinely sharpens your ability to think on your feet under real pressure, like a test or presentation. There's a final, practical value too: no instructor or textbook is ever completely error-free — friends can genuinely help validate and untangle confusion when a "credible" source turns out to be flat-out wrong, saving you real hours chasing a false lead.

**One genuine caution: study groups are powerfully effective for math, science, engineering, and technology — but only if they stay actual study groups.** If sessions consistently devolve into socializing, all bets are off. Keep small talk minimal, keep the group genuinely on task, and finish the actual work. If your group consistently starts 5–15 minutes late, members show up without having read the material, and conversation constantly drifts off-topic, it's genuinely time to find a different group.

> **Paul Blowers, University Distinguished Professor, University of Arizona:**
> "I'm an introvert, and I genuinely don't like working with people. But struggling in my college engineering classes back in the 1980s, I realized I needed a second pair of eyes — even though I still didn't want to actually work with anyone. Without online chat back then, my classmate Jeff and I left notes on each other's dorm doors. I'd write '1) 1.7 m/s' — my answer to homework problem one. I'd come back from a shower and find Jeff had written, 'No, 1) 11 m/s.' I'd dig back through my own work, find a mistake, arrive at 8.45 m/s — go to Jeff's room, argue our respective solutions out loud while he sat there with his guitar slung over his shoulder — then we'd both go back to our own separate work, and I'd suddenly land on 9.37 m/s, and so would he. We'd both get full marks on the assignment. As you can see, there are genuinely effective ways to work with others requiring only minimal actual interaction, even if you genuinely don't like group work."

## Summing It Up

- **Focused mode can produce a critical error while leaving you feeling completely confident everything was done correctly.**
- **Rechecking your work** brings a genuinely broader perspective, engaging slightly different neural processes able to catch blunders your original focused pass missed.
- **Working with others willing to genuinely disagree** helps catch errors in your own thinking, sharpens your ability to think on your feet under pressure, deepens your own understanding as you explain material to others, and can meaningfully build useful career connections.
- **Criticism — giving or receiving it — isn't genuinely about you.** It's about the material you're actually trying to understand.
- **You are, genuinely, the easiest person in the world to fool.**

## Expert Tips: Physics Professor Brad Roth

> **Brad Roth**, Fellow of the American Physical Society, co-author of *Intermediate Physics for Medicine and Biology*:
>
> "One thing I genuinely stress in my classes: think *before* you calculate. I really dislike the 'plug and chug' approach many students default to. I constantly remind students that equations are NOT simply expressions you feed numbers into to get other numbers out — equations tell a genuine *story* about how the physical world actually behaves. For me, understanding a physics equation means seeing the underlying story; a genuine qualitative grasp of an equation matters more than getting the correct number out of it.
>
> **A few more concrete tips:**
> 1. **Checking your work often takes far less time than solving the problem did.** It's a real shame to spend 20 minutes solving something correctly in principle, and lose it all because you didn't spend two minutes checking.
> 2. **Units of measurement are your friend.** If units don't match on both sides of an equation, the equation is genuinely wrong — you can't add something measured in seconds to something measured in meters, any more than you can meaningfully add apples to rocks. Tracing back through your work to find exactly where the units stop matching will usually lead you straight to the actual mistake. (I've seen this same unit error in research papers submitted to professional journals — it happens to genuine experts too.)
> 3. **Think about what the equation genuinely means, so your math result and your intuition actually agree.** If they don't match, either your math has an error, or your intuition does — either way, figuring out *why* they disagree is a real win.
> 4. **(Slightly more advanced) For a complicated expression, test limiting cases** — let one variable go to zero, or to infinity — and see whether that clarifies what the equation is genuinely saying."

## Key Takeaways, Tips, and Tricks

- **Always recheck your work, even when you feel fully confident.** Confidence itself can be a symptom of one-sided, unchecked focused-mode thinking, not proof of correctness.
- **Watch for "equation sheet bingo"** — forcing your work to match a memorized pattern instead of genuinely asking what the equation actually means and why it applies.
- **Deliberately seek out people willing to disagree with you**, not just agreeable collaborators. Genuine, honest criticism produces measurably better results than uniformly polite feedback.
- **Remember that criticism — giving or receiving — is about the material, not about you personally.** Depersonalizing it makes both giving and receiving genuinely easier.
- **Lean on acquaintances, not just close friends, for new ideas and opportunities.** Weak ties (classmates, teammates) circulate in genuinely different circles than close friends do, and often deliver the more useful tip or lead.
- **Study with others, even if you're an introvert.** Minimal, low-effort collaboration (Paul Blowers's dorm-door notes) still catches real errors your own focused mode would miss.
- **Keep study groups genuinely on-task.** If a group consistently arrives late, unprepared, or off-topic, it's a real signal to find a different one.
- **Check units on both sides of an equation** — a mismatch is a fast, reliable way to locate a genuine calculation error.
- **Test whether your math result matches your intuition.** A mismatch between the two is worth investigating either way — you'll either catch a real math error, or correct a flawed intuition.
- **For complex equations, test extreme (limiting) cases** — letting a variable approach zero or infinity — to build a clearer sense of what the equation is actually describing.
- **Checking your work is almost always cheaper than the mistake it prevents.** A couple of minutes spent verifying an answer is a small price against losing full credit on work that was otherwise done correctly.
