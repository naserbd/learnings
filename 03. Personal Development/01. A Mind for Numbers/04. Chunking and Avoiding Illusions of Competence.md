# Chapter 4: Chunking and Avoiding Illusions of Competence — The Keys to Becoming an "Equation Whisperer"

## Solomon's Story

Solomon Shereshevsky, a journalist in 1920s Soviet Russia, first caught his editor's attention for being "lazy" — he never took notes. When asked why, Solomon was genuinely confused: why would he need to, when he could remember anything he heard, word for word? He assumed everyone had a memory like his — perfect, indelible.

Sounds like a dream, right? It wasn't. Alongside his extraordinary memory, Solomon had a real, specific problem — one that gets right at the heart of how focus connects to both understanding *and* memory.

## What Actually Happens When You Focus

The last chapter covered Einstellung — getting stuck on one way of seeing a problem, unable to step back and see a better one. Focused attention helps you solve problems, but it can just as easily block you from spotting a *new* solution.

When you focus on something, your brain reaches out — like an octopus stretching its tentacles — connecting different regions together. Focus on a shape, and one "tentacle" links your visual processing to your sense of geometry; focus on color, and a different connection produces the sense of "green." String enough of these connections together, and you land on "Granny Smith apple." This kind of deliberate, focused connecting is central to focused-mode learning — and interestingly, when you're stressed, angry, or afraid, the octopus loses some of its ability to make these connections. That's part of why your brain genuinely doesn't work as well under stress.

Learning a language as a child happens almost automatically this way — your mother says "mama," you echo it back, and a durable neural loop forms linking that sound to her face. The best structured language programs (the U.S. Defense Language Institute, for instance) pair heavy focused-mode repetition with more free-flowing, diffuse-style conversation practice — the goal being fluency as natural as your first language.

This same focused repetition — building what are called **memory traces** — underlies a perfect golf swing, a chef's practiced omelet flip, or a free throw. A toddler's clumsy spin becomes a professional dancer's graceful choreography the exact same way: piece by piece, memorized moves gradually folding into larger, more creative expression.

## What Is a "Chunk"? Solomon's Real Problem

Solomon's extraordinary memory came with a real cost: his individual memories were so vivid, so richly detailed, that they actively got in the way of combining into bigger conceptual units. He couldn't see the forest — because every individual tree was rendered in such overwhelming, specific detail.

A **chunk** is a piece of information bound together by *meaning*. The letters p-o-p combine into one simple, easy-to-remember chunk: the word "pop" — like zipping a bulky file into a compact one. Underneath that simple chunk sits a whole web of neurons that have learned to fire together in sync. This same chunking process — abstracting complex detail into a simple, usable unit — underlies much of science, literature, and art.

**Example:** in the early 1900s, Alfred Wegener studied maps, rock formations, and fossil patterns, and realized the continents fit together like puzzle pieces — evidence they'd once been a single landmass that slowly drifted apart. That's continental drift — a genuinely elegant discovery.

If Solomon had read that same story, he could have repeated every word back perfectly — but the actual *concept* of continental drift would likely have escaped him entirely, because he couldn't link his individually vivid memories into a unified conceptual chunk.

**One of the very first steps toward real expertise in math and science is learning to build these conceptual chunks** — mental leaps that tie separate bits of information together through genuine meaning. Chunking makes your brain run more efficiently: once something's properly chunked, you don't need to consciously track every underlying detail — the chunk itself is enough, the same way "I'll get dressed" is one simple thought standing in for a genuinely complex sequence of actions.

## Three Steps to Building a Chunk

**Step 1: Focus your full attention on the material.** If the TV's on in the background, or you keep checking your phone, chunking simply won't happen well — your brain is trying to build new connections while also getting pulled in other directions. Real chunking needs your attentional "octopus" fully engaged, not split.

**Step 2: Understand the basic idea you're trying to chunk** — whether that's continental drift, force being proportional to mass, supply and demand, or a specific type of math problem. This synthesis step is exactly what Solomon struggled with — but most people manage it naturally, especially when they let focused and diffuse thinking take turns doing their part.

Understanding acts like a kind of superglue, binding memory traces together into something broader and more connected. **You can technically build a "chunk" without real understanding, but it's a useless one** — it won't connect to anything else you're learning. And critically: understanding *how* a problem was solved isn't the same as actually having built a durable chunk you can recall later. Don't confuse the "aha!" moment of a breakthrough with genuine mastery — that's exactly why a concept can feel crystal clear in class, and completely foreign again a week later if you don't revisit it. Testing yourself — closing the book and trying to solve the problem again — speeds this whole process up.

**Step 3: Gain context** — not just *how* to use the chunk, but *when.* This means practicing with a genuine mix of related and unrelated problems, so you learn not only when a technique applies, but when it *doesn't*. A tool in your problem-solving toolbox is only useful if you actually know when to reach for it. Practice broadens the web of neurons connected to a chunk, making it both sturdier and reachable from more directions.

Concept-chunks and procedure-chunks reinforce each other — solving lots of problems teaches you *why* a procedure works, understanding *why* it works makes your own mistakes easier to catch, and both together make it far easier to apply your knowledge to a genuinely new problem — something researchers call **transfer**.

**Learning happens two ways at once:** a **bottom-up** process, where repetition and practice build and strengthen individual chunks, and a **top-down** process, where you keep sight of the bigger picture and see where each piece fits. Context is where these two meet — chunking is *learning a technique*; context is *learning when to actually use it*. Both matter equally for real mastery.

> **Debra Gassner Dragone, Accounting Instructor, University of Delaware:**
> "I tell students that internalizing accounting fundamentals is like internalizing how to type — I'm not consciously thinking about typing right now, just about my thoughts; the typing happens on its own. My end-of-class mantra: review the Debit and Credit Rules and the Accounting Equation right before falling asleep — let that be the last thing you repeat to yourself at night (after meditation or prayer, of course)."

## Illusions of Competence — and Why Recall Beats Rereading

**Actively trying to recall material — retrieval practice — is dramatically more effective than simply rereading it.** Psychologist Jeffrey Karpicke's research found most students genuinely believe rereading notes or the textbook works well, and rarely test themselves — despite retrieval practice being the far more effective strategy. Having the book (or a search engine) open right in front of you creates a genuine illusion that the material is already in your head. It isn't. Because rereading *feels* easier than actively recalling, students keep choosing the less effective method.

This is exactly why merely *wanting* to learn something, and spending real time on it, doesn't guarantee it actually sticks. As memory researcher Alan Baddeley put it: "Intention to learn is helpful only if it leads to the use of good learning strategies."

**Even highlighting and underlining need care** — done carelessly, they can actively mislead you, since the physical motion of marking up a page can trick you into feeling like you've absorbed the concept, when you haven't. Train yourself to identify the actual main idea *before* marking anything, and keep markings minimal — a sentence or less per paragraph. A short synthesizing note in the margin is genuinely more useful than a highlighted sentence.

**Using recall instead of passive rereading makes your study time genuinely more focused and effective.** (Rereading only becomes useful again if enough time has passed that it functions more like spaced repetition than pure repetition.)

**Always work through math and science homework yourself.** If a textbook includes worked solutions in the back, use them only to *check* your answer afterward — not as a shortcut. This is exactly why instructors insist on seeing your full work and reasoning: it forces you to genuinely think the problem through, functions as a built-in self-test, and gives graders something concrete to give you feedback on.

**Don't let too much time pass before revisiting new material** — otherwise you're essentially starting the reinforcement from scratch each time. Try to touch new, challenging material again within a day. This is exactly why many professors recommend rewriting your notes the same evening after a lecture — it solidifies the forming chunk, and reveals exactly the gaps in your understanding that tests love to target. (Knowing where the gaps actually are is the first step to closing them.) Once a chunk is genuinely solid, you can stretch the gap between "maintenance" reviews out to weeks or months, and eventually the knowledge becomes nearly permanent. (True to this: returning to Russia years later, the author found long-forgotten Russian words popping out of her mouth in an argument with a taxi driver — words she had no conscious memory of still knowing.)

> **Thomas Day, Professor of Audio Engineering, McNally Smith College of Music:**
> "The difference between a student who's just learned a concept in class and an actual scientist or engineer who can apply it to a real physical problem is exactly this: working with the concept until it becomes second nature — until you can use it like a tool, not a memorized fact."

**Well-designed spaced-repetition flashcard apps** (like Anki) build the right review intervals directly into the system, automatically optimizing when you revisit each item.

### Why Chunking Frees Up Mental Space

Recall from earlier: working memory holds roughly four items. When you're first learning something, the raw, unchunked pieces take up *all four slots* — a tangled mess of connections. Once it's genuinely chunked, it collapses down into a single slot, freeing up the rest of your working memory for other things — while the chunk itself functions like a compressed link back to a much richer body of long-term knowledge, ready to expand the moment you actually need it.

This is exactly why *you* need to be the one solving the problem — not whoever wrote the solution manual. Looking at a worked solution and thinking "oh yeah, I see why they did that" doesn't actually knit the concept into your own neural circuitry. **Glancing at a solution and assuming you now genuinely know it yourself is one of the single most common illusions of competence in learning.**

### Try It: Spotting Your Own Illusion of Competence

Take the anagram "Me, radium ace" — rearranged, it spells the last name of a famous physicist (**Marie Curie**). Working it out yourself takes real effort — but if you'd simply been *shown* the answer, that instant "aha!" feeling would have made you overestimate your own anagram-solving skill.

The same illusion happens constantly with rereading — the material already sitting on the page in front of you creates a false sense that it's also sitting in your head. Try this instead: pick a concept from your notes, read it once, look away, and genuinely try to recall and understand it from memory. Then glance back, reread it, and try again. Most people are surprised how much this simple recall exercise sharpens real understanding, compared to just rereading passively.

Retaining information genuinely matters — not just for tests, but for creative thinking. Combining chunks in new ways underlies a huge share of historical innovation. In *Where Good Ideas Come From*, Steven Johnson describes the "slow hunch" — a years-long simmering of focused and diffuse thinking that produced breakthroughs from Darwin's theory of evolution to the invention of the World Wide Web. What makes the slow hunch work is simply having real, ready mental access to the relevant pieces, so they can randomly, tentatively recombine until something genuinely new emerges. (Notably, Bill Gates and other industry leaders reportedly block out extended reading weeks specifically to hold many varied ideas in mind at once — fueling exactly this kind of cross-pollination. A recurring difference between creative and merely technically competent people is genuinely the *breadth* of their interests.)

**The bigger your library of chunks, the more easily you solve new problems** — and the more chunking experience you build, the *bigger* the chunks you're able to form. It can feel like an overwhelming number of concepts to master in a single chapter — but the "Law of Serendipity" applies: luck favors the one who actually tries. Once the first concept goes into your library, the second gets noticeably easier — and the third easier still. It doesn't become effortless, but it genuinely does get easier.

**There are two fundamentally different ways to solve a problem:** sequential, step-by-step reasoning (focused mode), and holistic intuition (diffuse mode, connecting several separate focused-mode ideas at once). Most genuinely difficult problems get solved intuitively, because they require a leap away from the familiar. But because diffuse mode makes connections somewhat randomly, always double-check an intuitive insight using focused mode afterward — intuition isn't always right.

Building a real library of chunks trains your brain to recognize not just *one* specific problem, but entire categories of problem — so you start spotting patterns that simplify things automatically, with the right technique surfacing from memory almost on its own. Coming up on midterms or finals, this makes review dramatically faster — you're brushing up on solutions already sitting at the ready, rather than relearning from scratch.

### Try It: When You Genuinely Can't Grasp Something

If a method taught in class isn't clicking, work backward — search for who first discovered or developed it, and how they actually arrived at the idea. A simpler, more intuitive explanation of *why* something works (and why it's taught) is often available once you trace it back to its origin.

## Practice Makes Permanent

Understanding alone usually isn't enough to build a lasting chunk. Picture a "chunk" as a memory trace that gets progressively darker and stronger the more you work with it: faint after your first pass, darker after a bit more practice and exposure to different contexts, and eventually solid and dark once it's genuinely embedded in long-term memory — much like practicing a piece on piano.

**Strengthening a new pattern within a day of first learning it genuinely matters** — skip that reinforcement, and the pattern can fade fast. (Spaced repetition gets covered in more depth later.) Also worth watching for: repeating the *wrong* process over and over just reinforces the wrong process. This is exactly why checking your work matters — even landing on the right answer through a flawed method can quietly mislead you into thinking that method is sound.

> **William Thurston, Fields Medal winner (mathematics' highest honor):**
> "Mathematics is amazingly compressible — you might struggle a long time, step by step, working through an idea from several angles. But once you truly understand it and can see it as a whole, there's a tremendous mental compression. You can file it away, recall it quickly and completely when needed, and use it as just one step within some larger process. That compression — and the insight behind it — is one of math's real joys."

Repetition and practice can genuinely be boring — and in the hands of a poor teacher, can turn into pure drudgery. But it's still essential — nobody masters chess, language, music, or dance without real repetition. Good teachers explain *why* the repetition is worth the effort.

**Both bottom-up chunking and top-down big-picture thinking are necessary** — creativity and "seeing the big picture" alone won't get you there in math or science without a genuine foundation of practice and repetition underneath.

**Solid research backs this directly.** In one published study, students who studied a text and then practiced *recalling* it — rather than rereading it, or building concept maps — learned significantly more, and more deeply, in the same amount of time. Interestingly, the students themselves had predicted concept mapping would work best — but building connections between chunks *before* the underlying chunks are actually solid doesn't work well. It's like trying to learn advanced chess strategy before you even know how the pieces move.

**Practicing math and science problems across a genuine variety of situations builds richer, more solidly contextual chunks.** Learning any new skill takes plenty of varied practice across different contexts — that's what actually turns a new skill into a comfortable, automatic part of how you think.

> **Robert R. Gamache, Associate Vice President, University of Massachusetts Lowell:**
> "As an undergrad, I got fascinated with physical chemistry derivations and made a habit of doing every problem in the book — I essentially hard-wired my brain to solve them, and by semester's end I could look at a problem and almost instantly know how to approach it. I recommend daily study — not necessarily long sessions, just enough to keep the material at the tip of your tongue. It's like being bilingual: when I go to France, my French takes a few days to kick back in; when I return home, I briefly struggle for English words. Practice daily, and the information is just *there* — you don't have to go searching for it."

### Recall Away From Your Usual Study Spot

Physical activity genuinely helps when you're stuck on a tough idea — many real scientific breakthroughs reportedly happened mid-walk. Recalling material somewhere *other* than your usual study spot also helps, by forcing your brain to grasp the concept independent of location-specific cues — otherwise, taking a test in an unfamiliar room can weaken recall simply because the physical setting differs from where you originally learned it.

Math and science concepts can actually be *easier* to internalize than something like a foreign vocabulary list, because the problem itself gives you feedback about what to do next — a bit like how a dancer can feel their body cueing the next move.

> **Jason Dechant, Ph.D., School of Nursing, University of Pittsburgh:**
> "The first thing I do with a struggling student is look at how they're organizing their notes. We often spend our whole first session on organizing and chunking information, rather than me explaining concepts directly. When they come back the next week with material actually organized, they're consistently amazed at how much more they retain."

## Interleaving: Mixing Problem Types, Instead of Overlearning

One last major tip: **interleaving** — practicing a genuine mix of different problem types requiring different strategies, rather than grinding repeatedly on one technique.

When you first learn a new approach, it's natural to practice it repeatedly in the same session. Continuing to drill a technique well past the point of understanding is called **overlearning**. It has its place — building the automatic reflex needed for a tennis serve, or a flawless piano performance — but research shows repetitive overlearning *within a single math or science study session* tends to waste valuable time. (Revisiting the same technique later, mixed in with others, is genuinely fine.)

Once you've grasped a technique in one sitting, continuing to hammer at it doesn't necessarily strengthen the long-term memory connections you actually want. Worse, focusing on just one technique is a bit like learning carpentry only by practicing with a hammer — eventually you start assuming everything can be fixed by bashing it.

**Real mastery means learning to select and apply the *right* technique for a given problem** — and the only way to genuinely learn that is by practicing problems that call for genuinely different techniques. Once you've got the basic idea of a new technique down (like training wheels on a bike), start deliberately interleaving it with other problem types. This can be awkward, since a textbook section is usually devoted to one specific technique — you already know which one to use just by which section you're in. Look ahead to the more varied, mixed problem sets often found at the end of a chapter, or deliberately ask yourself *why* one problem calls for a particular technique instead of another. The goal is training your brain that knowing *how* to use a technique isn't enough — you also need to genuinely know *when*.

**A practical tool: index cards.** Put the question on one side, the solution steps on the other, then shuffle them — forcing yourself to face a genuinely random mix of techniques. At first, sit down and try writing out the full solution on blank paper before checking the back. Later, once you're more confident, you can review anywhere — even on a walk. Use the question itself as a cue to recall the steps, and flip the card to verify. Another option: open the textbook to a random page, cover everything but the problem itself, and work it cold.

> **Doug Rohrer, Psychologist, University of South Florida, on overlearning vs. interleaving:**
> "Overlearning, in the research sense, means continuing to study or practice immediately after you've already met some benchmark — solving a type of problem correctly, then immediately doing several more of the same kind. Working a few extra problems of the same kind often helps a bit — but doing too many in immediate succession runs into diminishing returns fast.
>
> Students should aim to maximize what they learn per unit of study time — get the most 'bang for the buck.' The research is unambiguous here: rather than one long session drilling a single skill (which produces overlearning), split your effort across several shorter sessions. Long sessions aren't inherently bad — they're fine, as long as you don't spend too much of that time on any single skill. Once you understand 'X,' move to something else, and come back to 'X' another day."

**Write your initial solutions and diagrams by hand.** There's real evidence handwriting embeds ideas more effectively than typing — and it's often genuinely faster to draw a symbol like Σ or Ω by hand than to hunt down the right keyboard shortcut. (You can still photograph or scan your handwritten work afterward to load into a digital flashcard app.)

**Watch for this illusion of competence:** continuing to practice a technique you already know simply because it's easy and feels satisfying. Interleaving — deliberately jumping around between chapters and problem types when reviewing for a test — can feel *harder* in the moment. It genuinely produces deeper learning anyway.

> **Mike Rosenthal, Senior Instructor of Mathematics, Florida International University:**
> "Students often get ten identical problems in a row for homework. By the second or third, they're not really thinking anymore — they're just mimicking what they did on the last one. I tell them: after a couple of section 9.4 problems, go back and do one from 9.3. Do a couple more 9.4 problems, then one from 9.1. This trains the same mental gear-shifting they'll need on the actual test.
>
> Too many students also do homework just to finish it — solve a problem, check the answer, smile, move on. I suggest inserting one extra step between the smile and moving on: ask yourself, *'how would I even recognize this needed this method, if I saw it mixed in with other problems on a test and didn't already know which section it came from?'* Treat every homework problem as test prep — not just a task to check off."

## Summing It Up

- Practice builds strong neural patterns — conceptual chunks of genuine understanding.
- Practice is also what gives you the mental flexibility and agility a real test demands.
- Chunks are built through three things: **focused attention**, real **understanding** of the basic idea, and enough **practice** to gain broader context.
- **Simple recall** — trying to remember key points without looking at the page — is one of the single best tools for building chunks. Recall builds the mental "hooks" your thinking can actually hang on.

Next time you're with a friend, family member, or classmate, try explaining — in your own words — the core of what you've just been learning, whether from this book or a class. Explaining it to someone else fuels your own enthusiasm, and also sharpens and cements the ideas in your own head. Even genuinely advanced material becomes clearer to *you* when you have to simplify it for someone without your background.

## A Student's Story: Learning Under Real Constraints

> **Paul Kruchko**, an Army veteran who survived multiple roadside bomb attacks in Iraq, came home with an undiagnosed traumatic brain injury — struggling with concentration, cognition, and irritability so severe he sometimes couldn't finish a sentence, and initially struggled even with fractions. Enrolling in a computer and electronics engineering technology program turned out to be, in his words, "a blessing in disguise" — the mental effort of learning seemed to actively help his brain heal, much like physical effort builds muscle in a gym.
>
> Going on to pursue a full engineering degree meant confronting calculus with weak math fundamentals, while married, a new father, and working full-time — meaning the real challenge shifted from cognition to genuinely limited time. After a rough D in differential equations, he developed a deliberate, structured approach:
>
> 1. **Read (don't yet solve) upcoming homework and practice exams** — priming his mental pump for the new material ahead of time.
> 2. **Review lecture notes, and attend every lecture possible** — one hour of lecture, in his experience, is worth two hours of textbook reading. He reviews notes the next day while the material is still fresh, and finds even thirty minutes asking a professor questions worth roughly three hours of solo reading.
> 3. **Rework worked example problems from lecture** — never practicing on problems without a solution available for feedback. He uses different colored pens (not just black) to keep his notes visually distinct and easier to actually read back.
> 4. **Work assigned homework and practice questions** — building genuine "muscle memory" chunks for actually solving that type of problem.

## Key Takeaways, Tips, and Tricks

- **Build chunks in three steps:** focus fully (no distractions), genuinely understand the basic idea, then practice broadly enough to learn the *context* — when to use it, and when not to.
- **A concept "clicking" once isn't the same as having a durable chunk.** Test yourself soon after, or it can fade and feel unfamiliar again by test time.
- **Recall beats rereading.** Close the book and try to remember the material from memory — this is measurably more effective than passive rereading, even though rereading *feels* more productive.
- **Watch for illusions of competence** — highlighting, rereading, or glancing at a worked solution can all create a false sense that you've learned something you haven't.
- **Always solve homework problems yourself first**, using worked solutions only to check your answer afterward — showing your work is itself a self-test.
- **Revisit new, difficult material within a day** of first learning it — rewriting lecture notes that same evening is a great way to do this, and it reveals gaps in your understanding early.
- **Once a chunk is solid, spread out your review** to weeks or months apart — over-drilling something you've already mastered wastes time.
- **Use spaced-repetition tools** (like Anki) to automate optimal review timing.
- **Practice recalling material somewhere other than your usual study spot** — it makes your understanding less dependent on location-specific cues, which helps on test day.
- **If a method won't click, look up who first invented it** and why — the original context often explains things more simply than the textbook does.
- **Interleave your practice** — mix different problem types and techniques in the same study session, rather than drilling one technique repeatedly (overlearning). Use index cards or randomly chosen problems to force this mix.
- **Write solutions by hand** when possible — it tends to embed ideas more effectively than typing.
- **Ask yourself "how would I recognize this technique out of context?"** after every homework problem — treat homework as test prep, not just a checklist item.
- **Explain what you're learning to someone else** — simplifying it for an outside audience sharpens your own understanding and memory.
- **If you're short on time**, prioritize: skim ahead before lectures, attend and review notes promptly, rework worked examples before attempting fresh ones, and treat every study session as building toward a specific, reusable chunk — not just "getting through" material.
