# Chapter 12: Learning to Appreciate Your Talent

## Working Toward Intuitive Understanding

There's a lot math and science can genuinely learn from sports. A baseball player doesn't master hitting in a single day — the body perfects a swing through years of repetition, building genuine muscle memory, so the whole complex motion collapses down into a single thought, a single chunk, instead of requiring conscious recall of every individual step.

**The same holds for math and science.** Once you genuinely understand *why* you do something, you don't need to keep re-deriving the *how* every single time. You don't carry 100 beans around in your pocket, laying out ten rows of ten every time you need 10 × 10 = 100 — at some point, it's simply known. The rule "add exponents when multiplying same-base numbers" (10⁴ × 10⁵ = 10⁹) becomes second nature the same way. Work through this procedure across enough varied problems, and you'll genuinely understand both the *why* and the *how* far more deeply than any conventional explanation from a teacher or book could give you — because your own mind constructed the pattern of meaning, rather than simply absorbing someone else's explanation. **People genuinely learn by making sense of information themselves — they rarely absorb something complex just by being told it.** (As math teachers like to say: "Math is not a spectator sport.")

**Chess masters, ER physicians, fighter pilots, and other experts facing rapid, complex decisions genuinely rely on trained intuition** — drawing on a deep, ingrained library of chunks, rather than consciously reasoning through every step. At a certain point, self-consciously trying to "understand why you're doing what you're doing" just slows you down, interrupts flow, and produces *worse* decisions.

**Teachers and instructors can accidentally get too caught up in strict rule-following.** In a revealing study, six people were filmed performing CPR — only one of them a genuine, trained paramedic. Real paramedics, asked to identify the actual professional in the lineup, got it right 90% of the time, often simply saying "they seemed to know what they were doing." **CPR instructors, by contrast, got it right only 30% of the time** — nitpicking the real expert for things like not pausing to precisely measure hand placement. For these overly rule-focused instructors, precise procedural correctness had come to matter more than genuine, practical competence.

## No Need for Genius Envy

Just as Olympic athletes don't build real athleticism from a weekend jog now and then, chess grandmasters don't build their mental structures through last-minute cramming. Their knowledge is built gradually, through extensive practice that develops genuine big-picture context — and this kind of practice places durable memory traces deep in long-term storage, ready for fast, easy access when needed.

**Consider Magnus Carlsen again** — the fast-thinking prodigy from earlier chapters. He has an extraordinary grasp of thousands of previously-played chess games; shown an endgame board position, he can instantly identify which of over ten thousand historical games it came from. In other words, he's built a genuinely vast chunked library of solution patterns, letting him quickly riffle through and see how others handled a similar situation before.

**Carlsen isn't unique in *what* he's doing — just exceptionally good at it.** It's genuinely typical for grandmasters to spend at least a decade practicing and studying to internalize thousands of these memory-chunk patterns. Having them readily available lets an expert recognize the key elements of any position far faster than an amateur — a trained eye that rapidly intuits the best move for the situation.

**But wait — aren't chess masters and mental-math prodigies simply exceptionally gifted?** Not necessarily. Straight talk: intelligence genuinely matters, and being smarter often correlates with a larger working memory — holding nine things instead of four, and gripping onto them tightly, genuinely makes math and science easier to learn. **But here's the catch: that same tight grip also makes it harder to be creative.**

**Why?** Our familiar friend (and enemy) Einstellung. The idea already occupying your mind blocks fresh ones from getting in — a superb working memory can hold its current thought so tightly that new ideas simply can't push through. Tightly controlled attention could genuinely use an occasional whiff of ADHD-like "fresh air" — the ability for attention to shift even when you don't consciously want it to. The very ability to solve complex problems can lead you to overthink simple ones, chasing an elaborate answer while overlooking an obvious, simple one sitting right there. Research genuinely shows that smart people can be *more* prone to getting lost in unnecessary complexity — while people with less apparent raw intellectual horsepower more easily cut straight to the simple solution.

> **Bill Zettler, Ph.D., Professor of Biology, longtime academic advisor and Teacher of the Year, University of Florida:**
> "In my experience, there's an almost *inverse* correlation between high GRE scores and ultimate career success. Many students with the lowest scores went on to genuinely successful careers, while a surprising number of the 'geniuses' fell by the wayside, for one reason or another."

**If you're someone who can't hold much in mind at once — losing focus and drifting off during lectures, needing real silence to use your working memory to its fullest — welcome to the club of the genuinely creative.** A somewhat smaller working memory means new learning generalizes more freely into creative new combinations. Because your working memory (rooted in the prefrontal cortex's focusing power) doesn't lock everything down quite as tightly, other parts of the brain — including the sensory cortex, which is more attuned to the surrounding environment and is also the source of both dreams and genuinely creative ideas — get more room to actually contribute. You may need to work harder, sometimes much harder, to grasp something at first — but once it's genuinely chunked, you can turn it inside out and put it through genuinely creative paces you might not have thought yourself capable of.

**Here's another point worth chunking: even elite chess players sometimes have only average IQs.** These "middling" intellects consistently outperform smarter but less dedicated players — purely because they simply practice more. **That's the real, underlying principle.** Talent in chess — average or elite — grows through practice, specifically **deliberate practice on the toughest aspects of the material**, and that practice is exactly what lifts an "average" brain toward the level of someone with more apparently "natural" gifts. Just as lifting weights over time builds bigger muscles, practicing specific mental patterns genuinely deepens and enlarges them in the mind. Interestingly, practice may even directly expand working memory itself — research shows exercises like repeating steadily longer strings of digits backward can genuinely improve it.

**Gifted people carry their own real burdens too.** Highly gifted kids are sometimes bullied for it, and learn to hide or suppress their giftedness — something that can be genuinely hard to recover from later. Smarter people can also struggle precisely *because* they can vividly imagine every possible complexity, good and bad — and, notably, exceptionally smart people are actually *more* likely to procrastinate, since procrastination "worked" for them throughout school without real consequence — which can mean they never build certain critical life skills early on that others were forced to develop.

**Whether you're naturally gifted or have to genuinely fight for a solid grasp of the fundamentals, you're not alone if you sometimes feel like a fraud** — convinced good test results are just a fluke, certain that *this* time, everyone will finally see through you. This feeling is common enough to have a real name: the **impostor phenomenon**. If you struggle with this, know that a great many people around you secretly carry the same feeling.

**Everyone carries different gifts.** As the old saying goes: when one door closes, another opens. Keep your chin up, and your eye on the open one.

> "There are only two ways to live your life. One is as though nothing is a miracle. The other is as if everything is."
> — Albert Einstein

> **Mark Porter, biology teacher, Mira Loma High School, on the limits of pure top-grade performance:**
> "I coach Science Olympiad — we've won the state championship eight of the last nine years, and often finish top ten nationally. We've consistently found that many seemingly top students (straight A's across the board) don't actually perform well under Olympiad pressure, compared to students who can genuinely, mentally *manipulate* the knowledge they have. Interestingly, this 'second tier' of students often view themselves as less intelligent than the top scorers. I'd much rather field lower-scoring students who can think creatively on their feet — exactly what the Olympiad demands — than top students who get flustered the moment a question doesn't perfectly match the chunks they've memorized."

## Summing It Up

- Once material is genuinely well-chunked, you can let go of consciously tracking every detail, and start doing things automatically.
- It can feel intimidating to work alongside classmates who grasp material faster than you — but "average" students often carry real advantages in initiative, follow-through, and creativity.
- Part of the real secret to creativity is being able to shift between full, focused concentration and the relaxed, daydreamy diffuse mode.
- Focusing too intently can actually *block* the solution you're after — like trying to hammer in a screw because you've mistaken it for a nail. When genuinely stuck, sometimes the right move is stepping away from the problem entirely, or simply sleeping on it.

## A Story: From "Slow Learner" to Success

> **Nick Appleyard**, now a vice president leading the Americas business unit at a high-tech company building advanced physics simulation tools used across aerospace, automotive, energy, and biomedical industries, holds a mechanical engineering degree from the University of Sheffield in England.
>
> "Growing up, I was branded a slow learner, and a problem child because of it. Those labels cut deep — I felt like my teachers had genuinely given up on me. My parents grew frustrated too, especially my father, a senior physician at a major teaching hospital (I only learned much later that he'd faced similar struggles himself as a child). It became a vicious circle, undermining my confidence in nearly every part of life.

> The actual problem was math — fractions, times tables, long division, algebra, all of it felt boring and completely pointless.
>
> One day, without realizing it at the time, something started to shift. My father brought home a computer. I'd heard about teenagers writing home computer games everyone wanted to play, becoming overnight millionaires — I wanted to be one of them.
>
> I read, practiced, and kept writing more ambitious programs — every single one involving some form of math. Eventually, a popular UK computer magazine actually published one of my programs — a real thrill.
>
> Now, I see every day how mathematics is actually applied — designing the next generation of cars, helping put rockets into space, analyzing how the human body works.
>
> Mathematics stopped being pointless. It became a genuine source of wonder — and the foundation of a great career."

## Key Takeaways, Tips, and Tricks

- **Let understanding become automatic over time.** Once you genuinely grasp *why* a procedure works, stop re-deriving it from scratch every time — trust the chunk you've built, the way a batter trusts a practiced swing.
- **Real understanding comes from figuring it out yourself, not just being told.** Working through many varied problems builds deeper understanding than any explanation handed to you passively.
- **Don't overthink a well-practiced skill under pressure.** Consciously second-guessing something you already know well can slow you down and hurt performance — sometimes trained intuition should simply be trusted.
- **Watch for excessive rule-following getting in the way of genuine competence** — the CPR study is a real reminder that rigid procedural correctness and actual expertise aren't always the same thing.
- **A bigger working memory isn't an unqualified advantage.** It makes learning easier but can make you *more* susceptible to Einstellung — locking onto one idea so tightly that better, simpler alternatives get missed.
- **If you're prone to daydreaming or losing focus, you may have a genuine creative advantage.** A "looser" working memory gives other brain regions more room to contribute new, unexpected connections.
- **Deliberate practice — specifically on your hardest material — builds ability that can rival "natural" talent.** Even elite chess players are sometimes only average in raw IQ; consistent, targeted practice is what actually separates them.
- **You can likely expand your own working memory with practice** — exercises like recalling increasingly long digit strings backward have shown real improvement in research.
- **Watch for smart-person procrastination.** If avoidance "worked" for you throughout school without real consequence, you may not have built the coping skills others were forced to develop earlier — worth addressing deliberately now.
- **The "impostor phenomenon" is genuinely common — you're not alone in it.** Feeling like a fraud despite real success doesn't mean you actually are one.
- **Value creative, on-the-feet thinking as much as raw scores.** The ability to flexibly apply knowledge to an unfamiliar question often matters more in the real world than a perfect memorized-chunk match.
