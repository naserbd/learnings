# Chapter 2: Easy Does It — Why Trying Too Hard Can Be Part of the Problem

## The Kasparov Story

In a famous photo, chess grandmaster Garry Kasparov sits across from thirteen-year-old Magnus Carlsen — mid speed-chess game, a format that gives almost no time to think. Carlsen calmly wanders away from the board entirely. It's a bit like casually deciding to do a backflip mid-tightrope-walk across Niagara Falls.

Carlsen was partly just psyching Kasparov out — and it worked; a rattled Kasparov only managed a draw against him. But there's more going on here than gamesmanship. Carlsen — who went on to become the youngest top-rated chess player in history — was doing something that offers real insight into how the brain learns math and science.

To understand *why* this works, it helps to first understand two very different ways the brain actually thinks.

> **A quick tip before diving in:** when starting a new chapter or section in a math or science book, take a "picture walk" through it first — skim the headings, diagrams, photos, the summary, and any end-of-chapter questions, *before* actually reading it in depth. This feels backwards, since you haven't read anything yet — but it primes your brain, creating little mental "hooks" that make the material easier to grasp once you do read it properly. A minute or two spent this way pays off.

## Two Ways the Brain Thinks: Focused and Diffuse

Neuroscience research over the past couple decades has identified two distinct networks the brain switches between: a highly attentive state, and a more relaxed, resting-state network. These get called **focused mode** and **diffuse mode**, and both matter enormously for learning. You're generally in one or the other — not consciously both at once — though diffuse mode does seem to keep working quietly in the background even while you're not actively paying attention to something.

**Focused mode** is the direct, rational, step-by-step approach — essential for actually working through math and science problems. It's tied to the brain's prefrontal cortex (just behind your forehead). Turn your attention onto something, and focused mode switches on instantly, like a flashlight beam narrowed to a tight, penetrating point.

**Diffuse mode** is what happens when you relax your attention and let your mind wander. It's associated with big-picture thinking, and with the sudden flashes of insight that arrive on a problem you've been stuck on. It's less tied to one specific brain region — more genuinely spread ("diffused") throughout. Diffuse-mode insight usually depends on groundwork already laid by focused-mode thinking beforehand — diffuse mode needs the clay that focused mode gathered, in order to actually make the bricks.

(In reality, learning involves constant flickering activity across many brain regions and both hemispheres — genuinely more complex than a clean two-mode switch. But the focused/diffuse framing is a useful, practical simplification.)

### The Pinball Metaphor

Picture two pinball machines, side by side.

**Focused mode** is a machine with its bumpers packed close together. A thought — the pinball — launches and bounces rapidly along a tight, familiar path. This is what lets you think a precise thought quickly, *as long as* that thought follows a route you've already practiced. Multiplying numbers you already know how to multiply. Conjugating a Spanish verb you learned last week. Reviewing your swim stroke technique. In each case, the "well-trodden" path already exists, and focused mode races along it efficiently.

**Diffuse mode** is a machine with its bumpers spread far apart. A thought can travel much farther before hitting anything — which is exactly what you need when there's no existing well-trodden path to follow, because the problem or concept is genuinely new to you. The trade-off: you lose the ability to think anything precise or detailed while in this mode.

**A useful way to hold both ideas at once:** think of a flashlight you can adjust. Narrow it, and the beam penetrates deep into a small area (focused mode). Widen it, and it casts broadly, but weakly, over a much larger area (diffuse mode). If you're trying to understand or figure out something genuinely new, the move is to deliberately switch off precision-focused thinking for a while, and let the broader diffuse-mode beam do its work — long enough to land on a more promising angle. (Diffuse mode can't simply be switched on by command, though — more on how to actually trigger it shortly.)

## Why Math and Science Feel Harder Than Other Subjects

Focused-mode problem solving in math and science tends to take noticeably more effort than focused thinking about language or people — likely because humans simply haven't evolved, over millennia, to naturally manipulate mathematical ideas the way we've evolved to handle language. Two specific properties make math and science harder to grasp:

- **Abstractness.** You can point at an actual cow in a field and connect it to the letters "c-o-w." You can't point at a real-world object the "+" symbol is modeled after — the idea it represents is inherently more abstract.
- **Encryptedness.** A single symbol can stand in for multiple different operations or ideas — a multiplication sign, for instance, is really shorthand for repeated addition.

In the pinball metaphor, this abstractness and "encryptedness" make the bumpers effectively spongier — it takes real, extra repetition before those pathways harden enough for the pinball to bounce cleanly and predictably. **This is exactly why procrastination is especially costly in math and science specifically** — more on that shortly.

### The Einstellung Effect

A closely related trap: the **Einstellung effect** (the German word roughly means "installation" — think of it as accidentally *installing* a mental roadblock). This happens when your very first idea about a problem — even a flawed one — blocks you from ever finding a better one. In the pinball picture, this looks like your thought launching straight toward an old, familiar (but wrong) pattern in one part of the brain, while the actual solution sits somewhere else entirely, in a spot your first instinct never leads you toward.

This is especially easy to fall into in science, where your gut intuition about *why* something happens is sometimes flatly wrong — meaning you have to actively unlearn an old, incorrect idea at the very same time you're trying to learn the correct one.

**A common, costly mistake this leads to:** students jumping straight into homework problems without first reading the textbook, attending the lecture, or talking to someone who actually understands the material — essentially "jumping in the water before learning to swim." Without any prior grounding, focused-mode thinking has nothing solid to work from, and just bounces randomly, far from wherever the real solution actually is.

*(This isn't just an academic-subjects problem, either — a little research and self-awareness about how you actually reason can keep you from wasting money on bogus "scientific" product claims, or from misunderstanding the math behind something like a mortgage — both genuinely high-stakes, real-world examples of the same trap.)*

## Why Two Separate Modes Exist at All

There's a plausible evolutionary reason for having both modes. Think of a bird pecking the ground for grain — it needs sharp, focused attention to actually find tiny food particles, *and* it needs to simultaneously scan the horizon for predators like hawks. Splitting these two very different jobs across the two brain hemispheres is one efficient way to handle both survival tasks at once. Watch birds closely, and you'll notice they actually alternate — peck, then pause and scan, almost like toggling between focused and diffuse modes in real time.

Humans show a broadly similar split: the left hemisphere leans more toward focused attention and sequential, logical, step-by-step reasoning; the right leans more toward diffuse, big-picture scanning, social interaction, and emotional processing. **Important caveat:** this doesn't mean some people are simply "left-brained" or "right-brained" — research doesn't actually support that popular idea. Both hemispheres genuinely participate in both modes. Getting good at math and science — and being genuinely creative in it — requires strengthening and actively using *both* modes, not favoring one over the other.

### A Simple Demonstration

Give someone two triangles and ask them to form a square — easy, done instantly. Now hand them two *more* triangles and ask for another square. Most people's first instinct is to arrange them into a rectangle instead — because the focused-mode pattern from the first task is still active, and it's the natural, well-worn path to follow. Actually solving the second puzzle requires a small diffuse-mode leap: recognizing you need to completely rearrange the pieces in a way that doesn't follow the first pattern at all.

**The core takeaway:** genuinely difficult problems typically require *both* modes, working in sequence. You need real focused effort first — grade school already taught us that much — but diffuse mode is just as essential a part of actually solving a hard problem. And critically: as long as you're consciously, actively focusing on a problem, you're blocking diffuse mode from kicking in at all. Like a rally in ping-pong, there's no "win" unless the ball genuinely goes back and forth between the two.

> **From a student, on creativity (Shaun Wassell, computer engineering freshman):** "My best guitar riffs always came to me when I was 'just messing around,' not when I sat down determined to create something great — those attempts usually turned out clichéd. The same held true writing papers, brainstorming school projects, or working through a hard math problem. The harder I push my brain to be creative, the less creative the result actually is. Relaxation turns out to be a genuine part of doing good, hard work."

> **From a teaching professor, on confusion (Kenneth R. Leopold, Chemistry, University of Minnesota):** "Confusion is a healthy, necessary part of learning. Students who hit a problem they don't immediately know how to solve often conclude they're just bad at the subject — and this hits strong students especially hard, since breezing through high school never taught them that confusion is normal. Learning really *is* the process of working your way out of confusion. Simply articulating what's actually confusing you is 80 percent of the battle — by the time you've pinned down the real question, you've often already answered it."

## Try It Yourself: Shifting Modes

Here's a small exercise to actually *feel* the shift from focused to diffuse thinking: given a triangle made of coins pointing one direction, try to rearrange it into a triangle pointing the *opposite* direction, moving only three coins. The trick is that relaxing your mind — releasing tight focus and thinking about nothing in particular — tends to be exactly what surfaces the answer. (Some children solve this instantly; some highly intelligent professors give up entirely. Channeling a bit of "inner child" thinking genuinely helps.)

## The Procrastination Connection

Procrastination deserves special attention in math and science specifically, because of everything covered above. **When you procrastinate, you leave yourself only enough time for shallow, last-minute focused-mode cramming** — and you raise your own stress level in the process, since you're racing an unpleasant deadline the whole time. The resulting neural patterns end up faint, fragmented, and quickly forgotten — a genuinely shaky foundation. Cramming the night before a test, or rushing through homework at the last minute, leaves *no* real time for diffuse mode to contribute anything — no time to genuinely synthesize connections, or work through the harder underlying concepts.

> **A concrete anti-procrastination tip — the Pomodoro technique:** turn off your phone and anything else that might interrupt you. Set a timer for 25 minutes, and commit to that stretch of focused work on one task — any task. Don't worry about actually *finishing* it; just focus on working on it for those 25 minutes. When the timer goes off, reward yourself — check your phone, browse the web, whatever you enjoy. That reward genuinely matters as much as the work session itself. You'll likely be surprised how productive a single focused 25-minute block can be, especially once you stop fixating on "finishing" and just focus on *working*.
>
> **A more advanced version:** at the end of each day, identify the single most important task you'll need to tackle tomorrow, and write it down. The next day, work through at least three 25-minute focused sessions on your most important task(s). At day's end, look back at what you actually crossed off, genuinely enjoy that sense of progress, then jot down what you want to tackle next — this small bit of advance planning lets your diffuse mode start quietly working on tomorrow's problem overnight, in the background.

## Summing It Up

- The brain relies on two genuinely distinct thinking processes — focused and diffuse — and toggles between them rather than running both at once.
- Getting stumped by a new concept or problem the first time you encounter it is completely normal.
- Solving a new problem well requires *both* an initial period of real focused effort, *and* a later period of deliberately turning your attention away from it.
- The Einstellung effect is what happens when an early, flawed idea blocks you from finding a better one — switching from focused to diffuse mode is often exactly what breaks that block. Stay flexible: your first instinct about how to solve something can be genuinely misleading.

**Pause and recall:** before moving on, close the book, look away, and try to recall the main ideas of this chapter from memory alone. Don't worry if not much comes back the first few times you try this — it gets easier, and noticeably improves how much you actually retain, with practice.

## A Student's Story: Nadia Noui-Mehidi, Senior, Economics

"Calculus I in eleventh grade was a nightmare — so different from anything I'd learned before that I genuinely didn't know how to even approach learning it. I studied harder than ever, but no matter how many problems I did, nothing was really sinking in. I fell back on pure memorization to scrape by, and did poorly on the AP exam as a result.

I avoided math entirely for two years. Then, as a college sophomore, I retook Calculus I and got a 4.0. I don't think I'd gotten any smarter in those two years — what actually changed was *how* I was approaching the subject.

Looking back, I think I was stuck entirely in focused-mode thinking in high school (Einstellung!) — convinced that if I just kept attacking problems the exact same way, it would eventually click.

I tutor math and economics now, and the students I work with almost always share the same issue: they're fixated on the surface-level details of a problem, hunting for clues on how to solve it, rather than genuinely understanding the problem itself. I don't think you can really *teach* someone how to think differently — it's a personal journey — but here's what's genuinely helped me:

1. **I read the book, rather than just listening to a lecture** — that's simply how I understand things best. I skim first to get the general shape of the chapter, then read it closely, more than once (though not back to back).
2. **If the book alone doesn't fully click, I search online or watch a video on the same topic** — not because the book or professor was inadequate, but because hearing the same idea phrased slightly differently can shift your angle on it just enough to spark real understanding.
3. **I think most clearly while driving.** Taking a break to just drive around genuinely helps — though I need to be at least somewhat occupied, since sitting still and 'just thinking' tends to leave me bored or distracted instead."

## Key Takeaways: Tips and Tricks From This Chapter

- **Do a "picture walk" before reading.** Skim headings, diagrams, summaries, and end-of-chapter questions *before* reading a section in depth — it primes your brain and makes the real reading easier to absorb.
- **You have two thinking modes — use both.** Focused mode (tight, step-by-step, detail-oriented) is essential for working a problem. Diffuse mode (relaxed, big-picture, wandering) is essential for genuine breakthroughs and new understanding. You can't run both at once, and you can't force diffuse mode on command — but you can deliberately step away to let it happen.
- **Getting stuck at first is normal, not a sign you're bad at the subject.** New concepts almost always feel confusing before they click — that confusion is part of the process, not evidence of failure.
- **Watch for the Einstellung effect.** If your first idea about a problem isn't working, that idea itself may be actively blocking a better one. Deliberately switching to diffuse mode — stepping away — can break the block.
- **Don't "jump in the water before learning to swim."** Read the material, attend the lecture, or talk to someone knowledgeable *before* diving into homework — tackling problems with zero grounding wastes focused-mode effort on essentially random guessing.
- **Procrastination is especially costly in math and science**, because these subjects need real focused-effort time *plus* downstream diffuse-mode processing — cramming leaves room for neither.
- **Use the Pomodoro technique to fight procrastination:** 25 minutes of distraction-free, focused work on one task, then a genuine reward. Don't aim to finish — aim to work. Do this for your most important task(s) each day, and jot down tomorrow's priority the night before, so diffuse mode can quietly work on it overnight.
- **Practice "pause and recall."** After reading, close the material and try to recall the key points from memory alone — this measurably strengthens retention over time.
- **Relaxation is part of hard work, not opposed to it.** Real creative breakthroughs — in guitar riffs, papers, or math problems alike — tend to arrive when you're not straining for them.
- **Confusion is 80% of the battle.** Simply pinning down exactly *what* is confusing you often gets you most of the way to the actual answer.
- **If one explanation doesn't click, try another source.** A different phrasing — a video, a different textbook, a different person's explanation — can unlock understanding that one source alone couldn't.
- **Multiple passes help.** Skim first for the big picture, then read closely — and consider rereading dense material more than once, spaced apart rather than back-to-back.
