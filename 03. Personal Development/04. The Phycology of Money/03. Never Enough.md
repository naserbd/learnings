## Chapter 3: Never Enough

### Opening Idea: The Word "Enough"
Housel opens with a story told by Vanguard founder John Bogle: at a lavish 
party, someone points out that their billionaire host had made more money 
in a single day than novelist Joseph Heller had earned from his entire 
bestselling career. Heller replies that he has something the billionaire 
never will — "enough."

Housel finds this word strikingly powerful. His concern: for a lot of the 
wealthiest and most powerful people today, there seems to be no fixed idea 
of what "enough" even means — the target keeps moving.

### Story 1: Rajat Gupta
Gupta rose from an orphaned childhood in Kolkata to become CEO of 
McKinsey, one of the most respected consulting firms in the world. He sat 
on major boards, partnered with Bill Gates on philanthropy, and by 2008 was 
worth roughly $100 million.

That wasn't enough for him — he wanted to be a billionaire. While serving 
on Goldman Sachs's board, he learned in advance that Warren Buffett was 
about to make a huge investment in the bank during the 2008 financial 
crisis. Within seconds of learning this, he tipped off a hedge fund 
manager, who used the info to make a fast $1 million. Prosecutors say 
Gupta's leaks generated $17 million in illegal profits overall. He was 
caught and sent to prison, permanently destroying his reputation.

### Story 2: Bernie Madoff
Long before his infamous Ponzi scheme, Madoff ran a completely legitimate 
and highly profitable stock-trading business — reportedly earning $25–50 
million a year fairly. He was already a hugely successful, legitimately 
wealthy businessman. Yet he still built one of history's biggest financial 
frauds on top of that success.

**The common thread:** both men already had enormous wealth, respect, and 
freedom — and threw it all away chasing more. They had no internal sense 
of "enough."

### A Non-Criminal Example: Long-Term Capital Management
A hedge fund staffed by traders already personally worth tens or hundreds 
of millions of dollars kept taking bigger risks chasing more — and 
collapsed entirely in 1998, during one of the strongest economies in 
history. Warren Buffett summed it up: risking money you need for money you 
don't need is simply foolish, no matter how smart you are.

### Key Lessons & Advice

**1. The hardest financial skill is making your goalpost stop moving.**
If your expectations rise every time your results improve, you'll never 
actually feel satisfied — you'll just keep taking bigger risks to "catch 
up" to your own rising ambitions. Happiness is often just your results 
minus your expectations.

**2. Social comparison is a trap you can't win.**
Housel illustrates this with a chain: a rookie athlete earning $500K feels 
poor next to a $36M/year superstar teammate — who in turn looks poor next 
to top hedge fund managers earning hundreds of millions — who look poor 
next to billionaires gaining billions per year. No matter how much you 
have, there's always someone with more. The only way to "win" this 
comparison game is to not play it — accept that you may have enough, even 
if others around you seem to have more. (Housel includes a great line from 
a Vegas dealer: "The only way to win in a casino is to exit as soon as you 
enter" — the same logic applies to chasing other people's wealth.)

**3. "Enough" isn't the same as "too little" or giving up ambition.**
Wanting enough isn't about settling — it's about avoiding the regret that 
comes from an appetite that never stops. Just like eating past the point of 
fullness eventually makes you sick, endlessly chasing more money 
eventually catches up with you — sometimes as burnout, sometimes as 
financial ruin, and in extreme cases, as fraud.

**4. Some things are never worth risking, no matter the potential reward.**
Reputation, freedom, relationships, and happiness are described as 
invaluable — meaning no financial gain is big enough to justify risking 
them. Knowing when to stop is what protects these things.

### Chapter Summary
- "Enough" means recognizing a point where you stop chasing more — without 
  it, no amount of money ever feels satisfying.
- Rajat Gupta went from an orphaned childhood to a $100M fortune, then threw 
  it away — and went to prison — chasing billionaire status through insider 
  trading.
- Bernie Madoff was already legitimately wealthy and successful before 
  building one of history's largest Ponzi schemes — proof that success 
  itself doesn't create a sense of "enough."
- Long-Term Capital Management's ultra-wealthy traders lost everything by 
  risking money they didn't need to chase money they didn't need.
- Tip: the hardest financial skill is stopping your expectations from 
  rising every time your results improve.
- Tip: avoid comparing yourself to people wealthier than you — the ladder 
  never ends, so it's a game you can't win by playing.
- Tip: "enough" isn't weakness or settling — it's what protects you from an 
  appetite for more that eventually causes real damage.
- Tip: some things — reputation, freedom, relationships, happiness — are 
  never worth risking for extra money, no matter how good the potential 
  payoff looks.
- Bottom line: know when you have enough, because the goalpost will keep 
  moving unless you consciously decide to stop it.
