# Chapter 7: Chunking versus Choking — How to Increase Your Expertise and Reduce Anxiety

Inventions almost never arrive fully formed. The first "mobile" phones were the size and weight of bowling balls. Early refrigerators were clunky industrial devices used mainly by breweries. The earliest engines were oversized, underpowered monstrosities, barely stronger than a modern go-kart. Real improvement comes only *after* an invention exists and people have had time to actually tinker with it — engine turbocharging, for instance, only emerged once engineers already had a working engine to experiment on, competing to squeeze more power out of the same basic design.

## How to Build a Powerful Chunk

Just like an invention, your chunking skills can be deliberately refined and enhanced. A solid personal library of chunks improves both test performance and creative problem-solving — and lays the real groundwork for expertise in anything you're working on. *(Jumping from procrastination back to chunking here is itself a deliberate example of interleaving — revisiting an earlier topic after a break to reinforce it.)*

**Here's a genuinely useful reframe:** learning the fundamental concepts of math and science can actually be *easier* than subjects requiring heavy rote memorization — without downplaying how demanding memorization can be (just ask any med student cramming for boards). Part of why: once you're actually working a math or science problem, each completed step naturally signals what comes next. Internalizing problem-solving techniques strengthens the neural activity that lets you actually "hear" your growing intuition. When you truly, deeply know how to solve a problem just by glancing at it, you've built a genuinely commanding chunk — one that flows through your mind almost like a familiar tune. A real library of these chunks gives you a grasp of fundamentals nothing else can match.

### Steps to Building a Powerful Chunk

1. **Work a key problem all the way through, on paper**, with a solution available to check against (but don't peek unless you genuinely must). No skipping steps, no "yeah, I basically get it" shortcuts — make sure every single step genuinely makes sense before moving on.
2. **Do it again**, paying close attention to the key processes involved. It might feel odd to repeat a problem you've already solved — but you'd never learn a song on guitar, or build strength, from doing it just once.
3. **Take a real break.** Work at a job, study something else, go play basketball — anything different. This gives diffuse mode genuine time to internalize what you just worked through.
4. **Sleep on it.** Right before bed, work through the problem once more. If you get stuck, "listen" to it — let your subconscious surface the next step rather than forcing it.
5. **Repeat again the next day, as soon as you can.** You should notice it's genuinely faster and deeper this time — you might even wonder why it ever felt hard. At this point, start easing up on the routine parts of the problem, and put your focus squarely on whatever part is genuinely still the hardest for you. This sustained focus on the truly difficult parts is called **deliberate practice** — tiring sometimes, but one of the single most valuable habits in real studying. (Alternatively, or in addition, try a genuinely *similar* problem and see how easily it comes now.)
6. **Add a new problem to your library.** Pick another key problem, and run it through the same five steps. Repeat, and move on to the next once you're comfortable. Even a handful of solidly built chunks can dramatically boost your overall mastery and problem-solving speed.
7. **Do "active" repetitions.** Mentally review the key steps of a problem while doing something physical — walking to the library, exercising, waiting for a bus, sitting in a car, killing time before class. This kind of active mental rehearsal genuinely strengthens your ability to recall the material exactly when it matters — homework, or a real test.

That's the full process for building a real chunked library — strengthening an increasingly interconnected web of neurons. This leans on what's called the **generation effect**: actively generating (recalling) material from memory teaches you far more effectively than simply rereading it.

**"But I'm already spending hours a week just solving each problem once — how am I supposed to do this four separate times per problem?"** Fair question — but ask yourself what your real goal actually is. Turning in homework? Or genuinely mastering the material well enough to perform on the tests that actually determine your grade? Solving a problem once, with the book open in front of you, doesn't guarantee you could solve something similar cold on a test — and it definitely doesn't guarantee real understanding. If time is tight, apply this deliberate-practice approach to just a *few* genuinely key problems — it still meaningfully accelerates and strengthens your learning.

> **The Law of Serendipity:** luck favors the one who actually tries. Don't feel overwhelmed by everything there is to learn in a new subject — focus on nailing down a handful of genuinely key ideas first. That simple framework carries more weight than it seems like it should.

**A useful comparison: how musicians actually practice.** A master violinist doesn't just play a piece start to finish, over and over — she isolates the *hardest* passages, the spots where her fingers fumble or her focus slips, and drills those specifically. Apply the same logic to your own deliberate practice: zero in on the genuinely toughest parts of a solution procedure, and get faster there specifically.

Research consistently shows that the more effort you put into *recalling* material, the more deeply it actually embeds — recall, not rereading, is the real engine of deliberate practice. This mirrors how chess grandmasters train, internalizing board positions as chunks tightly linked to the best next move — the real gap between an average player and a grandmaster is how much deliberate time the grandmaster spends specifically identifying and strengthening their own weak spots, not just playing casually for fun. It's more work than it sounds — but the payoff is genuinely worth it.

Retrieval practice remains one of the single most powerful learning tools available, far more productive than passive rereading. A chunked-solution library works precisely *because* it's built on repeated retrieval, not repeated review. Don't let illusions of competence fool you — staring at material already sitting on the page in front of you can easily feel like knowledge, without actually being any.

**This process can feel awkward at first** — like a thirty-year-old sitting down for their very first piano lesson. Stick with it, and it comes together more quickly and smoothly than expected. Be patient with yourself; as ease grows, so does genuine enjoyment. Yes, it's work — so is learning piano with real style — but the payoff more than justifies the effort.

> **Mike Orrell, junior, electrical engineering:**
> "Between being a full-time engineering student and working full-time as an engineering tech, I have far too much to keep at the front of my mind. My trick: build separate big 'chunks' for each subject area — thermo, machine design, programming. When I need to recall a specific project, I set aside whatever I'm currently focused on and pull up the relevant chunk, like clicking a link on a desktop. I can zoom in on one specific area, or step back into diffuse mode and scan across the whole 'desktop' for conceptual links between chunks. A clean, organized mental desktop makes connections come much more easily — it sharpens my mental agility, and also lets me dig deeper into any one topic when I need to."

## Hitting the Wall: When Understanding Suddenly Seems to Collapse

Learning genuinely doesn't progress in a smooth, linear line — it's not a neat daily stack of new knowledge added on top of the last. Sometimes you genuinely hit a wall: things that made complete sense yesterday suddenly feel confusing again today.

**This "knowledge collapse" seems to happen while your mind is actively restructuring its understanding**, building a more solid foundation underneath. Language learners hit this often — a foreign language that felt manageable can suddenly, briefly, feel as incomprehensible as an alien tongue.

Assimilating new knowledge genuinely takes real time. You'll go through stretches that feel like an exasperating step backward — this is a completely natural sign your mind is genuinely wrestling with the material at a deeper level. Push through these periods of temporary frustration, and you'll typically emerge with a real, surprising step forward on the other side.

## Organizing Your Materials for Test Prep

Before a test, keep your worked problems and solutions genuinely well-organized, so you can review them quickly. Some students tape handwritten solutions directly onto the relevant textbook pages (painter's tape or sticky notes work well if the book needs to stay resellable). Writing the solution by hand matters — handwriting genuinely improves retention over typing. Alternatively, keep a dedicated binder of key problems and solutions from class and the textbook, ready to review before every test.

> **William James, one of history's most influential psychologists, on active vs. passive recall:**
> "A curious peculiarity of our memory is that things are impressed better by active than by passive repetition. In learning something by heart, when we almost know it, it pays better to wait and recollect it through effort from within, than to look at the book again. Recover the words the first way, and we'll likely know them next time; recover them the second way, and we'll probably need the book again."

## Testing as a Powerful Learning Tool — Give Yourself Constant Mini-Tests

Well-chunked solution methods held genuinely at the ready are exactly what protects you from **choking** on a test — the kind of panicked freeze that happens when working memory is already at capacity, with no room left for the additional pieces a problem actually needs. Chunking compresses knowledge down, freeing up working memory space, so you're far less likely to hit that mental overload — and far more likely to actually recall the details you need mid-problem.

**Practicing this way is itself a form of mini-testing.** Research shows testing isn't just a way to *measure* what you know — it's a genuinely powerful form of learning in its own right. It actively changes and adds to what you know, and dramatically improves retention. This improvement is called the **testing effect**, and it appears to work because testing directly strengthens and stabilizes the relevant neural patterns (the same "darkening pattern" idea from Chapter 4's "Practice Makes Permanent" section).

**Interestingly, the testing effect kicks in even when you perform badly on the test and receive no feedback at all.** That said, when self-testing while actually studying, do try to check your answers — a solutions manual, the back of the book, wherever you can find genuine feedback. (Interacting with peers and instructors also genuinely helps here, covered later.)

**Part of why building a solid chunk library works so well is that the *process itself* is packed with mini-tests.** Research consistently shows students — and even educators — are often genuinely unaware of how powerful this kind of retrieval-based mini-testing actually is. Students tend to assume a quick self-quiz is just a check-in on their progress — but that active recall is actually one of the single best learning methods available, well ahead of passively sitting and rereading. Building your chunk library through repeated, active retrieval and self-testing is genuinely one of the strongest learning strategies you have available.

### Try It: Build a Mental Solution Library

Building a library of chunked solution patterns is your rapid-access mental data bank, ready in a pinch — and this idea extends well beyond math and science. It's the same logic behind a good habit like checking where the emergency exits are relative to your airplane seat or hotel room — a mental chunk you'd rather have ready before you actually need it.

## Summing It Up

- **Chunking** means weaving a concept into one smooth, connected neural pattern.
- Chunking effectively increases how much working memory you have available for other things.
- Building a real library of chunked concepts and solutions genuinely builds problem-solving intuition.
- While building that library, keep deliberate focus specifically on the toughest concepts and hardest parts of a problem.
- Sometimes hard work still meets bad luck — but the **Law of Serendipity** holds: consistent preparation and practice tilts luck increasingly in your favor. Not trying guarantees failure; consistent effort brings far more wins over time.

## Key Takeaways, Tips, and Tricks

- **Follow the seven-step chunk-building process:** work the problem fully yourself → repeat it → take a real break → sleep on it → repeat again the next day, easing off the easy parts and drilling the hard ones → add a new problem to your library → do "active" mental repetitions during everyday moments (walking, waiting, commuting).
- **Deliberate practice means specifically drilling your weakest, hardest points** — not repeating the whole problem or piece equally, the way a master musician isolates the passage they keep fumbling.
- **Retrieval beats rereading, every time.** Recalling material from memory (the "generation effect") embeds it far more deeply than passively looking it over again.
- **Don't overload every problem with this full process if time is tight** — apply it deliberately to just a handful of genuinely key problems for the biggest return.
- **Expect occasional "knowledge collapse"** — a sudden sense that something you understood is now confusing again. This is a normal sign your understanding is being rebuilt on a deeper foundation, not a sign you're falling behind.
- **Write solutions out by hand**, and keep them organized (taped into your textbook, or in a dedicated binder) for quick pre-test review.
- **Use active recall over passive review** — try to remember something from within before flipping back to check it, not the other way around.
- **Treat self-testing as real learning, not just a progress check.** The "testing effect" means the act of testing yourself — even without feedback — measurably strengthens memory on its own.
- **Get feedback when you self-test**, using a solutions manual, answer key, or another person, to catch mistakes you'd otherwise reinforce.
- **Build a "chunk library" the way you'd note emergency exits on a plane** — not because you'll need every entry immediately, but because having it ready removes panic when you actually do.
- **Tackle your hardest subject when you're mentally freshest** (often morning), and expect real breakthroughs to arrive during unrelated downtime — a shower, a walk — once you deliberately step away from the problem.
- **Avoid highlighting as a primary study method** — it creates a false sense of having learned something. Favor active recall instead.
