# Chapter 11: More Memory Tips

## Create a Lively Visual Metaphor or Analogy

One of the best ways to both remember *and* genuinely understand a math or science concept is to build a metaphor or analogy for it — the more visual, the better. A metaphor is simply recognizing that one thing resembles another in some useful way. A geography teacher once described Syria as shaped like a bowl of cereal, and Jordan as shaped like a Nike Air Jordan sneaker — simple images students remembered for decades afterward.

**Trying to understand electrical current?** Picture it as flowing water. Voltage can "feel like" pressure — pushing current where you want it to go, the same way physical pressure pushes water through a pump. As your understanding deepens, feel free to revise a metaphor, or discard it entirely for something more accurate.

**Trying to grasp limits in calculus?** Picture a runner approaching a finish line — the closer he gets, the slower he moves, in one of those slow-motion shots where he never quite reaches the ribbon, just as a value approaches (but never quite touches) its limit. (The short classic *Calculus Made Easy* by Silvanus Thompson has helped generations of students exactly this way — sometimes a textbook gets so buried in detail that the big-picture concept gets lost, and a short, focused book can restore that clarity.)

**Try becoming the concept itself.** Imagine yourself as an electron burrowing through a slab of copper, or slip inside the "x" of an algebraic equation and feel what it's like poking your head out of the rabbit hole (just don't let it get vaporized by an accidental divide-by-zero).

> **Anthony Sciuto, senior, industrial and systems engineering:**
> "I always study right before bed, and for some reason I usually end up dreaming about the material. These 'school dreams' are strange, but genuinely useful — in an operations research class, I dreamed I was physically running back and forth between nodes, acting out the shortest-path algorithm myself. People think I'm crazy, but I think it's great — it means I don't have to study as much as others do. I think my subconscious is basically building metaphors on its own."

**In chemistry:** a *cation* (positive ion) is like a cat with paws — "pawsitive." An *anion* (negative ion) is like an onion — it makes you cry, hence "negative."

**No metaphor is ever perfect** — but then, every scientific model is really just a metaphor too, and every model eventually breaks down somewhere. That's fine — metaphors (and models) still give you real, physical intuition for the underlying idea you're studying. Interestingly, metaphors can genuinely help break Einstellung — getting stuck fixated on a flawed approach. A simple story about soldiers attacking a fortress from many directions at once, for instance, has helped students intuitively grasp how many weak, low-intensity radiation beams can be combined to destroy a tumor without damaging surrounding tissue.

**Metaphors help ideas stick because they hook into neural structures you already have** — like tracing over a pattern with tracing paper, giving you a real sense of what's happening underneath. If you're stuck for a metaphor, just grab a pen and paper — whether through words or a quick sketch, a minute or two of genuine noodling often surfaces something useful.

> **A quick historical example:** in the 1800s, chemists began genuinely visualizing molecules — and progress accelerated dramatically once they could picture this miniature world directly. A famous 1886 satirical illustration from German academic chemistry even drew a benzene ring as a circle of monkeys — single bonds shown as linked hands, double bonds as linked tiny tails.

## Spaced Repetition: Locking Ideas into Memory

Focusing attention brings something into temporary working memory — but for it to move into *long-term* memory, two things genuinely need to happen: **the idea needs to be memorable**, and **it needs to be repeated**. Without repetition, your brain's natural "metabolic vampires" quietly suck away faint, newly formed connections — which is actually a *good* thing overall, since most of what surrounds you day to day is genuinely trivial. Remembering everything would leave you a hoarder of useless memories.

**Repetition matters even for already-memorable material** — it's what genuinely locks a vivid image into long-term memory for good. But how many repetitions? How much time between them? And can the process itself be made more effective?

**A worked example — remembering that density is symbolized "ρ" (pronounced "row"), measured in kilograms per cubic meter:**

1. Write "ρ" on one side of an index card, and the rest of the info on the other. **Writing by hand appears to genuinely deepen encoding** — converting the idea into a real neural structure — more effectively than typing.
2. While writing "kilograms per cubic meter," build a vivid image: a shadowy kilogram (feel its actual mass!) crammed into an oversized suitcase, exactly one meter on each side.
3. **Say the word and its meaning out loud** — this starts building an auditory hook alongside the visual one.
4. Look at just the "ρ" side of the card, and try recalling the rest from memory. If you can't, flip it over and remind yourself. If you can, set the card aside.
5. Make another card, test yourself on it too. Once you've built up several, shuffle through the whole set together — this naturally interleaves your review. Expect some struggle here — that's fine.
6. Set the cards aside, then pull them out again right before bed — sleep is when your brain replays patterns and assembles solutions.
7. **Briefly revisit the cards over several days** — a few minutes each morning or evening, occasionally reshuffling their order. As mastery grows, gradually stretch the gap between reviews longer — this increasing spacing is exactly what locks material in most firmly. (Good spaced-repetition apps like Anki automate this scheduling for you, spacing reviews from days out to months.)

**Interestingly, remembering people's names works the same way** — deliberately try recalling a new name at gradually increasing intervals after first hearing it. Material you never review tends to fade or get discounted faster, since the "metabolic vampires" strip away the unused connections. This is exactly why you should be genuinely careful about what you decide to skip when reviewing for a test — skipping related-but-unreviewed material can weaken your memory for it too.

> **Fabian Hadipriono Tan, Professor of Civil Engineering, The Ohio State University:**
> "I advise my students to use spaced repetition over days and weeks — not just in analytical courses, but even in my History of Ancient Engineering class. When memorizing unfamiliar names and terms, spreading practice over several days always works best. In fact, that's exactly what I do preparing my own lectures — repeating terms out loud over several days, so they roll easily off my tongue in class."

## Create Meaningful Groups

Another key memory tool: build **meaningful groups** that simplify the material. Trying to remember four vampire-warding plants — garlic, rose, hawthorn, mustard? The first letters spell GRHM — close enough to "GRAHAM," so you just picture a graham cracker (pull it off the kitchen table in your memory palace, dust off the missing vowels, and you're set).

**Numbers stick better tied to a memorable event or an already-familiar number system.** The year 1965 might be when a relative was born; 11.0 seconds is a solid 100-meter dash time; 75 might be a familiar knitting stitch count. One personal approach: tying numbers to ages and life stages — 18 is easy to remember (heading out into the world), and 104 conjures the image of a happy, elderly great-grandmother.

**Many fields lean on mnemonic sentences**, where the first letter of each word matches the first letter of each item on a list to memorize. Medicine is full of these — "Some Lovers Try Positions that They Can't Handle" for the carpal bones, "Old People from Texas Eat Spiders" for the cranial bones. For the metric system's scale of ten: **"King Henry died while drinking chocolate milk"** — Kilo (1,000), Hecto (100), Deca (10), "while" (1), Deci (0.1), Centi (0.01), Milli (0.001).

**These tricks genuinely work, over and over.** If you're memorizing something commonly taught, search online — someone's likely already built a memorable version. Otherwise, build your own.

> **William Pietro, Professor of Chemistry, York University:**
> "In chemistry, we use 'skit ti vicer man feconi kuzin' — with the cadence of a rap — to remember the first row of transition metals (Sc, Ti, V, Cr, Mn, Fe, Co, Ni, Cu, Zn). Students then place the rest with further tricks — remembering that Ag (silver) and Au (gold) sit in the same column as Cu (copper), since all three are used to make coins.
>
> Unfortunately, some students walk away actually believing *that's* the real reason those metals share a column — because they're all used for coins. The real reason is shared chemical properties and valence. This is a genuine risk with memory tricks: mistaking the trick itself for the actual underlying knowledge. Always stay aware of the difference between the metaphor helping your memory, and what's genuinely going on underneath it."

## Create Stories

Notice how these tricks often build genuine meaning through a small story — poor King Henry, drinking that chocolate milk! Storytelling has always been a powerful way to understand and retain information. Historian of science Vera Pavri encourages her students to treat lectures not as lectures, but as *stories* — with a plot, characters, and a real driving purpose. The best math and science lectures are often framed like a thriller, opening with a genuinely intriguing problem you're pulled into wanting to solve. If a lecture or textbook doesn't hand you that hook naturally, try to find the compelling question yourself — then set out to answer it. Don't underestimate story as a memory tool in its own right.

> **Jason Dechant, Ph.D., School of Nursing, University of Pittsburgh:**
> "The number one thing I stress with struggling students: there's a direct link between your hand and your brain, and rewriting and organizing notes by hand is essential for breaking large amounts of information into smaller, digestible chunks. Many students prefer typing notes into a document or slides — and when they're struggling, my first recommendation is always: stop typing, start writing. Every single time, they perform better on the next section of material."

## Muscle Memory

Writing something out by hand appears to genuinely help cement it in memory. While the research here is still limited, many educators have observed a real "muscle memory" effect tied specifically to handwriting. An equation that looks like meaningless symbols at first glance can genuinely start to "come alive" after writing it out a few times by hand. Some learners also find reading a problem or formula out loud helps deepen understanding. (One caution: writing an equation out a hundred times by hand isn't the goal — the first few repetitions genuinely add value, but beyond that, it becomes rote busywork, and your time is better spent elsewhere.)

> **Dina Miyoshi, Assistant Professor of Psychology, San Diego Mesa College:**
> "I often tell students to talk to themselves instead of just highlighting and rereading. They usually look at me like I'm completely insane (which might be true) — but plenty of them come back later and tell me it genuinely worked, and it's now a real part of their study toolkit."

## Real Muscle Memory: Exercise

If you genuinely want to boost memory and overall learning ability, one of the best tools available is simply **exercise**. Multiple studies in both animals and humans show that regular physical activity meaningfully improves memory and learning — apparently by helping generate new neurons in memory-related brain regions, and building new signaling pathways between them. Different exercise types (aerobic vs. strength training) may work through subtly different biological mechanisms — but both produce genuinely strong benefits for learning and memory.

## Memory Tricks Help You Become an Expert Faster

**The bottom line: using mental images instead of bare words to remember things helps you reach expert-level understanding faster.** Learning to process math and science visually is genuinely a powerful path toward mastering the material — and layering in other memory tricks meaningfully accelerates learning and retention further.

**Purists sometimes dismiss these "gimmicks" as not real learning — but the research says otherwise.** Students who use these techniques consistently outperform those who don't, and brain-imaging research on how people develop real expertise shows these tools genuinely speed up the formation of both individual chunks and broader conceptual templates — turning novices into semi-experts in a matter of weeks, not years. Memory tricks essentially let you expand your effective working memory by making long-term memory more easily and quickly accessible.

**The memorization process itself becomes a genuine exercise in creativity.** The more you memorize this way, the more creative you become — because you're building wild, unexpected potential connections right from the start, even while you're still absorbing the basic material. Keep exercising this "memory muscle," and it gets dramatically faster — an evocative image that took 15 minutes to build and place in your memory palace at first might take only seconds once you're genuinely practiced.

**Internalizing key material this way also deepens real understanding.** A formula you've genuinely committed to memory carries far more meaning than one you'd simply look up — and you'll wield it far more fluently, both on tests and in real-world application.

**One study of professional actors found they don't actually memorize their lines verbatim** — instead, they rely on genuinely understanding their character's needs and motivations, and the lines follow from that understanding. The same logic applies to your own studying: the real priority is understanding what a formula or solution step actually *means* — that understanding makes the memorization itself much easier.

You might object that you're "just not creative" — that an equation surely can't have its own dramatic motivations to help you remember it. But your inner child's creativity is still genuinely there — it just needs to be deliberately invited back in.

> **William Koehler, sophomore, mechanical engineering:**
> "While working toward my engineering degree, I'm also two months from finishing my paramedic license, which means memorizing a huge list of drugs and dosages for adult and pediatric patients — initially overwhelming, especially knowing lives are at stake. But I quickly found little tricks that made it manageable. Take furosemide (Lasix), which draws fluid out of the body — the dose I needed was 40 milligrams. That felt like a gift, since '4-0' is basically hiding right inside the word: *4-0*semide = furosemide. Little things like that genuinely cement knowledge in your head — I don't even have to think twice about it anymore. Truly remarkable."

## Summing It Up

- **Metaphors** help you learn difficult ideas faster.
- **Repetition** is essential for locking in what you want to remember before it fades.
- **Meaningful groups and abbreviations** simplify material, making it easier to chunk and store in memory.
- **Stories** — even silly memory tricks framed as tiny stories — help material stick.
- **Writing and saying** what you're learning out loud both appear to strengthen retention.
- **Exercise** is genuinely powerful for helping neurons grow and form new connections.

## Key Takeaways, Tips, and Tricks

- **Build a vivid, visual metaphor for hard concepts** — the sillier and more physical, the better it sticks. Revise or discard it as your understanding deepens.
- **Try "becoming" the concept** — imagine yourself as the electron, the variable, the molecule — to build genuine intuition, not just memorized facts.
- **Metaphors can break Einstellung** — a fresh analogy can dislodge a flawed mental approach you're stuck on.
- **Never mistake the memory trick for the real explanation** — a mnemonic gets you to recall the fact; make sure you separately understand *why* it's actually true.
- **Use the flashcard method deliberately**: write by hand, build a vivid image, say it out loud, self-test, shuffle with other cards, review again before bed, then space out reviews over days as mastery grows.
- **Space your repetition, and let it grow over time** — cramming close together works far worse than spreading review out, with growing intervals as material solidifies.
- **Turn a list into a word or sentence mnemonic** (first letters of each item), and lean on existing ones online before inventing your own.
- **Tie numbers to something already meaningful** — a birth year, a life stage, a familiar stat — rather than memorizing them as bare digits.
- **Treat lectures and textbook sections like stories** — find (or invent) the compelling question driving the material, rather than treating it as a flat list of facts.
- **Write by hand rather than typing** when the goal is genuine retention — it appears to encode material more deeply.
- **Say things out loud while studying** — it feels strange, but consistently helps people who try it.
- **Exercise regularly** — it's one of the most effective, non-subject-specific things you can do to improve memory and learning generally.
- **Understand before you memorize where possible** — like actors who learn a character's motivation rather than lines verbatim, understanding *why* something is true makes memorizing it dramatically easier.
- **Don't overdo repetitive drilling** — writing the same thing dozens of times past the point of usefulness becomes rote busywork; redirect that time once the value drops off.
