# Chapter 5: Preventing Procrastination — Enlisting Your Habits ("Zombies") as Helpers

## The Arsenic Eaters

For centuries, arsenic was a go-to poison — a sprinkle on morning toast, and death followed within a day. So imagine the shock at an 1875 science conference in Germany, when two men calmly swallowed more than double a lethal dose of arsenic in front of the audience — and showed up the next day, smiling and healthy. Urine tests confirmed it wasn't a trick — they'd genuinely ingested the poison.

**How does someone survive something that deadly, and even look healthy doing it?** The answer connects, surprisingly directly, to procrastination. Understanding the psychology behind procrastination — much like understanding the chemistry of a poison — is what actually lets you build real, lasting defenses against it.

This chapter (and the next couple) cover a genuinely "lazy person's" approach to beating procrastination — built around understanding your inner "zombies": the automatic, habitual responses your brain falls into whenever it hits a specific cue. These zombie habits are generally built to make the *immediate present* feel better — but with the right tricks, you can actually redirect some of them to work *for* you instead of against you. (Not all procrastination is even bad, as you'll see later.)

**One important note up front: willpower is expensive.** It draws on real, limited mental resources — unlike procrastination, which you fall into effortlessly. That means willpower is *not* the tool to spray liberally at procrastination like air freshener. Save it for when it's genuinely necessary — and as this chapter shows, you usually won't even need much of it.

> **A calculus student, on distraction:**
> "Procrastination is one of our generation's biggest problems — there are just so many distractions. I always think, 'before I start homework, let me just check Facebook, Twitter, Tumblr, email.' Before I even notice, an hour's gone. Even once I finally start, those distracting tabs are still open in the background. I need to find a way to just focus — it depends a lot on my environment and timing. I shouldn't be waiting until the last minute for everything."

## Procrastination Is Really About Discomfort

Imagine training for a marathon by doing your very first practice run at midnight, the night before the race — your calves would revolt. Cramming for math and science works exactly the same way. Learning these subjects genuinely depends on two things: **short study sessions** that lay down new mental "bricks," and **real time in between** for the mental "mortar" to dry. That's exactly why procrastination — a genuinely common problem for students in general — matters especially for math and science.

**We procrastinate on things that make us feel uncomfortable.** Brain imaging studies of self-described "mathphobes" show something striking: their brain's actual *pain* centers light up the moment they anticipate doing math — not while doing it, but while merely *thinking* about doing it. Once they actually start the math, the pain disappeared entirely. As procrastination researcher Rita Emmett puts it: **"The dread of doing a task uses up more time and energy than doing the task itself."**

Avoiding something painful feels reasonable in the moment — but the long-term cost of habitual avoidance is genuinely nasty. Put off studying math, and thinking about it only gets more painful over time. Delay SAT/ACT prep, and you choke on test day because you never built the comfortable neural foundation you needed — and a scholarship opportunity quietly disappears with it. Maybe you'd genuinely love a career in math or science, but give up and settle elsewhere, telling people "I just couldn't hack the math" — when the real story is that procrastination simply won.

**Procrastination is what's called a "keystone" bad habit** — one that quietly influences a large number of other areas of your life. Fix it, and a surprising number of other things tend to improve alongside it. And here's the encouraging flip side: it's easy to dislike something you're not good at — but the better you actually get at it, the more you'll genuinely enjoy it.

## How the Brain Actually Procrastinates

Picture a typical Saturday: you finally sit down at 11 a.m. with coffee, planning a full day tackling math homework, a history essay, and a confusing chemistry section. You open the math textbook — and there's a faint, almost imperceptible "ouch." Your brain's pain centers flicker at the thought of tangled graphs and confusing notation. The idea of spending hours on this makes even opening the book feel worse.

You shift to your laptop instead. Ah — much better. No pain there, just a small dose of pleasure as you check messages, look at a funny picture a friend sent... two hours later, the math homework still hasn't started.

**This is the classic procrastination loop:** thinking about something unpleasant activates your brain's pain centers, so your attention automatically narrows toward something more enjoyable instead — offering temporary, genuine relief. Procrastination genuinely works like a mild addiction: brief excitement and escape from boring reality, at a real long-term cost.

**It's remarkably easy to build convincing-sounding excuses around this.** "Organic chemistry needs spatial reasoning, which I'm just bad at, so of course I'm struggling" — conveniently ignoring that the actual issue is avoidance, not ability. "If I study too far ahead of a test, I'll forget it by exam day" — conveniently ignoring that you have several other exams competing for that same last-minute cram window. Only once finals week arrives, and the desperate cramming begins, does the *real* cause of a poor grade — sustained procrastination, not the subject itself — become obvious.

**Procrastination can even become a strange point of pride.** "I crammed for the quiz last night after the lab report and the marketing interview — of course I could've done better, but look how much I had going on" makes chronic avoidance sound impressive rather than costly. Some people even *falsely* claim they procrastinated on something they actually worked hard on, because "I crammed the night before" sounds cooler than "I studied steadily."

**Like any habit, procrastination is something you simply fall into** — a cue triggers the same comfortable, unthinking response, again and again. Over time, this zombie-like pattern of chasing small, temporary pleasure quietly erodes self-confidence, and further saps your motivation to actually learn how to work effectively. Procrastinators, on average, report higher stress, worse health, and lower grades — and the longer the habit runs unchecked, the more entrenched (and hopeless-feeling) it becomes.

> **Paula Meerschaert, freshman, creative writing:**
> "I used to be a procrastinator, but I changed. An AP class in high school — 4 to 6 hours of American history homework a night — really forced me into gear. What I learned: take it one task at a time. Once I feel like I've actually accomplished something, it's much easier to keep moving forward and stay on track."

**Occasionally, pulling an all-nighter still nets a decent grade** — and finishing can even feel like a mild "win," the same rush that keeps a gambler coming back to the table. That small reward reinforces the habit, tempting you to gamble on procrastination again next time. You might start believing procrastination is just a fixed trait — as innate as your height — reasoning, "if it were fixable, wouldn't I have fixed it by now?"

**The higher you go in math and science, the more procrastination genuinely costs you.** Habits that got you through earlier, easier material can turn around and bite you hard later. The good news: the actual strategies for beating procrastination are genuinely simple — they're just not intuitively obvious, which is exactly what the next couple of chapters cover.

**Back to the arsenic eaters:** they built up their tolerance with small doses at a time — tiny amounts that seem harmless individually, allowing them to eventually handle a large dose while looking outwardly healthy, even as the poison quietly damaged their organs and raised their cancer risk. Procrastinators follow a strikingly similar pattern — putting off just one small thing, repeatedly, gradually growing used to it, looking outwardly fine the whole time. The long-term effects, though, are genuinely not good.

> **Richard Nadel, Senior Instructor of Mathematics, Florida International University:**
> "When a student who failed tells me they studied ten straight hours the day before the exam, I say: 'that's exactly why you failed.' When they look at me confused, I explain: 'you should have been studying a little bit all along.'"

## Summing It Up

- We procrastinate on things that make us genuinely uncomfortable — but what feels good in the short term isn't necessarily good for us over time.
- Procrastination works a lot like taking small doses of poison — each individual dose feels harmless, but the cumulative, long-term damage is real.

## A Story: Ask for Advice Early

> **Norman Fortenberry**, a national leader in engineering education, on a mistake worth learning from:
>
> As a first-year college student already set on engineering, he signed up for "Calculus with Applications" instead of the regular calculus track most classmates took — a mistake. Many classmates in the *regular* track had already taken calculus in high school and were simply building on it, putting him at a real disadvantage. Worse, his smaller specialized section left him with very few potential study partners — and unlike high school, college (especially engineering, a field that leans heavily on teamwork) offers no reward, and even a real penalty, for going it alone. Professors often design homework assuming students are working together.
>
> He squeaked through with a B, but always felt his conceptual grasp of calculus stayed genuinely shaky — and ended up relearning pieces of it "just in time" throughout later courses that depended on it, at real cost to time he could have spent elsewhere. He still made it through to a bachelor's in mechanical engineering, and — with real mentorship from peers and a faculty advisor — on to a doctorate.
>
> **His actual takeaway:** genuinely ask peers and teachers for advice when choosing your classes. Their collective experience is worth leaning on.

## Key Takeaways, Tips, and Tricks

- **Procrastination is about avoiding discomfort, not laziness.** The anticipation of a task is usually what actually hurts — the pain fades the moment you actually start.
- **Don't waste willpower fighting procrastination head-on.** It's a limited resource — the upcoming chapters cover smarter, lower-effort tricks that work better than brute-force willpower.
- **Watch for your own procrastination "stories"** — convenient-sounding excuses ("I'm just bad at this," "studying early makes me forget it") are usually rationalizations for avoidance, not real reasons.
- **Beware the "productive-feeling" all-nighter.** An occasional decent grade from cramming reinforces the habit like a gambling win — it doesn't mean the habit is actually working for you long-term.
- **Small, repeated avoidance adds up like tiny doses of poison** — individually harmless-feeling, but genuinely damaging over time. Treat procrastination as a "keystone" habit worth fixing, since improving it tends to improve much more than just your grades.
- **Study a little, consistently, rather than cramming.** Learning needs both short focused sessions *and* real time between them for understanding to "set" — cramming skips the second half entirely.
- **Take it one task at a time.** A sense of finishing something — even something small — makes it easier to keep moving to the next task.
- **Ask peers and teachers for advice before committing to a class or path** — their collective experience can help you avoid a costly, hard-to-undo early mistake.
- **Practice recalling material in a new location** (not just where you usually study) — it strengthens your ability to think clearly about it anywhere, including in an exam room.
- **Remember: liking a subject often follows competence, not the other way around.** Getting better at something is frequently what makes you start enjoying it — not a prerequisite for starting.
