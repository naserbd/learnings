# Chapter 3: Learning Is Creating — Lessons from Thomas Edison's Frying Pan

Thomas Edison held more than a thousand patents — one of the most prolific inventors in history. Nothing seemed to derail his creativity: even while watching his own lab burn to the ground in an accidental fire, he was already sketching plans for an even bigger, better lab. How was he so relentlessly creative? A lot of it comes down to his unusual tricks for deliberately shifting between modes of thinking.

## Shifting from Focused to Diffuse Mode

For most people, the shift from focused to diffuse mode happens naturally — just distract yourself, and give it a little time. Go for a walk, take a nap, hit the gym, or do something that occupies a *different* part of your brain: listen to music, practice Spanish verbs, clean out a drawer. The key is doing something else until your conscious mind is genuinely free of the problem. Without other tricks involved, this usually takes a few hours — which sounds like a lot, until you realize you were going to spend that time on other tasks and breaks anyway.

Creativity researcher Howard Gruber pointed to "the three B's" as reliable triggers: **the bed, the bath, or the bus.** A 19th-century chemist, Alexander Williamson, once said a solitary walk was worth a week in the lab for moving his work forward. Walking has sparked creativity across fields — Jane Austen, Carl Sandburg, and Charles Dickens all found inspiration on long walks.

Once you step away from a problem, diffuse mode gets access to it, and starts working through it in its own loose, big-picture way. When you return, the solution often clicks into place with surprising ease — and even if it doesn't fully arrive, you'll often find you've made real progress. That sudden "aha!" feeling — after real, hard focused work beforehand — is one of the genuinely great feelings in math, science, art, or any creative pursuit. (Yes: math and science, even at the level you learn them in school, are genuinely creative acts.)

**Edison's own trick:** faced with a tough problem, instead of grinding at it, he'd reportedly take a nap — sitting in a lounge chair, holding a ball bearing over a metal plate on the floor. As he drifted off, his thinking loosened into diffuse mode. The moment he actually fell asleep, the ball bearing dropped, and the clatter woke him up — right as he could grab hold of whatever loose, diffuse-mode insight had just surfaced.

## Creativity Is About Using and Stretching What You Already Have

There's a real, deep connection between technical and artistic creativity. Salvador Dalí used the exact same trick as Edison — a nap, with something dropping from his hand to wake him — which he called "sleeping without sleeping." Tapping into diffuse mode helps you learn at a genuinely deeper, more creative level.

There's more creativity underneath math and science than most people assume. It's easy to think there's only one "correct" way to solve a problem — but there are usually several, if you're able to see them. (There are more than 300 known proofs of the Pythagorean theorem, for instance.) Technical problem-solving genuinely has something in common with writing poetry.

Creativity isn't just a fixed set of artistic or scientific talents you either have or don't — it's about actively using and extending whatever ability you already have. Plenty of people wrongly assume they're "just not creative." Everyone has the ability to form new neural connections, and pull something out of memory that was never explicitly stored there in the first place — what researchers Liane Gabora and Apara Ranjan call "the magic of creativity." Understanding how your own mind works helps you recognize the creative nature of thoughts you might otherwise dismiss.

### Try It: From Focused to Diffuse

Read this sentence and count the errors:

> *Thiss sentence contains threee errors.*

The first two typos (*Thiss*, *threee*) jump out with a plain focused-mode read. The third is trickier — it only becomes visible once you step back and look at the sentence from a different angle (the sentence claims three errors, but if you only spot two typos, is that claim itself the third error, or not? The paradox only resolves with a shift in perspective).

## Working Back and Forth to Actually Master Material

We learn a lot from our failures in math and science — every mistake you catch while solving a problem is genuine progress, and should feel like a small win. Edison himself reportedly said: *"I have not failed. I've just found 10,000 ways that won't work."*

Mistakes are inevitable — the way through them is starting early, and (unless you're genuinely enjoying yourself) keeping individual study sessions short. During your breaks, diffuse mode keeps quietly working in the background — you keep learning while you're relaxing, which is about as good a deal as it gets. Some people think they never enter diffuse mode at all, but that's not true — any time you relax and think about nothing in particular, your brain naturally slips into a default, diffuse-style state. Everybody does this.

**Sleep is probably the single most effective way to let diffuse mode work on a hard problem.** But don't mistake diffuse mode's relaxed nature for laziness. Think of it like a base camp on a mountain climb — an essential place to rest, check your gear, and reassess your route — but nobody confuses resting at base camp with the actual work of reaching the summit. Diffuse mode alone won't get you anywhere without genuine focused effort too. What actually gets results, over days and weeks, is **distributed practice** — the steady back-and-forth between focused attention and diffuse relaxation.

Focused mode takes real mental energy — studies show we have a genuinely limited daily supply of it, closer to willpower than to an unlimited resource. When your focus starts flagging, switching to a *different* focused task (math to French vocabulary, say) can help — but the longer you stay in focused mode overall, the more of that resource you burn through. It's genuinely like an extended set of mental weightlifting — which is exactly why short breaks involving movement, or casual conversation, feel so refreshing.

It's tempting to want your diffuse mode to just work *faster* — but compare it to physical exercise. Constantly lifting weights doesn't grow your muscles; the muscle actually grows during the *rest* between sessions. Consistency over time, not nonstop effort, is what builds real strength — mental or physical.

### Diffuse-Mode Activators — Use These as Rewards After Real Focused Work

**General activators:**
- Go to the gym
- Play a sport (soccer, basketball)
- Jog, walk, or swim
- Dance
- Go for a drive
- Draw or paint
- Take a bath or shower
- Listen to music (especially without lyrics)
- Play a familiar song on an instrument
- Meditate or pray
- Sleep (the ultimate diffuse-mode activity)

**Best used briefly, as a short reward** (these tend to pull you back toward focused mode if overdone):
- Video games
- Web browsing
- Talking with friends
- Helping someone with a simple task
- Reading something relaxing
- Texting
- A movie or play
- TV (dozing off mid-show doesn't count as a nap)

## Don't Compare Yourself to the "Fast" Students

Struggling students often look at classmates who seem to breeze through material, and feel pressure to keep pace — which leads them to skip giving themselves the extra time they actually need, and fall even further behind. This spiral is exactly why many students end up dropping math and science unnecessarily.

Take an honest, dispassionate look at your own strengths and weaknesses. If you genuinely need more time to learn this material, that's simply the reality — not a character flaw. In high school, try to structure your schedule to give the harder subjects real breathing room, and keep the total load manageable. In college, avoid stacking a full course load of heavy technical classes, especially while working part-time — a lighter load of math and science can be just as demanding as a full load of other subjects. Especially early in college, resist the urge to match your peers' pace just to keep up appearances.

Learning slowly can genuinely mean learning more *deeply* than classmates who move faster. One of the single most useful changes was learning to avoid taking too many math and science courses at once.

## Watch for Einstellung (Getting Stuck)

Locking onto the first idea that comes to mind can block you from ever finding a better one. Chess players experiencing Einstellung genuinely *believe* they're scanning the whole board for alternatives — but eye-tracking studies show their gaze (and their thinking) stays locked on the original, flawed idea. It's not just their eyes that can't move on — it's their whole mind.

Interestingly, blinking itself may serve as a tiny reset — a brief moment where attention deactivates and refreshes, giving your perspective a chance to shift. (Though closing your eyes can also work the opposite way, helping you concentrate more deeply by blocking out distraction — both effects seem to be genuinely real, depending on the situation.)

This helps explain Magnus Carlsen's odd move at the start of this chapter. By standing up and looking at other boards, he likely helped his own mind briefly leap out of focused mode — giving his diffuse intuition room to work on his game against Kasparov. His deep chess expertise probably made this quick mode-switching possible. As you build real expertise in any subject, you may find yourself developing a similar ability to toggle quickly between focused and diffuse thinking. (Carlsen probably also knew the move would rattle Kasparov — a reminder that deep focused concentration is a genuinely valuable resource you don't want casually disrupted, unless you're the one deliberately choosing to step back.)

**Figuring out a hard problem or learning something new almost always requires stepping away from it at least once.** Each break lets diffuse mode look at it fresh; returning afterward lets you consolidate whatever new patterns diffuse mode surfaced. Learning well means genuinely letting time pass between focused study sessions — like letting mortar dry between bricks. Cramming everything into a couple of frantic sessions doesn't give your long-term memory time to properly consolidate — you end up with a jumbled pile of loose bricks instead of a solid wall.

> **From Trevor Drozd, junior, computer science:**
> "As a pianist for over a decade, I'd sometimes hit a run I just couldn't get — I'd force my fingers through it slowly (and often wrong), then take a break. The next day, I'd play it perfectly, almost like magic. I tried the same thing with a tricky calculus problem that was starting to infuriate me — on the drive to a festival, the answer suddenly came to me, and I had to scribble it on a napkin before I forgot. Always keep napkins in your car. You never know."

**A rough rule of thumb:** rest periods between focused sessions should be long enough to genuinely clear your conscious mind of the problem — usually a few hours is enough for diffuse mode to make real progress, without the insight fading before it gets handed back to focused mode. When you're first learning something new, try not to let more than a day pass without revisiting it.

Diffuse mode doesn't just offer a fresh angle — it also seems to help weave new ideas into what you already know. This is part of why "sleeping on it" before a big decision is generally sound advice, and why taking real vacations matters.

Resolving the tension between focused and diffuse learning takes time — focused mode supplies the bricks, diffuse mode supplies the mortar holding them together. Patient, steady effort — a little at a time — is what actually works. If procrastination is a struggle for you, that's exactly why the upcoming chapters' tricks for tackling it matter so much.

### Try It: Observe Yourself

Next time you feel yourself getting frustrated at something (or someone), take a mental step back and just observe the reaction as it happens. Frustration can occasionally motivate you — but it can also shut down exactly the brain regions you need for learning. Rising frustration is generally a good signal that it's time to shift into diffuse mode.

## What to Do When You're Really Stuck

People with strong self-discipline sometimes struggle *most* with actually turning off focused mode — they've succeeded precisely because they can keep pushing when others give up. If that's you, here's a useful trick: make it a rule to listen when a study partner, friend, or family member notices you're getting dangerously frustrated and tells you to stop. It's often easier to take that advice from someone else than to give it to yourself.

When you're genuinely stuck, nothing beats getting a fresh perspective from classmates, peers, or an instructor — a different way of explaining the concept can be exactly what unlocks it. But it's best to wrestle with the problem yourself *first*, before asking — that initial struggle embeds the basic concepts deeply enough that you're actually receptive to the explanation when it comes. And don't wait until the week before exams to ask for help — go early, and go often. A teacher can often rephrase or reframe something in a way that finally makes it click.

> **From Cassandra Gordon, sophomore, computer science:**
> "In tenth grade, I took AP computer science and failed the exam. I refused to accept that, so I took the class *and* the test again the next year. Staying away from programming for nearly a year and then coming back to it made me realize how much I actually enjoyed it — I passed easily the second time. If I'd been too afraid of failing to try again, I wouldn't be the happy, passionate computer scientist I am today."

### Try It: The Paradoxes of Learning

Learning is genuinely full of paradoxes. The very focus you need to solve a problem can also be exactly what blocks you from a fresh approach. Success matters — but so, critically, does failure. Persistence matters — but misplaced persistence just breeds needless frustration. Keep an eye out for more of these paradoxes as you go.

## Working Memory vs. Long-Term Memory

Two memory systems matter most here: **working memory** and **long-term memory**.

**Working memory** is whatever you're actively, consciously processing right now. It was once thought to hold around seven items — current thinking puts it closer to just **four "chunks"** of information (we tend to unconsciously group related items together, which makes working memory *feel* bigger than it really is).

Think of working memory like a juggler: those four items only stay "in the air" because you're actively feeding them energy. Stop actively maintaining them, and they get pulled away by the brain's natural forgetting processes — you have to keep working at holding onto them, or they slip.

Working memory matters for math and science because it's essentially your personal mental scratchpad — the space where you juggle a few ideas at once while working something out. You keep things there through active rehearsal (repeating a phone number to yourself until you can write it down, say), and you might close your eyes specifically to keep other input from crowding out your limited working-memory slots.

**Long-term memory**, in contrast, is more like a giant storage warehouse — once something's in there, it generally stays, though a warehouse this vast (room for billions of items) makes things easy to lose track of and hard to retrieve. When something first lands in long-term memory, you typically need to revisit it a few times before you can reliably find it again later. (If you like the computer analogy: working memory is like RAM, long-term memory is like hard drive space.)

Long-term memory matters for math and science because it's where the fundamental concepts and techniques you actually rely on live. Moving something from working memory into long-term memory takes real time — a technique called **spaced repetition** helps: repeating what you're trying to learn (a new term, a new problem-solving technique), but spreading that repetition out across several days rather than cramming it all into one sitting.

Research backs this up directly — repeating something 20 times in one evening sticks far worse than repeating it the same 20 times spread across several days or weeks. It's the same brick-and-mortar idea again: skip the drying time, and the wall just doesn't hold.

### Try It: Let Your Mind Work in the Background

Next time you're stuck on a tough problem, work at it for a few minutes, then deliberately switch to a different problem when you hit a wall. Diffuse mode keeps quietly chewing on the harder one in the background. When you come back to it later, you'll often be pleasantly surprised at how much progress has quietly happened.

> **Amy Alkon, syndicated columnist, on napping:**
> "Many people insist they 'can't nap.' The one thing that stuck from a single yoga class years ago: slow your breathing, and don't fixate on needing to fall asleep — just think 'sleepytime' and focus on the breath. I also darken the room, or use a sleep mask, and set an alarm for 21 minutes — turning a short nap into a long sleep just leaves you groggy. Twenty-one minutes gives me a genuine cognitive reboot."

## Why Sleep Matters So Much for Learning

Simply being awake generates toxic byproducts in the brain. During sleep, your brain cells actually shrink slightly, widening the space between them — like opening a faucet, letting fluid flush those toxins out. This nightly cleanup is a genuine part of keeping the brain healthy; too little sleep, and the resulting buildup is thought to explain why thinking gets foggy. (Chronic sleep deprivation is linked to everything from Alzheimer's to depression — going without sleep for extended periods is genuinely dangerous.)

Sleep is also directly essential to memory and learning. Part of what happens overnight is memory "housekeeping" — trivial details get erased, while important patterns get strengthened. Your brain also actively rehearses tougher material during sleep, replaying and reinforcing the relevant neural patterns.

Sleep also measurably improves your ability to solve hard problems and find real meaning in what you're learning — as if fully powering down the conscious, prefrontal-cortex "you" frees up other brain regions to communicate more freely and assemble a solution while you're out. (You still need to plant that seed first, through real focused-mode effort while awake.) Reviewing material right before a nap or bedtime seems to increase your odds of actually *dreaming* about it — and deliberately intending to dream about it seems to boost those odds further still. Dreaming about what you're studying appears to genuinely strengthen understanding, consolidating memory into more digestible chunks.

**If you're tired, it's usually better to just go to sleep and wake up earlier** than to keep pushing through fatigue. An hour of reading with a well-rested brain consistently beats three hours with a tired one — a sleep-deprived brain simply can't make the connections a rested one makes automatically. Pulling an all-nighter before an exam can mean that even with perfect preparation, your mind just can't function well enough to show what you actually know.

> **Paul Schwalbe, senior, English, on applying this outside STEM:**
> "If I'm stuck on a problem, I lie in bed with an open notebook and pen, and just write out thoughts as I'm falling asleep — sometimes right after waking up too. Some of it makes no sense at all. But sometimes it hands me a completely new way of seeing the problem."

## Summing It Up

- Use focused mode first, to genuinely grapple with a new concept or problem.
- After real focused effort, let diffuse mode take over — relax, and do something different.
- Rising frustration is a good signal to deliberately shift into diffuse mode.
- Work in small, regular doses rather than marathon sessions — this gives both modes time to actually do their job, building solid understanding.
- If procrastination is an issue, try a 25-minute focused timer, free of texts, web browsing, or other distractions.
- Two memory systems matter: **working memory** (a juggler holding about four items) and **long-term memory** (a huge warehouse that needs occasional revisiting to stay accessible).
- **Spaced repetition** — spreading practice across several days — helps move things from working memory into long-term memory far more effectively than cramming.
- **Sleep is essential**, not optional: it enables normal thinking (hence sleep the night before a test matters), helps solve hard problems and find real meaning in material, and actively strengthens the important parts of what you're learning while pruning the rest.

## A Researcher's Perspective on Creativity

Psychiatry professor Robert Bilder directs UCLA's Tennenbaum Center for the Biology of Creativity, and leads the university's "Mind Well" initiative. A few of his research-backed ingredients for a personal creativity "recipe":

- **Just do it.** Creativity is genuinely a numbers game — the best predictor of how many creative works you'll produce over a lifetime is simply how many you actually produce. Exposing your work to others is often excruciating in the moment, but it consistently turns out for the best.
- **Deal with fear directly.** A motivational poster from a Facebook HQ talk: *"What would you do if you weren't afraid?"* Aim to do one fearless thing daily. Name what you're afraid of — and don't let it stop you.
- **Redos come with the territory.** Don't like how it turned out? Do it again.
- **Criticism sharpens your work.** Exposing your work to others — and to your own scrutiny — gives you a fresh, external perspective, and a clearer plan for the next version.
- **Being a little disagreeable helps.** Research shows a negative correlation between "agreeableness" and creativity — the most creative people tend to challenge existing answers rather than accept them. Real creative progress tends to come from stripping a problem back to its roots, questioning your own assumptions (and everyone else's), and repeating the process.

## Key Takeaways, Tips, and Tricks

- **Deliberately step away from a problem** after real focused effort — a walk, a bath, a bus ride, a nap. This isn't wasted time; it's when diffuse mode does its real work.
- **Try Edison's trick**, or your own version of it: hold something loosely as you doze off, so waking the moment you fall asleep captures a fresh, diffuse-mode insight.
- **Creativity isn't a fixed trait** — everyone can form new connections and stretch existing ability; there's usually more than one valid way to solve any problem.
- **Every mistake is genuine progress.** Treat catching an error as a small win, not a setback — "I've just found ways that don't work."
- **Keep study sessions short**, and give yourself real breaks — diffuse mode keeps working quietly in the background even while you rest.
- **Sleep is your most powerful diffuse-mode tool.** Review tricky material right before a nap or bedtime to boost your odds of dreaming about it — and consolidating it.
- **Use "reward" activities deliberately** after real focused work — sport, music, a walk, a shower — save video games, social media, and TV for short, occasional rewards, since they pull you back toward focused mode.
- **Don't compare your pace to classmates.** Learning slowly can mean learning more deeply — take a lighter course load if you genuinely need the time.
- **Watch for Einstellung** — locking onto your first idea and missing a better one. If you're stuck, deliberately shift modes rather than pushing harder on the same idea.
- **When frustration rises, that's your cue to stop and switch modes** — not a sign to push through.
- **Let a friend or study partner call your "stop" for you** if you're the type who struggles to self-interrupt.
- **Struggle with a problem yourself before asking for help** — the struggle is what makes you receptive to the explanation once you get it. Then ask early, and ask often — don't wait until the week before exams.
- **Use spaced repetition:** revisit new material within about a day, and spread practice across several sessions rather than cramming it into one.
- **When stuck, switch to a different task** rather than grinding — your brain keeps working on the original problem in the background.
- **Never sacrifice sleep to "get more done."** An hour of study with a rested brain beats three hours exhausted — and pulling an all-nighter before a test can undercut even solid preparation.
