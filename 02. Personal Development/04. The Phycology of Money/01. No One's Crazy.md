## Chapter 1: No One's Crazy

### Core Argument
Morgan Housel's main point is that people's money decisions look "crazy" 
from the outside, but they almost never actually are. Everyone forms their 
beliefs about money based on their own personal experiences — when and where 
they were born, what their family went through, what the economy was doing 
during their most formative years. Since no two people live through the 
exact same slice of history, no two people end up with the same "logic" 
about money.

He points out that your own lived experience is a tiny fraction of everything 
that's happened in the world, but it shapes the vast majority of how you 
personally think money works. So two equally smart, equally informed people 
can look at the same financial decision and reach completely opposite 
conclusions — not because one is foolish, but because their internal 
reference points are different.

He also argues that modern personal finance — saving, investing, retirement 
planning — is a surprisingly new invention. Things like 401(k)s, index 
funds, and the idea that everyone deserves a comfortable retirement are only 
a few decades old. So a lot of "bad" financial behavior isn't stupidity — 
it's simply a lack of accumulated experience, because as a species we're all 
still new to this game.

### Key Messages from the Stories
- **JFK and the Great Depression:** Kennedy grew up wealthy and had almost no 
  personal experience of the Depression, while most of his generation was 
  scarred by it for life — showing how two people from the same era can walk 
  away with completely different relationships to money and risk.
- **Investors and their generation's economy:** Research cited shows people's 
  lifelong appetite for risk (stocks vs. bonds) is shaped heavily by the 
  economic conditions they experienced early in adulthood — not by 
  intelligence or education.
- **The Foxconn factory comment:** A relative of a factory worker defended 
  "sweat shop" conditions as an improvement over the worker's previous life, 
  illustrating how the same situation can look completely different 
  depending on someone's starting point.
- **Lottery tickets:** Low-income households spend heavily on lottery 
  tickets despite struggling to cover emergencies — which looks irrational, 
  until you consider it may be the only affordable way for someone to buy a 
  tangible shot at the kind of life they otherwise feel locked out of.

The throughline in all of these: what looks irrational from the outside 
usually makes complete internal sense once you understand the experiences 
behind it.

### Simple Analogy
Imagine everyone in the world is wearing a different pair of glasses. Your 
glasses were custom-made from everything that's ever happened to you — your 
family, where you grew up, what you saw your parents struggle with or enjoy. 
Nobody else has your exact glasses, and you don't have anyone else's. So when 
you look at how someone else spends or saves money, it might look weird or 
wrong through *your* glasses — but through *their* glasses, shaped by a 
totally different life, it makes perfect sense. No one is crazy. They're just 
wearing different glasses than you.

### Memory Hook
**"No one's crazy — they're just wearing a different pair of glasses than 
you."**

Next time someone's money choice seems irrational, picture them literally in 
a different pair of glasses shaped by a life you haven't lived.

### Chapter Summary
- Everyone's beliefs about money are shaped by their own narrow slice of 
  lived experience — not by intelligence or access to information.
- Your personal experience is a tiny fraction of world history but a huge 
  driver of how you personally think money works.
- People born into different decades (inflation, stock market performance, 
  recessions) end up with fundamentally different "common sense" about money.
- Financial decisions are made emotionally, at kitchen tables — not purely 
  on spreadsheets.
- Modern personal finance concepts (401(k)s, index funds, retirement as a 
  "right") are only a few decades old — so a lot of financial mistakes come 
  from inexperience, not stupidity.
- Bottom line: what looks like a crazy money decision usually makes complete 
  sense once you understand the experiences behind it.
