# Chapter 10: Enhancing Your Memory

## Joshua Foer's Story

Joshua Foer was an ordinary guy — a recent college grad living with his parents, trying to make it as a journalist. His memory was genuinely unremarkable: he forgot his girlfriend's birthday, misplaced his car keys constantly, once left food burning in the oven, and — no matter how hard he tried — kept typing "its" instead of "it's."

But he was fascinated by people who seemed genuinely different: people who could memorize a shuffled deck of cards in 30 seconds, casually absorb dozens of phone numbers or names, or recite an unfamiliar poem back perfectly after just a few minutes with it. Foer assumed these "memory masters" must simply be wired differently.

**But every memory champion he interviewed insisted their untrained memory had once been perfectly average.** What set them apart, they claimed, was a set of ancient visualization techniques — and anyone, they told him repeatedly, could learn to use them. Even him.

That challenge is how Foer eventually found himself, improbably, sitting as a top finalist at the U.S. Memory Championships, staring down a deck of cards.

> **Forrest Newman, Professor of Astronomy and Physics, Sacramento City College:**
> "As educators, in our push to encourage chunking over rote memorization, we sometimes accidentally imply memorization doesn't matter. ('Why memorize an equation I can just look up?') But memorizing key facts genuinely matters — those facts are exactly the seeds the creative process of chunking grows from. The real lesson is that we need to keep actively mentally playing with what we've memorized, in order to actually turn it into real chunks."

## Your Surprisingly Powerful Visual-Spatial Memory

Humans have genuinely excellent visual and spatial memory systems — and techniques that tap into them don't rely on raw repetition to force information in. Instead, they use fun, vivid, creative approaches that make information easier to see, feel, or hear — and, as a bonus, these techniques free up working memory. Grouping information in a wacky but logically retrievable way genuinely strengthens long-term memory, and can meaningfully reduce test-day stress.

**Here's a concrete demonstration of how strong this system already is:** walk through a house you've never visited, and within minutes, you'll retain a real sense of the furniture layout, room arrangement, color scheme — even the contents of the bathroom cabinet. Weeks later, you'll still recall dramatically more of that house than you would from staring at a blank wall for the same amount of time. Your mind is genuinely built to retain this kind of spatial information about a place.

**Ancient and modern memory techniques both exploit this natural strength.** Our ancestors never needed to memorize phone numbers — but they genuinely needed to remember the route home from a three-day hunt, or exactly where the ripest blueberries grew on a rocky slope. That evolutionary pressure locked in a powerful "where things are and what they look like" memory system, which memory techniques deliberately hijack.

## The Power of a Vivid Mental Image

Start by creating one genuinely memorable image for a single key fact. For **Newton's second law, f = ma** (force = mass × acceleration), you might picture a *flying mule* — "f" for flying, "m" for mule, and "a" left up to your own imagination.

**Why images work so well:** they connect directly to the right brain's visuospatial centers, letting you encode an otherwise dry, hard-to-remember concept through a channel with genuinely enhanced memory power.

> **A calculus student, on the classic "knuckle" mnemonic for which months have 31 days:**
> "Oddly enough, with that one simple trick I doubt I'll ever forget which months have 31 days — which honestly amazes me. Ten seconds to learn something I'd avoided learning for twenty years, because I assumed it'd be too tedious to just sit and memorize through repetition."

**The more senses an image engages, the easier it is to recall.** Don't just picture the mule — smell it, feel the wind pressure it feels, hear the wind whistling past. The funnier and more vivid the image, the more effectively it sticks.

## The Memory Palace Technique

The **memory palace technique** uses a genuinely familiar place — your house, your commute, your favorite restaurant — as a mental "notepad" where you deposit vivid images representing whatever you want to remember. Any well-known space instantly becomes your memory palace.

**Example — a grocery list (milk, bread, eggs):** picture a giant milk bottle just inside your front door, a loaf of bread plopped on the couch, and a cracked egg dribbling off the coffee table. Walk yourself mentally through the familiar space, "placing" a shockingly memorable image of each item along the way.

**Example — the mineral hardness scale (1–10: talc, gypsum, calcite, fluorite, apatite, orthoclase, quartz, topaz, corundum, diamond):** first build a mnemonic sentence ("Terrible Giants Can Find Alligators Or Quaint Trolls Conveniently Digest"), then anchor each word to a spot in your memory palace — a terrible giant at your front door, holding a can; an alligator just inside; and so on. The same approach works just as well for finance, economics, chemistry, or any other subject with a list to remember.

**The first attempt will feel slow** — conjuring a solid mental image takes real effort at first. It gets noticeably faster with practice. One study found people using this technique could recall over 95% of a 40–50 item list, after just one or two mental "walks" through a familiar campus. Used this way, memorization becomes a genuine creative exercise — one that simultaneously builds the neural "hooks" for even more future creativity.

> **One caveat worth taking seriously:** because this method leans on your visuospatial system, don't use it while doing anything else spatially demanding — like driving. The distraction could genuinely be dangerous.

### Try It: Use the Memory Palace

> **Tracey Magrann, top anatomy professor, demonstrating the technique for the five layers of the epidermis** (deep to superficial: stratum basale, stratum spinosum, stratum granulosum, stratum lucidum, stratum corneum):
>
> "Visualize your basement — that's the stratum *basale* (the deepest layer). Climb your basement stairs — carefully, they're covered in cactus spines (stratum *spinosum*). That leads to the kitchen, where someone's spilled granulated sugar all over the floor (stratum *granulosum*). Head upstairs, and stop to apply suntan lotion before heading to the roof — the stratum *lucidum* is like a layer of sunscreen, since it protects against UV rays but only exists on the palms and soles, so that's exactly where you picture applying it. Finally, you're on the roof, enjoying a nice corn on the cob (stratum *corneum*)."

## Songs and Movement as Memory Tools

Songs work similarly to the memory palace, since they also lean heavily on the brain's right hemisphere. Songs exist for the quadratic formula, geometric volume formulas, and plenty more — search online, or make up your own. Many nursery rhymes pair a tune with physical action specifically to help lyrics stick (think of songs with built-in gestures) — meaningful movement adds yet another sensory "hook," since the physical sensation of movement itself becomes part of the memory.

**These techniques go far beyond equations, concepts, or grocery lists.** Even a nerve-wracking speech or presentation gets noticeably easier once you tie its key ideas to memorable images — Joshua Foer's own TED talk demonstrates exactly this, using a memory palace to structure and remember an entire speech. For visual shorthand specifically for math symbols (the "/" divide symbol as a children's slide, for instance), sites like SkillsToolbox.com offer ready-made examples.

**Memory aids — vivid images, catchy songs, imagined "palaces" — work because they help you actually focus and pay attention when your mind would rather wander off.** They're a reminder that *meaning* is what actually drives memory, even when that meaning starts out completely absurd. Fundamentally, memorization techniques are really an invitation to make what you're learning meaningful, memorable, and genuinely fun.

> **Malcolm Whitehouse, senior, computer engineering:**
> "In tenth-grade chemistry, we were introduced to Avogadro's number — 6.02214 × 10²³ — and none of us could remember it. A friend made up a song for it, borrowing the tune from a cereal commercial (which turned out to actually be a much older song, 'Oh, Them Golden Slippers'). Thirty years later, as an older student, I still remember Avogadro's number — entirely because of that song."

> **Tracey Magrann, Professor of Biological Sciences, Saddleback College, on engaging multiple senses:**
> "Pacing while memorizing, and having a small snack beforehand, genuinely helps — mental activity burns real energy. It also matters to engage multiple brain regions while learning: the visual cortex for what you see, the auditory cortex for what you hear, the sensory cortex for what you feel, the motor cortex for what you physically move. Using more regions at once builds a stronger, tighter memory web — one less likely to fail under exam stress. In anatomy lab, for instance, I have students physically pick up anatomy models, close their eyes, feel each structure, and say its name out loud. You can skip smell and taste — gotta draw the line somewhere!"

## Summing It Up

- The **memory palace technique** — placing vivid, memorable cues throughout a scene you already know well — lets you tap directly into the real strength of your visual memory system.
- Using memory in this more disciplined-yet-creative way trains focused attention, even while you're simultaneously building wild, diffuse-mode connections that strengthen memory further.
- Memorizing material you genuinely *understand* internalizes it deeply — and directly reinforces the same mental library you need to become a real master of the subject.

## A Story: Spatial Skills Are Learnable, Not Fixed

> **Sheryl Sorby**, an award-winning engineering professor whose research includes 3D computer graphics for visualizing complex behavior, on a common misconception:
>
> "Many people wrongly believe spatial intelligence is fixed — you either have it, or you don't. I'm here to say emphatically that this is false. I'm living proof that spatial ability can genuinely be learned. I nearly left engineering entirely because of poorly developed spatial skills — but I worked at it, built the skill, and finished my degree. Because I struggled with this myself as a student, I've dedicated much of my career to helping other students build theirs — and virtually everyone I've worked with has improved through practice.
>
> Human intelligence takes many forms — musical, verbal, mathematical, and more. Spatial thinking is an important one: the ability to imagine an object from a different angle, after rotation, or sliced open — or to mentally plot a route between two places using only a map.
>
> Strong spatial thinking is genuinely important for careers like engineering, architecture, and computer science — think of an air traffic controller mentally tracking several flight paths at once to keep them from crossing, or a mechanic visualizing how to fit parts back into an engine. Recent research even links spatial intelligence to creativity and innovation — the stronger your spatial thinking, the more creative and innovative you tend to be.
>
> We've found that students with weaker spatial skills usually just had fewer relevant childhood experiences — kids who spent real time taking things apart and rebuilding them tend to develop strong spatial ability, as do kids who played certain sports (think basketball, and the constant mental calculation of a ball's arc into the hoop from any position on the court).
>
> But if you missed those experiences growing up, it's genuinely not too late — spatial skills can still be developed well into adulthood, with practice and patience. **Concrete things to try:** accurately sketch an object, then sketch it again from a different angle. Play 3D computer games. Assemble 3D puzzles (starting with 2D ones, if needed). Put away the GPS occasionally and navigate with a paper map instead. Above everything else — don't give up. Just keep working at it."

## Key Takeaways, Tips, and Tricks

- **You already have a genuinely powerful visual-spatial memory** — techniques that use it beat raw repetition, and free up working memory in the process.
- **Turn dry facts into vivid mental images.** The sillier, more exaggerated, and more multi-sensory (sight, sound, smell, touch) the image, the more effectively it sticks.
- **Use the memory palace technique for lists.** Pick a genuinely familiar place, and "deposit" a vivid image for each item as you mentally walk through it.
- **Never use the memory palace technique while doing something else spatially demanding**, like driving — it's genuinely distracting.
- **Turn facts into songs or rhymes**, ideally borrowing an already-familiar tune — a song learned once can stick for decades.
- **Add physical movement where you can.** Pacing, gesturing, or physically handling an object while learning engages more of the brain and builds a stronger memory.
- **Snack before a serious memorization session** — mental effort genuinely burns real energy.
- **Engage multiple senses deliberately when studying** — see it, say it out loud, physically touch or handle it if possible. More sensory "hooks" mean a sturdier memory.
- **Practice makes memory techniques faster.** The first attempt at a memory palace will feel slow and effortful — it gets dramatically quicker with repeated use.
- **Apply these techniques beyond math and science** — speeches, presentations, and any information-heavy task can benefit from the same vivid-image approach.
- **Spatial ability is a learnable skill, not a fixed trait.** Sketching from different angles, 3D puzzles, 3D games, and map-based navigation can all genuinely build it — at any age, with enough patience.
