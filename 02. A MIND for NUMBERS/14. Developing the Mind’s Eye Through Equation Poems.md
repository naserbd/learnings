# Chapter 14: Developing the Mind's Eye Through Equation Poems

## Two Very Different Physics Classes

Poet Sylvia Plath once described her physics class as an experience of pure dread:

> "The day I went into physics class it was death... A short dark man with a high, lisping voice, named Mr. Manzi, stood in front of the class in a tight blue suit holding a little wooden ball. He put the ball on a steep grooved slide and let it run down to the bottom. Then he started talking about let a equal acceleration and let t equal time and suddenly he was scribbling letters and numbers and equals signs all over the blackboard and my mind went dead."

Her teacher had, in effect, written a 400-page book of pure diagrams and formulas — no drawings, no photos. It's a bit like trying to appreciate Plath's own poetry secondhand, through description, rather than actually reading it yourself. Plath got the only A in the class — and came away with a lasting dread of physics regardless.

> "What, after all, is mathematics but the poetry of the mind, and what is poetry but the mathematics of the heart?"
> — David Eugene Smith, American mathematician and educator

**Nobel laureate Richard Feynman's introductory physics classes were something entirely different** — Feynman was an exuberant, bongo-playing physicist who talked more like a down-to-earth cab driver than an ivory-tower intellectual.

At around eleven years old, Feynman remarked to a friend that thinking is basically just talking to yourself internally. "Oh yeah?" his friend replied. "Do you know the crazy shape of the crankshaft in a car?" "Yeah, what of it?" "Good — now tell me: how did you describe it when you were talking to yourself?" That question was Feynman's realization that thoughts can be genuinely *visual*, not just verbal.

Feynman later wrote about his own struggle, as a student, to visualize concepts like electromagnetic waves — the invisible energy streams behind everything from sunlight to cell signals — and how hard he found it to put what he "saw" in his mind's eye into words. **If even one of history's greatest physicists struggled to visualize genuinely difficult concepts, that's a real comfort for the rest of us.**

## Finding the Poetry Hidden in an Equation

**Inspiration for this comes from an unexpected place: poetry itself.** Consider a few lines from Jonathan Coulton's song "Mandelbrot Set," about mathematician Benoit Mandelbrot:

> *Mandelbrot's in heaven / He gave us order out of chaos, he gave us hope where there was none / His geometry succeeds where others fail / So if you ever lose your way, a butterfly will flap its wings / From a million miles away, a little miracle will come to take you home*

**These emotionally resonant lines genuinely capture the essence of Mandelbrot's mathematics** — the gentle flap of a butterfly's wings, spreading effects a million miles away, becomes a vivid image for real ideas embedded in his work: that visually messy, "rough" things (clouds, coastlines) actually hide real underlying order, that visual complexity can emerge from surprisingly simple rules, and that tiny shifts in one part of a system can ripple out to affect everything else. The more you actually understand Mandelbrot's mathematics, the more layers of meaning you find in Coulton's lyrics.

**Equations hide meaning the same way poetry does.** To a genuine novice, unprepared to see the "life" underneath the symbols, an equation's lines simply look dead — flat notation to memorize. Learning to supply that hidden meaning is exactly what lets an equation slip, slide, and finally leap to life.

**Physicist Jeffrey Prentis compared how a brand-new physics student and a seasoned physicist actually "see" the same equation.** To the novice, it's just one more item added to a huge pile of unrelated things to memorize. To the advanced student or physicist, the equation's meaning is genuinely visible in the mind's eye — how it fits the bigger picture, even a felt *sense* of how its individual parts behave.

> "A mathematician who is not at the same time something of a poet will never be a full mathematician."
> — Karl Weierstrass, German mathematician

**A worked example — building an "equation poem" for physics.** Seeing the letter *a* (acceleration), you might feel the actual physical press of a gas pedal, the seat pushing back against you as a car accelerates. You don't need to consciously conjure this feeling *every* time you see "a" — but the sensation should sit as a chunk in the back of your mind, ready to surface into working memory whenever you're actually analyzing what a specific "a" means in an equation.

Seeing *m* (mass), picture the stubborn inertia of a 50-pound boulder — real effort required just to get it moving. Seeing *f* (force), you can now feel what's underneath it: force depends on both mass *and* acceleration (m·a, as in f = ma) — a heaving push (acceleration) working against the lazy resistance of the boulder (mass).

**One more layer:** in physics, "work" means energy — you do work whenever you push (force) something across a distance, captured compactly as w = f×d. Once you see *w* this way, you can feel what's behind it too. The full "equation poem," unfolding line by line:

```
w
w = f · d
w = (m·a) · d
```

**Symbols and equations, in other words, carry hidden text beneath the surface** — meaning that only becomes visible with real familiarity. Scientists often treat equations essentially as poetry — dense, symbolic shorthand for something they're genuinely trying to see and understand. Just as a genuinely observant reader can find multiple layers of meaning in a poem, a maturing student gradually learns to see an equation's hidden meaning through the mind's eye, and even to sense different valid interpretations of it. Graphs, tables, and other visuals carry this same kind of hidden depth — often even more vividly represented in the mind's eye than on the printed page.

## Simplify and Personalize What You're Studying

**One of the most powerful things you can do while learning math or science: bring the abstract ideas to genuine life in your mind.** Santiago Ramón y Cajal (from the previous chapter) treated the microscopic cells he studied as though they were living creatures with hopes and inner lives of their own. His colleague Sir Charles Sherrington (who coined the word "synapse") said he'd never met another scientist with Cajal's intense ability to breathe life into his work this way — and genuinely wondered if that was a key ingredient in Cajal's success.

**Einstein famously imagined himself as a photon**, moving at the speed of light, then imagined how a *second* photon might perceive him — what would it see and feel? Notably, his theory of relativity emerged not from raw mathematical skill (he frequently needed collaborators for the actual math) but from this ability to genuinely *pretend*, to inhabit the physical scenario imaginatively.

**Barbara McClintock**, who won the Nobel Prize for discovering genetic transposition ("jumping genes"), wrote about vividly imagining the corn plants she studied: *"I even was able to see the internal parts of the chromosomes — actually everything was there. It surprised me because I actually felt as if I were right down there and these were my friends."*

**Staging a mental "play," treating what you're studying as living characters with feelings and motives, might sound silly — but it genuinely works.** It brings dry material to life, and helps you see and understand things that pure numbers and formulas leave invisible.

**Simplification matters just as much as personalization.** Feynman was famous for demanding that scientists and mathematicians explain their ideas *simply* enough for him to genuinely grasp them — and surprisingly, a simple explanation is possible for almost any concept, however complex. Deliberately breaking complicated material down to its essential core actually *deepens* your understanding of it, not just your ability to explain it. Learning expert Scott Young formalized this into what he calls the **Feynman technique**: find a simple metaphor or analogy that captures an idea's genuine essence.

**Darwin used a similar trick.** Trying to explain a concept, he'd imagine someone had just walked into his study, put his pen down, and try to explain the idea in the simplest possible terms — which then helped him figure out how to actually write it up clearly. (Reddit's "Explain Like I'm 5" community runs on exactly this same principle.)

**You might assume you need to fully understand something before you can explain it — but the reverse is often truer.** Genuine understanding frequently *emerges* from the act of trying to explain something, to others or even just to yourself, rather than preceding it. This is exactly why so many teachers report they only truly understood a topic the first time they had to teach it.

> **Kathleen Nolta, Ph.D., Senior Lecturer in Chemistry, University of Michigan (Golden Apple Award recipient):**
> "Learning organic chemistry isn't really any harder than getting to know a set of new characters. Each element genuinely has its own personality. The better you understand those personalities, the better you can read a given situation, and predict how a reaction will actually play out."

### Try It: Stage a Mental Play

Imagine yourself genuinely inside whatever you're studying — seeing the world from the perspective of a cell, an electron, even an abstract mathematical concept. Stage a small mental play with these new "characters," imagining how they might feel and react to each other.

## Transfer: Applying What You've Learned in a New Context

**Transfer** is the ability to take something learned in one context and genuinely apply it somewhere else. Learn one foreign language, and a second one often comes more easily — because learning the first one built genuinely transferable, general language-learning skills (not just vocabulary), which carry over directly to the next language.

**Learning math only through one narrow discipline** (accounting, engineering, economics) is a bit like deciding you're not really going to learn a *language*, just memorize a handful of extra English vocabulary words instead. Many mathematicians feel discipline-specific math instruction genuinely makes it harder to later use math flexibly and creatively elsewhere.

**This is exactly why mathematicians tend to teach math abstractly** — the chunked, application-free essence, rather than tied to one specific use case. Learned this way, the underlying skill transfers far more readily across contexts — a physics student, for instance, might use purely abstract math knowledge to quickly grasp how the same underlying structure applies to a biology, finance, or even psychology problem. It's the difference between memorizing one specific foreign phrase ("I run" in Albanian) versus understanding the *general* grammatical category — "verb," and how verbs conjugate — which applies across every language you'll ever learn.

**That said, a concrete, applied example is often genuinely easier to grasp in the moment** — even though it can make the underlying idea harder to transfer to a new context later. There's a real, ongoing tension here between abstract and concrete approaches to teaching math: mathematicians tend to defend the abstract, high-ground approach, while engineering, business, and other applied fields naturally lean toward concrete, discipline-specific math to keep students engaged and head off the "when will I ever use this?" complaint (concrete framing also avoids the common trap of "real-world" textbook word problems that are really just thinly disguised abstract exercises). **Both approaches carry genuine trade-offs — neither is simply "correct."**

**Transfer genuinely makes learning easier as you advance through a discipline.** As Professor Jason Dechant of the University of Pittsburgh puts it: "I always tell my students they'll actually study *less* as they progress through their nursing program, and they never believe me. They're genuinely doing more each semester — they just get better and better at pulling it all together." That's transfer in action.

**One of procrastination's most damaging side effects is exactly this: it breaks transfer.** Constantly interrupting focus to check a phone, email, or notification doesn't just shallow out learning in the moment — it also makes it harder to transfer whatever little you *did* learn to other topics later. It can feel like you're still learning between phone checks, but in reality, your brain isn't staying focused long enough to build the solid neural chunks that transfer actually depends on.

> **Patrick Scoggin, senior, history, on transfer in an unexpected place:**
> "I took fishing techniques I'd learned on the Great Lakes and tried them down in the Florida Keys this past year — completely different fish, different bait, a technique nobody down there had ever used. It worked great. People thought I was crazy, and it was genuinely funny proving them wrong by actually catching fish with it."

## Summing It Up

- Equations are simply a way of abstracting and simplifying a concept — which means they genuinely carry hidden depth, much like poetry does.
- Your **"mind's eye"** matters because it lets you stage mental "plays," and genuinely personalize whatever you're studying.
- **Transfer** is the ability to take what's learned in one context and apply it somewhere else entirely.
- Grasping a mathematical concept's chunked *essence* — not just its one specific application — makes it far easier to transfer and reapply that idea in new, different contexts.
- Multitasking while learning genuinely shallows your understanding — and that shallower learning is harder to transfer elsewhere.

## Key Takeaways, Tips, and Tricks

- **Look for the "hidden text" underneath an equation**, not just its symbols — the meaning becomes visible with real familiarity, the same way a poem's depth reveals itself on repeated, informed reading.
- **Build a physical, felt sense for individual symbols** (a for the press of acceleration, m for a boulder's stubborn inertia) — this turns abstract notation into something genuinely intuitive, ready to surface from memory when needed.
- **Try writing your own "equation poem"** — unfolding a formula line by line, building up the physical feeling behind each added piece.
- **Personalize what you're studying — genuinely.** Treat cells, particles, or abstract concepts as characters with feelings and motives, the way Cajal, Einstein, and McClintock all did. It sounds silly; it genuinely works.
- **Use the Feynman technique**: force yourself to explain a concept in the simplest possible terms, as if to someone with no background — the act of simplifying itself deepens your own understanding.
- **Imagine explaining a concept to an ordinary listener** (Darwin's trick) — it clarifies your own thinking before you even try to write or say it for real.
- **Don't wait until you "fully understand" something to try explaining it.** Understanding often *emerges* from the attempt to explain — not the other way around.
- **Seek out transferable, abstract understanding, not just one narrow application.** Grasping the general "shape" of an idea (like understanding what a verb is, not just one verb) makes it far easier to reuse in a completely different field later.
- **Balance concrete and abstract learning deliberately.** A concrete example is easier to grasp right now; an abstract understanding transfers further later. Neither alone is enough.
- **Avoid multitasking while learning.** Constant interruption doesn't just weaken learning in the moment — it directly damages your ability to later transfer what you learned to a new context.
