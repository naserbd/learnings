# Chapter 17: Test Taking

**Worth repeating in bold: testing is itself an extraordinarily powerful learning experience.** That means the effort you put into test-taking — including the mini-tests of recall and problem-solving woven into your prep — carries genuinely fundamental importance. Compare an hour spent studying against an hour spent actually testing yourself on the same material, and the testing hour wins, hands down, for what you actually retain and learn. Testing genuinely has a way of concentrating the mind.

Everything covered so far in this book has really been building toward making the actual test-taking process feel straightforward and natural — simply an extension of how you've already been learning the material. So let's get directly into one of this chapter's (and the whole book's) central tools: a checklist to gauge whether your test prep is genuinely on target.

## The Test Preparation Checklist

Professor Richard Felder is a genuine legend among engineering educators — arguably one of the most influential figures of this century in helping students worldwide succeed in math and science. One of his simplest and most effective tools is a memo he wrote for students disappointed with a test grade:

> "Many of you have told your instructor that you understood the course material much better than your last test grade showed, and some of you asked what you should do to keep the same thing from happening on the next test.
>
> Let me ask you some questions about how you prepared for the test. Answer them as honestly as you can. If you answer 'No' to many of them, your disappointing test grade should not be too surprising. If there are still a lot of 'Nos' after the next test, your disappointing grade on that test should be even less surprising. If your answer to most of these questions is 'Yes' and you still got a poor grade, something else must be going on — it might be worth meeting with your instructor or a counselor to figure out what.
>
> Several of these questions assume you're working with classmates on homework — either comparing individually-obtained solutions, or actually working through problems together. Either is fine. If you've been working entirely alone and your test grades are unsatisfactory, I'd strongly encourage finding one or two homework and study partners before the next test. (One caution: if what you're actually doing is mostly *watching* others work out the solution, you're probably doing yourself more harm than good.)"

**Test Preparation Checklist** — answer "Yes" only if you *usually* did the thing described (not just occasionally):

**Homework:**
1. Did you make a genuine effort to understand the text (not just hunt for a matching worked example)?
2. Did you work with classmates on homework problems, or at least check your solutions against theirs?
3. Did you attempt to outline every homework solution *before* meeting up with classmates?
4. Did you actively participate in homework group discussions — contributing ideas, asking questions?
5. Did you consult your instructor or a TA whenever you got genuinely stuck on something?
6. Did you genuinely understand *all* of your homework solutions before turning them in?
7. Did you ask in class for clarification on any homework solution that still wasn't clear to you?

**Test Preparation:**
8. If you had a study guide, did you carefully work through it before the test and confirm you could actually do everything on it?
9. Did you practice outlining lots of problem solutions *quickly*, without getting bogged down in the actual algebra?
10. Did you review the study guide and problems with classmates, quizzing each other?
11. If there was a review session before the test, did you attend and ask about anything you were unsure of?
12. Did you get a genuinely reasonable night's sleep before the test? *(If the answer here is no, your answers to 1–11 may not matter much.)*

**The more "Yes" answers you can honestly give, the stronger your preparation.** Two or more "No" answers is a real signal to rethink your approach before the next test.

## The Hard-Start–Jump-to-Easy Technique

The classic advice for math and science tests is to tackle the easiest problems first — the idea being that finishing the simple ones builds confidence for the harder ones. **This works for some people, mostly because something works for everyone eventually — but for most people, it's genuinely counterproductive.** Hard problems typically need more time, meaning you'd actually want to start them *first*. Difficult problems also genuinely call for the diffuse mode's creative power — but accessing diffuse mode requires *not* actively focusing on the exact thing you're desperate to solve.

**So — easy first, or hard first? The real answer: start hard, but jump to easy quickly.**

When the test is handed out, glance through it first to get a sense of the whole thing (do this regardless), and identify what looks like the hardest problem. **Start there — but deliberately pull yourself away within the first minute or two if you get stuck, or sense you're not on the right track.**

**Why this genuinely helps:** "starting hard" loads that difficult problem into your mind, then immediately switches your conscious attention away from it — both steps that let diffuse mode actually start working on it in the background. If your initial crack at the hard problem left you unsettled, pivot to an easy one and finish as much as you genuinely can. Then move to another tough-looking problem, make what progress you can, and switch away again the moment you feel stuck.

> **Bob Bradshaw, Professor of Math, Ohlone College:**
> "I talk with my students about good worry versus bad worry. Good worry provides real motivation and focus; bad worry just burns energy for nothing."

**When you eventually return to a difficult problem, the next step often feels noticeably more obvious than before.** You may not reach the full solution immediately, but you'll typically get further before needing to switch away again.

**Think of it like an efficient chef:** while a steak's frying, you slice the tomato garnish, season the soup, stir the onions — juggling several dishes at once rather than finishing one entirely before starting the next. The hard-start–jump-to-easy technique lets different parts of your brain work on different problems roughly simultaneously.

**This approach also guarantees at least some work gets done on every single problem**, and genuinely helps you dodge Einstellung — getting mentally stuck on a flawed approach — by giving you multiple chances to revisit a problem from a different angle at different times. Both benefits matter especially when partial credit is on the table.

**The real challenge: genuine self-discipline to actually pull yourself off a problem after a minute or two of being stuck.** Easy for some students, genuinely hard for others — but by now you already understand how misplaced persistence can create unnecessary trouble in math and science. This is exactly why a test answer sometimes "pops" into mind right as you're walking out the door — the moment you gave up, attention shifted, and diffuse mode finally got the traction it needed. Too late to use it, unfortunately — which is exactly why practicing this deliberately, *during* the test, matters.

**Some people worry that starting and stopping a problem repeatedly will cause confusion during an exam.** In practice, this generally isn't an issue — the same way a chef learns to juggle multiple dishes at once. If you're still unsure, try the technique out first on regular homework before a real test.

**A few situations where hard-start–jump-to-easy might genuinely *not* apply:** if an instructor deliberately assigns very few points to a genuinely hard problem, it may make more sense to focus effort elsewhere. Some computerized licensing exams don't allow backtracking at all — in that case, take a couple of deep belly breaths and simply do your best on a tough question in the moment. And if you genuinely didn't prepare well for the test, none of this really applies — just grab whatever easy points you can.

> **Tracey Magrann, Professor of Biological Sciences, Saddleback College, on pre-test panic:**
> "I tell students: face your fears directly. Often the underlying fear is 'what if I don't get the grade I need for my chosen career?' The simple fix: have a genuine Plan B for an alternative career. Once that backup plan actually exists, the fear noticeably subsides.
>
> Study hard right up until test day — then genuinely let it go. Tell yourself: 'let's just see how many I can get right — I've got my other option if I need it.' That release of pressure genuinely helps you perform better, and often gets you closer to your first-choice outcome anyway."

## Why Test Anxiety Happens, and What to Do About It

**Under stress, your body releases chemicals like cortisol** — sweaty palms, a racing heart, a knot in your stomach. **What genuinely matters isn't the physical symptoms themselves, but the story you tell yourself about them.** Reframing "this test has made me afraid" into "this test has got me excited to do my best" produces a genuine, measurable improvement in performance.

**Another genuinely useful tool for panic: shift attention briefly to your own breathing.** Relax your stomach, place a hand on it, and slowly draw a deep breath — your hand should visibly move outward as your chest expands like a barrel. This deep breathing sends oxygen to critical brain regions, signaling that everything's genuinely okay, and helps calm you down.

**Don't wait until test day to try this for the first time.** Practicing this breathing for even a minute or two here and there, in the weeks beforehand, means it'll come naturally when you actually need it during the test (practice, as always, makes it permanent). It's especially useful in the final anxious moments right before the test is actually handed out. (Plenty of apps exist to help build this habit, if that's genuinely useful to you.)

**Mindfulness is another genuinely helpful technique.** It teaches you to distinguish between a naturally arising thought ("I have a big test next week") and the anxious emotional *projection* that can tag along after it ("if I flunk this, I'll wash out of the program, and I don't even know what I'd do then"). These tagalong thoughts appear to be diffuse-mode projections — and even a few weeks of practice reframing them as *just* mental tagalongs (rather than fighting or suppressing them outright) genuinely calms and quiets the mind. Students who practiced mindfulness for a few weeks genuinely performed better on tests, with fewer distracting intrusive thoughts.

**This is exactly why saving the hardest questions for the very end of a test is genuinely risky.** Right when time pressure is spiking your stress, you're also suddenly facing the toughest problems — and as stress climbs, the instinct to concentrate even *harder* actually works against you, since intense focus is precisely what blocks diffuse mode from contributing. **The result: "paralysis by analysis."** The hard-start–jump-to-easy technique is a genuine defense against exactly this trap.

> **Susan Sajna Hebert, Professor of Psychology, Lakehead University, on multiple-choice and practice tests:**
> "On multiple-choice tests, students often barge into the answer options before fully understanding what the question is genuinely asking. I advise covering the answers, and trying to recall the answer from memory first, before even looking at the options.
>
> When students complain that a practice test felt way easier than the real one, I ask: what actually differed between the two situations? Were you relaxed at home with music playing? Studying alongside a friend? No time limit? Answer key and notes within reach? None of that matches a crowded classroom with a ticking clock. I genuinely encourage students with test anxiety to bring a practice test into a different, large class (one big enough to sit unnoticed at the back) and take it there instead — closer to real conditions."

## Final Thoughts on Testing

**The day before a test, do a brief review to refresh what you already know — don't push hard.** You'll need both your focused *and* diffuse "muscles" fully rested the next day (you wouldn't run a ten-mile training run the day before a marathon). Don't feel guilty if serious studying just doesn't happen the day before a big exam — with proper earlier preparation, this is a genuinely natural reaction: your subconscious pulling back to conserve real mental energy for the next day.

**During the test itself, remember your mind can genuinely trick you into believing flawed work is correct.** Whenever possible, blink, shift attention briefly, and then re-examine your answers with a genuine big-picture question: "does this actually make sense?" There's often more than one route to a solution — checking from a genuinely different angle is a real opportunity to catch a mistake the original approach missed. If a fresh angle isn't available and you're stuck re-walking the same logic, remember that even advanced math, science, and engineering students routinely trip on simple things — a missed minus sign, a misadded number, a "dropped" variable. Just do your genuine best to catch them. In science classes specifically, checking that units match on both sides of an equation is a real, fast clue to whether a solution is actually correct.

**The order you work through a test matters too.** Most students naturally work front to back — when *checking* your work, though, working *backward*, from the end toward the front, sometimes gives your brain a genuinely fresh perspective, making errors easier to actually spot.

**Nothing's ever fully certain — occasionally you'll study hard and still hit a bad day.** But genuinely solid preparation — real practice, a strong mental library of techniques, and a wise approach to the test itself — tilts the odds increasingly in your favor over time.

## Summing It Up

- **Skipping sleep the night before a test can undo virtually every other bit of preparation you've done.**
- Test-taking is genuinely serious business — the same way fighter pilots and doctors rely on checklists, running through your own test-prep checklist can meaningfully improve your odds of success.
- Counterintuitive strategies like **hard-start–jump-to-easy** give your brain real room to reflect on harder problems even while you're actively working on something simpler.
- The body genuinely releases stress chemicals under pressure — but *how you interpret* that physical reaction makes the real difference. Reframe "I'm afraid" into "I'm excited to do my best," and performance genuinely improves.
- **When panic hits mid-test, shift attention to your breathing** — relax your stomach, place a hand on it, and take a slow, deep breath that visibly expands your chest.
- Your mind can genuinely convince you flawed work is correct — whenever possible, blink, shift attention, and re-check your answer with a genuine big-picture question: "does this actually make sense?"

## Expert Advice: Psychologist Sian Beilock on Preventing "Choke"

> **Sian Beilock**, psychology professor at the University of Chicago and author of *Choke: What the Secrets of the Brain Reveal About Getting It Right When You Have To*, is one of the world's leading experts on reducing panic under high-stakes pressure:
>
> "High-stakes learning and performance situations genuinely put you under real stress. But a growing body of research shows fairly simple psychological interventions can lower test anxiety and boost classroom learning — and notably, these interventions don't teach academic content at all; they target your attitude.
>
> Our research team found that writing about your thoughts and feelings regarding an upcoming test, right before taking it, genuinely lessens the negative impact of pressure on performance. We believe writing helps release negative thoughts from active memory, making them less likely to intrude and distract you in the moment.
>
> The minor stress of frequent self-testing while learning also genuinely prepares you for the more intense stress of a real exam. As covered throughout this book, testing yourself while learning is a genuinely powerful way to commit material to memory, making it easier to retrieve under real pressure.
>
> Negative self-talk — critical thoughts arising from your own mind — can genuinely hurt performance. Keep what you tell yourself while preparing consistently upbeat; cut off a negative thought mid-stream if you need to, even if doom feels imminent. If you flub one problem, or several, keep your spirits up and shift focus cleanly to the next one.
>
> One real reason students choke: diving frantically into a solution before genuinely thinking through what they're actually facing. Learning to pause for a few seconds before starting a problem, or the moment you hit a roadblock, helps you actually see the best path forward — and helps you avoid the genuinely awful feeling of realizing, too late, that you've spent real time chasing a dead end.
>
> You can genuinely learn to keep stress within healthy bounds. Interestingly, eliminating stress entirely isn't even the goal — a little stress genuinely helps you perform at your best exactly when it matters most.
>
> Good luck!"

## Key Takeaways, Tips, and Tricks

- **Testing yourself is itself one of the most powerful learning tools available** — an hour spent testing beats an hour spent purely rereading or studying passively.
- **Run through Felder's 12-item checklist honestly before your next test.** Two or more "No" answers is a genuine signal to change your prep approach.
- **Sleep the night before a test is non-negotiable** — poor sleep can undermine every other bit of preparation you've done.
- **Use hard-start–jump-to-easy:** tackle the hardest-looking problem first, but pull away within a minute or two if stuck; work an easy problem to completion, then cycle back to another hard one, switching away again the moment you stall.
- **Give yourself an actual time limit before abandoning a stuck problem** — a minute or two, not longer — so diffuse mode gets a genuine chance to work on it in the background while you make progress elsewhere.
- **Reframe pre-test fear as excitement**, not dread — the same physical stress response, interpreted differently, genuinely produces a better outcome.
- **Practice deep belly breathing well before test day**, not just in the moment — a habit built in advance is far more reliable under real pressure.
- **Use mindfulness to separate a fact ("I have a big test") from the anxious story that tags along after it** — reframing beats suppressing.
- **Cover the answer choices on multiple-choice questions and try to recall the answer first**, before reading the options, to avoid being misled by plausible-sounding distractors.
- **Test yourself under conditions that genuinely resemble the real exam** — time pressure, no notes, an unfamiliar room — not just relaxed, open-book practice at home.
- **Rest, don't cram, the day before a big test.** A light review is fine; heavy new studying the day before can leave both focused and diffuse mode under-rested.
- **Double-check your work from a different angle whenever possible** — work backward through a test when reviewing, and check that units match on both sides of an equation as a fast correctness check.
- **Have a genuine backup plan for the worst-case outcome.** Knowing you have a Plan B measurably reduces pre-test anxiety, and often improves actual performance as a result.
