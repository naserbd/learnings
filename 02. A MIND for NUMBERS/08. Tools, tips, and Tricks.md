# Chapter 8: Tools, Tips, and Tricks

> "We trick ourselves into doing what we ought to be doing... To a great degree, the highest-performing people I know are those who have installed the best tricks in their lives... The smart part of us sets up things for us to do that the not-so-smart part responds to almost automatically, creating behavior that produces high-performance results."
> — David Allen, management specialist

Allen means tricks like wearing exercise clothes to get in the mood to actually exercise, or leaving an important report by the front door so it's impossible to miss. Students consistently report that studying in a genuinely new environment — a quiet library corner with few interrupting cues — works wonders against procrastination. Research backs this up: a dedicated space used *only* for working genuinely helps.

**Another useful trick: meditation**, specifically as a way to practice ignoring distracting thoughts. (This isn't just a New Age habit — real science backs its value.) *Buddha in Blue Jeans* by Tai Sheridan is a short, free, faith-neutral guide to getting started, and plenty of meditation apps exist if that format suits you better.

**A final major trick: reframing your focus.** One student manages a consistent 4:30 a.m. wake-up not by dwelling on how tired he feels, but by focusing on how good breakfast will taste.

**The most striking reframing story: Roger Bannister**, the first person to run a mile in under four minutes. A medical student with no trainer, no special diet, and barely 30 minutes a day to actually train around his studies, Bannister didn't dwell on all the logical reasons he had no real shot. He simply refocused on reaching his goal *his* way. On the morning he made history, he ate his usual breakfast, did his hospital rounds, then caught a bus to the track.

**It's normal to sit down with some negative feelings before starting work.** What actually separates fast starters from chronic procrastinators is how they handle that feeling — fast starters push the negative thoughts aside with something like: "Quit wasting time, just get on with it. You'll feel better once it's moving." That's a learnable skill, not a personality trait.

> **Elizabeth Ploughman, Lecturer of Physics, Camosun College:**
> "I tell my students they can procrastinate, as long as they follow three rules:
> 1. No computer during procrastination time — it's too engrossing.
> 2. Before procrastinating, identify the *easiest* homework problem (no need to solve it yet).
> 3. Copy the relevant equation(s) for that problem onto a small piece of paper, and carry it around until you're ready to get back to work.
>
> This works because it lets the problem quietly simmer in diffuse mode — students are still working on it, even while technically procrastinating."

## Self-Experimentation: The Key to a Better You

Dr. Seth Roberts, professor emeritus of psychology at UC Berkeley, began experimenting on *himself* while learning to run experiments as a grad student. His first target: his own acne. Prescribed tetracycline by a dermatologist, he simply counted his pimples at varying doses — and found the drug made **no difference at all**. He'd stumbled onto a finding medicine wouldn't catch up to for another decade: tetracycline (which carries real side-effect risk) doesn't reliably treat acne, while benzoyl peroxide cream — contrary to his own assumption — actually did work.

As Roberts put it: *"From my acne research I learned that self-experimentation can be used by non-experts to (a) see if the experts are right and (b) learn something they don't know. I hadn't realized such things were possible."* He went on to use self-experimentation to study his own mood, manage his weight, and test omega-3's effect on his cognitive function.

**Self-experimentation is genuinely useful for testing ideas and generating new hypotheses.** As Roberts notes: *"By its nature, self-experimentation involves making sharp changes in your life: you don't do X for several weeks, then you do X for several weeks... this, plus the fact that we monitor ourselves in a hundred ways, makes it easy for self-experimentation to reveal unexpected side effects... daily measurements of acne, sleep, or anything else, supply a baseline that makes it even easier to see unexpected changes."*

**Your own first self-experiment should genuinely target procrastination.** Keep notes on when you fail to finish what you planned, what triggered it, and your habitual zombie response. Logging this creates exactly the subtle pressure needed to gradually shift your response, and improve your working habits. In *The Now Habit*, Neil Fiore recommends logging a detailed daily activity schedule for a week or two, specifically to pinpoint your real procrastination trouble spots. Different people also work better in different environments — some thrive in a busy coffee shop, others need genuine library silence. Figuring out what actually works for *you* is part of the experiment.

> **Aukury Cowart, sophomore, electrical engineering:**
> "My tip for procrastination: isolate yourself from anything you know will distract you — including people. Go somewhere alone, like the library, with nothing to pull your attention away."

> **Michael Pariseau, junior, mechanical engineering:**
> "If I'm genuinely struggling with a subject, studying with classmates from the same class helps — I can ask questions, and we can work out together what's actually confusing us. Chances are I understand what's confusing them, and vice versa."

*(These two approaches aren't actually in conflict — isolation helps with pure procrastination, while group work helps with genuine confusion about material. Different problems, different tools.)*

## The Planner-Journal: Your Personal Lab Notebook

**The simplest way to genuinely gain control of your habits:** once a week, write a brief list of key tasks. Then, each day, write a short list of what you can realistically work on — ideally, written the evening *before*.

**Why the night before?** Research shows this lets your subconscious start grappling with the list overnight, effectively enlisting your zombies to help tackle it the next day.

Most people already track due dates on a phone, calendar, or planner. From that calendar, distill a **weekly to-do list of 20 or fewer key items.** Each night, pull from that weekly list to build the **next day's list — kept to 5–10 items.** Try not to add to the daily list once it's set (barring a genuinely urgent surprise), and avoid constantly swapping items around.

**Why write it down at all?** Unwritten tasks lurk at the edge of your roughly four working-memory slots, quietly eating up mental space. Writing them down frees that space for actual problem-solving — but only if you genuinely trust yourself to check the list. If your subconscious doesn't trust that you'll actually look at it, those tasks start creeping back into working memory anyway.

**One more tip, borrowed from writing coach Daphne Gray-Grant: "eat your frogs first thing in the morning."** Tackle the most important, most dreaded task first, right when you wake up. It's genuinely effective.

**A sample day from the author's own planner-journal:**

```
NOV. 30
- PNAS paper (1 hour)
- Go for a walk
- Book (1 section)
- ISE 150: demo prep
- EGR 260: prepare 1 question for final exam
- Finalize upcoming talk
- Focus, fun!

Goal finish time for day: 5:00 P.M.
```

**Notes on this example:** just six items, some process-oriented (a paper due in months gets a little focused time most days) and some product-oriented (only because they're genuinely doable in a limited window). The reminder "Focus, fun!" is deliberate — a nudge to stay present *and* enjoy the work. Getting sidetracked one day (forgetting to close email) prompted a quick 22-minute Pomodoro to reset — an arbitrary length, since there's no rule the timer has to be exactly 25 minutes every time, and switching into "Pomodoro mode" itself is a shift back toward process-orientation.

None of the day's items are huge, since other things (meetings, a lecture) are already competing for the day. Sprinkling in a physical task now and then (pulling weeds, sweeping) — used deliberately as a diffuse-mode break — tends to make it genuinely more enjoyable than it sounds, and helps break up long stretches of sitting.

**With experience, you'll get noticeably better at estimating how long any given task actually takes** — and more realistic about what fits into a day. Some people rank tasks 1–5 by priority; others star high-priority items, or use checkboxes. The author personally likes drawing a bold line through a finished item. Whatever system genuinely works for you is the right one.

> **Randall Broadwell, mechanical engineering student with a German minor:**
> "To fight procrastination, I schedule everything: 'Friday, start the paper, finish Saturday. Also Saturday, do math homework. Sunday, study for German.' It keeps me organized and practically stress-free. If I skip the schedule, I just end up with double the work the next day — and that's genuinely not something I look forward to."

*(If a written planner hasn't worked for you before, try a chalkboard or whiteboard by your door instead — it builds in a more obvious, constant visual reminder, and checking things off still delivers that same satisfying hit.)*

**Notice the 5:00 p.m. quitting time in the sample day above — planning when to *stop* matters just as much as planning when to work.** The author generally aims to quit by 5 p.m. — though revisiting something new right before bed can genuinely be enjoyable, and big projects occasionally demand more. The 5 p.m. cutoff exists specifically to preserve real family time and evening reading — made feasible by rising early and working six days a week, which obviously isn't required unless your actual workload genuinely calls for it.

You might assume this only works because the author is well past the demands of student life — but Cal Newport, a widely respected study-skills expert, kept a 5 p.m. quitting time through most of *his* own student career, and went on to earn a Ph.D. from MIT. Repeatedly, the people who protect genuine leisure time alongside hard work outperform those who just grind endlessly.

**Once you finish your daily list, you're genuinely done for the day.** If you notice yourself consistently blowing past your planned stop time, or leaving items unfinished, the planner-journal itself is what lets you catch and correct that pattern. Even if your actual life doesn't easily allow for scheduled breaks (juggling two jobs and a full course load, say), try to genuinely squeeze in *some* break time regardless.

**Translate distant deadlines into daily ones.** Break a big task down until pieces of it show up on your *daily* list — the only way to walk a thousand-mile journey is one actual step at a time.

### Try It: Planning for Success

Pick a small piece of a task you've been avoiding. Decide specifically *where* and *when* you'll tackle it — the library this afternoon, phone on airplane mode? A different room tomorrow evening, laptop left behind, writing by hand to get started? Simply having a concrete implementation plan — not just a vague intention — makes actually following through dramatically more likely.

**Watch for a common trap:** chronic procrastinators tend to treat each instance of procrastination as a unique, "just this once" exception that surely won't happen again — even though it isn't true. Without a written planner-journal to actually check yourself against, there's nothing to counter that convenient story. As Chico Marx once quipped: *"Who you gonna believe, me or your own eyes?"*

> **Jonathon McCormick, industrial engineering student, on avoiding procrastination:**
> 1. "I write assignments in my planner as due one day *before* they actually are — so I never rush at the last minute, and still have a full day to think things through before submitting."
> 2. "I tell friends I'm doing homework — so if one catches me active on Facebook, they'll call me out and hold me accountable."
> 3. "I keep a framed sheet showing the starting salary of an industrial engineer on my desk. When I can't focus, I look at it and remind myself it'll pay off long-term."

**Some procrastination is unavoidable — but mastering your habits is essential for real progress in math and science.** Your zombies need to stay under *your* control. A planner-journal functions as your eyes on what's actually working. Expect your first few task lists to be overly ambitious — you'll naturally calibrate toward realistic, achievable goals with practice.

**The weekly to-do list is what handles overall prioritization** — it forces a calm step back to see the big picture and set genuine priorities, while building tomorrow's list the night before helps prevent costly, impulsive last-minute decisions. Unforeseen events will still occasionally force plan changes — that's fine. Recall the Law of Serendipity: luck favors the one who tries, and planning well is part of trying. Keep your eyes on the goal, and don't let occasional roadblocks throw you off course.

> **Michael Gashaj, sophomore, industrial engineering:**
> "I stay organized during the week with a daily list on a folded lined sheet I keep in my pocket — I pull it out a couple times a day to check progress. Crossing things off feels great, especially when the list is long — I've got a whole drawer of these folded papers. I find it's easier to start one or a few things at once, so that next time I return to them, they're already partly done, and there's less left to worry about."

## Technology Tools Worth Trying

A simple timer plus pen and paper is often all you genuinely need — but plenty of digital tools can help too.

**Timers:**
- The Pomodoro Technique — pomodorotechnique.com

**Tasks, Planning, and Flashcards:**
- **30/30** — combines a timer with a task list
- **StudyBlue** — pairs flashcards and notes with text reminders when it's time to review again
- **Evernote** — a favorite for task lists and jotting down random ideas, replacing the old paper notebook
- **Anki** — a strong pure-flashcard system with a genuinely excellent spaced-repetition algorithm, with premade decks available across many subjects
- **Quizlet** — build your own flashcards, and split the workload with classmates
- **Google Tasks and Calendar**

**Limiting Time on Distracting Websites:**
- **Freedom** (macOS, Windows, Android) — many people swear by it
- **StayFocusd** — for Chrome
- **LeechBlock** — for Firefox
- **MeeTimer** — for Firefox, tracks and logs where your time actually goes

**Motivation and Accountability:**
- **43 Things** and **StickK** — goal-setting sites
- **Coffitivity** — ambient coffee-shop background noise

**The single easiest block of all: just disable sound notifications on your computer and phone.**

## Summing It Up

- Mental tricks are genuinely powerful tools:
  - Work somewhere with few interruptions (like a library) to fight procrastination.
  - Practice letting distracting thoughts simply drift past, rather than engaging with them.
  - Reframe a troubled attitude — deliberately shift focus from the negative to the positive.
  - Accept that starting with a few negative feelings is completely normal.
- Deliberately planning "playtime" is one of the most important things you can do to prevent procrastination in the first place.
- At the core of procrastination prevention: a reasonable daily to-do list, backstopped by a weekly review to stay on track with the bigger picture.
- Write your daily task list the evening before.
- Eat your frogs first.

For the next two weeks: write weekly goals at the start of each week, then each day, write 5–10 small, realistic daily goals drawn from that weekly list. ## A Story of Resilience: Mary Cha

> Mary Cha's father left when she was three weeks old; her mother died when she was nine. She struggled badly through middle and high school, and left her adoptive parents' home as a teenager with $60 to her name. Today, she's a 3.9 GPA biochemistry major working toward medical school.
>
> Joining the Army at 25, after her life became financially unmanageable, turned out to be — in her words — the best decision of her life, though not an easy one. The hardest stretch was in Afghanistan, where she felt genuinely alienated from coworkers she had little in common with, and studied math in her spare time partly just to keep her mind engaged.
>
> Military life forced her into genuinely good study habits — not long focused stretches, but grabbing a few minutes wherever she could, working in short bursts between constant interruptions. That's how she stumbled onto what she calls **"magical math marination"** — her own name for diffuse-mode processing. Stuck on a problem, she'd get pulled away to respond to an incident; while leading her team, or just waiting quietly, part of her mind kept working the problem in the background. Returning to her room that night, the answer would often simply be there.
>
> **Another trick: active review** — mentally reviewing already-solved problems while doing something else physical, like showering or styling her hair, keeping the material at the front of her mind so it doesn't slip away.
>
> **Her actual study process:**
> 1. Do all the odd-numbered problems in a section (or enough of each *type* to genuinely round out understanding).
> 2. Let the problems marinate.
> 3. Build reference sheets covering every important concept, with one example of each problem type worth keeping in her toolbox.
> 4. Before an exam, be able to *list*, from memory, everything on those sheets — the subjects, problem types, and techniques. Simply being able to name the sections and topics builds real recognition speed and genuine confidence walking into the exam.
>
> "When I was younger, I thought that if I didn't get something immediately, it meant I'd never get it — or that I just wasn't smart. That isn't true at all. Now I understand how important it is to start early, and leave real time for things to digest. That leads to stress-free understanding, and makes learning a lot more enjoyable."

## Key Takeaways, Tips, and Tricks

- **Change your environment to change your behavior** — a quiet, dedicated workspace (like a library) with few interrupting cues genuinely reduces procrastination.
- **Try meditation** to practice letting distracting thoughts pass by, rather than engaging with every one.
- **Reframe your focus** — shift attention from a task's unpleasant side to something genuinely positive about doing it (Bannister's breakfast, the early riser's good meal ahead).
- **It's normal to feel some resistance before starting** — the difference between fast starters and procrastinators is simply pushing past that feeling, not avoiding it entirely.
- **Try "structured procrastination":** if you're going to procrastinate anyway, ban the computer, pick the easiest homework problem first, and carry its key equation around on paper — letting diffuse mode work on it even while you delay.
- **Run self-experiments on your own procrastination** — log when you miss a goal, what triggered it, and your habitual response, the same way you'd track any experimental data.
- **Match your environment to the problem**: isolate yourself to fight pure procrastination; study with classmates to resolve genuine confusion about material.
- **Keep a planner-journal**: a weekly list of 20 or fewer key items, distilled each night into a daily list of 5–10 tasks — written the evening before, so your subconscious can start working on it overnight.
- **"Eat your frogs first"** — do the most important, most dreaded task first thing.
- **Plan your quitting time, not just your working time.** A firm daily stop time protects real leisure and rest — and top performers, not just casual students, use this successfully.
- **Break big, distant deadlines into small daily tasks** — the only way through a huge project is one concrete daily step at a time.
- **Make a concrete implementation plan, not just an intention** — decide the specific where and when for a task you've been avoiding.
- **Watch for the "just this once" trap** — procrastination habitually gets excused as a one-time exception. A written record is what keeps you honest about the actual pattern.
- **Use technology deliberately, not accidentally** — timers, flashcard apps with spaced repetition, and website blockers can all help; disabling notifications alone is the single easiest win available.
- **Try "active review"** — mentally rehearsing already-solved problems during routine physical activity (showering, walking) to keep material fresh without dedicated study time.
- **Start early, and let things "marinate."** Understanding rarely arrives instantly — giving a problem real time to digest, including time away from it, leads to deeper, less stressful learning.
