# Chapter 11: Powering Up Your Productivity

> "Focus on being productive instead of busy."
> — Tim Ferriss, American entrepreneur, investor, and author

You've probably heard of the so-called "10x developer" — the engineer thought to be ten times as productive as an average one. Whether such a person truly exists is debatable, but it's undeniable that some people get things done faster and more efficiently than others. Becoming one of them starts with constantly looking for ways to improve — your editor, your command line, and all those small utilities senior engineers seem to just... know about. This chapter helps you customize your development environment and become a genuinely more productive engineer: taking control of your editor, navigating the command line with ease, and understanding why mastering keyboard shortcuts is a real superpower.

## Optimizing Your Development Environment

Early developers made do with fairly rudimentary tools. Today, you have a wealth of full-featured editors — IntelliJ IDEA, VS Code, and plenty more. But installing a tool is just the starting line, not the finish.

> **A word of caution:** feel free to explore settings, but don't let it become a time sink. Left unchecked, you might find yourself endlessly tinkering with fonts and themes when you should really be working. Timebox your experiments. Taken too far, heavy customization can also make it genuinely hard to pair or collaborate with others — Nate once tried pairing with a developer who worked exclusively inside Vim, on a specialized keyboard, with Dvorak key layout enabled. It did not go well.

### Know Your Development Tools

In many professions, you're personally responsible for your own tools. There's no communal knife block in a professional kitchen — chefs bring their own knives. Mechanics maintain their own toolbox, organized to their own standards. In plenty of trades, owning the right tools is simply the cost of entry. Your company might cover software license costs — but you're still responsible for your toolkit, and you shouldn't hesitate to genuinely make it your own.

As a software engineer, take real ownership of your development tools. Modern IDEs actively encourage this through plugin ecosystems, letting you build the exact environment that fits you. That doesn't mean memorizing every obscure menu item — but the tools you touch every single day, you should know like the back of your hand.

**The real goal here is staying in a flow state** — the deep, absorbed concentration Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi describes in *Flow: The Psychology of Optimal Experience*. In flow, code seems to appear on the screen almost effortlessly. Stopping every few minutes to look up a command, or reaching for the trackpad when a keyboard shortcut would have worked, disrupts that state and breaks your concentration (more on shortcuts shortly).

> "The code just kind of flows from brain to IDE and, before you realize it (since you know your IDE's key combinations by heart), you're building/testing/running."
> — Mark Heckler, author of *Spring Boot: Up and Running*

Invest real time learning your editor and your version control tool of choice. You'd genuinely rather not be the person who costs the team three weeks of work by accidentally force-pushing over the main branch.

Modern IDEs pack in an enormous range of capabilities — genuinely impossible to stumble across all of them by accident. A more realistic approach: commit to learning one new feature a week. IntelliJ IDEA, for instance, offers a "Tip of the Day" on launch (mentioned back in Chapter 2) — a great way to passively discover shortcuts, tools, refactoring capabilities, and plugins you'd never have found on your own. Look for similar features in whatever editor you use, and make a habit of learning something new regularly.

### Build Your Own Lightsaber

When you get behind the wheel of a new car, you adjust the seat, the mirrors, the steering wheel — driving with the factory defaults untouched simply wouldn't be comfortable, or safe. The same logic applies to your development environment. Your tools have a huge range of settings — spend some real time tuning them to your preferences. Think of it as building your own lightsaber: it should fit your hands, your personality, your needs. Never be shy about making your environment genuinely yours.

**Font and theme.** You'll spend hours upon hours staring at code in your editor — your eyes will genuinely thank you for exploring your font options. A different font isn't just aesthetic — it's another way to shape your environment around your own preferences. Plenty of excellent monospace fonts exist; a quick search turns up dozens of "best monospace fonts for coding" lists. Try one for a day or two, then try another. There's nothing magical about 11- or 12-point size either — if something else genuinely reads better to your eyes, change it.

Most editors offer countless themes, altering nearly everything visually. Many developers love dark mode, since staring at a bright display all day can strain the eyes — though dark themes aren't universally the right fit, so try a few until you find one that genuinely works for you. Some operating systems can even auto-adjust brightness based on ambient light or time of day, further easing eye strain. Whatever your preference, actively explore themes rather than settling for the default.

**Beyond the editor.** Your operating system itself can usually be tuned extensively — the defaults aren't necessarily right for you. Try relocating the dock, adjusting magnification, changing your background. A whole ecosystem of free or low-cost utilities exists to unlock further customization — clipboard managers, finer sound controls, menu bar organizers. It's genuinely likely that removing some daily friction is just a short install away. If you spot something unfamiliar in a colleague's menu bar, ask about it — that one question could save you hours a month.

**Your physical environment matters too.** Adjust your chair and monitor height to keep your body in a neutral position. Some people prefer a standing desk, with plenty of solid adjustable options available. If your company has an ergonomics team, request an assessment of your setup, and don't hesitate to ask for a better chair or an adjustable desk. If you work remotely and your company doesn't offer an equipment stipend, it's genuinely worth investing in a quality desk and chair yourself — your back will thank you.

**Eye strain is a real, physical cost of this job.** Take regular breaks to focus on something other than your monitor throughout the day. If you wear glasses, make sure your optometrist knows how much screen time you actually get — special coatings and lens options exist specifically to ease this. Some developers even keep a separate pair of glasses with a prescription tuned specifically for their screen-viewing distance.

### Buy a Better Keyboard

Speaking of physical setup — don't be afraid to buy a genuinely better keyboard. Whatever your company bulk-ordered probably isn't the ideal fit for you specifically. An ergonomic split keyboard — something like the highly customizable ErgoDox EZ, where you can even choose your preferred keyswitch feel — can make a real, daily difference. Use whatever genuinely works best for you, and don't hesitate to ask your company to cover some or all of the cost — many will.

## Leverage the Power of the Command Line

Before graphical, "what you see is what you get" interfaces existed, computers were purely text-based, requiring cryptic, unforgiving commands. Modern GUIs are genuinely great — but as a software engineer, you should also be comfortable in the **command-line interface (CLI)**.

The command line can feel intimidating at first, but it quickly becomes a genuine superpower and force multiplier — many common tasks are dramatically faster with a handful of keystrokes than clicking through a GUI. Spend a few minutes a week experimenting with new commands.

> **The manual is built right into the command line.** If you're unsure what something does, just look it up with the `man` command (e.g., `man grep`).

**Shell commands are genuinely your friend.** You're probably already familiar with `cd`, `pwd`, `ls`, and `mkdir` — but there's far more available. From the command line, `open` launches an application directly. Commands like `cat`, `cut`, `grep`, and `pbcopy` (macOS's clipboard copy command) can all save real time.

**These commands become dramatically more powerful when combined using the pipe (`|`) operator.** A pipe takes the *output* of one command and feeds it directly in as the *input* to the next — letting you chain together several small, single-purpose tools into a much more powerful combined operation.

**A concrete example — grabbing a Git repository's remote URL and copying it straight to your clipboard:**

```bash
cat .git/config | grep url | cut -f2 -d= | pbcopy
```

**Walking through this pipeline, stage by stage:**

1. `cat .git/config` — prints the entire contents of the Git config file.
2. `| grep url` — filters that output down to just the line(s) containing the word "url."
3. `| cut -f2 -d=` — splits that line on the `=` character (`-d=` sets the delimiter) and keeps only the second field (`-f2`) — i.e., everything *after* the equals sign, which is the actual URL.
4. `| pbcopy` — sends that final result straight to your clipboard, ready to paste.

Four small, individually simple commands, chained together, accomplish something genuinely useful in one line — no manually opening the file, scanning for the right line, and selecting the text by hand.

**A fun one — making your computer talk to you:**

```bash
say "I can't let you do that Nate."
```

**Beyond built-in commands, plenty of third-party CLI tools are worth adding to your toolkit:**

- **`bat`** — like `cat`, but with syntax highlighting.
- **`diff-so-fancy`** — produces noticeably nicer, more readable diffs.
- **`fx`** — handy if you spend a lot of time staring at raw JSON.
- **`exa`** — like `ls`, but with better color output.

**Shell aliases** let you save real keystrokes for commands you type often. Add a line or two to your shell's config file (`~/.bashrc` for Bash, `~/.zshrc` for Zsh), and you've got yourself a permanent shortcut:

```bash
alias k=kubectl
alias d='docker'
alias dc='docker compose'
```

After adding these, typing `k get pods` runs `kubectl get pods` — a small thing, but one that adds up meaningfully across a day of heavy CLI use. (One caveat: aliases live only on machines where you've set them up — be mindful of that when you're working on someone else's machine, or a fresh server, where your custom shortcuts won't exist.)

**Shell history is a genuinely underrated productivity tool.** Press the up arrow to scroll through recent commands, and Tab to autocomplete based on recent history. The `history` command lists everything you've previously run, and **Ctrl-R** lets you reverse-search through your command history interactively — type a few characters, and it jumps straight to the most recent matching command. All of this is tunable too — you can increase your history size, or even exclude certain commands from being recorded at all. Add a fuzzy-finder tool into the mix, and there's a real chance you'll rarely type the exact same long command twice.

**Finally, don't feel locked into your OS's default shell.** Alternatives like **Oh My Zsh** exist, bundling helpful built-in functions alongside thousands of community plugins, themes, and productivity helpers.

## Harness the Power of Keyboard Shortcuts

Modern operating systems lean heavily on the pointer — mouse, trackpad, or touch. That's genuinely made computing more accessible — but your goal, as a developer, should be keeping your hands on the keyboard's home row as much as possible. Learning keyboard shortcuts isn't just faster — it also spares your wrists and hands the repeated strain of constantly reaching for a mouse.

> **If you don't touch-type, learn to. Seriously.** Smartphones taught an entire generation to type with their thumbs — fine for texting friends, but as a developer, you want all ten fingers engaged when writing code. Touch typing is faster, and genuinely easier on your hands, eyes, and neck over the long run. You probably weren't taught this in school, but plenty of gamified online tools exist specifically to teach it. Learn to touch type — you'll thank yourself later.

Mastering keyboard shortcuts is one of the clearest things separating beginners from seasoned engineers, and it becomes a real force multiplier over time. Learn the shortcuts for your editor *and* your operating system both.

**Some shortcuts become so automatic you barely think about them** — most computer users already know `Cmd-C`/`Cmd-V` (or `Ctrl-C`/`Ctrl-V` on Windows/Linux) by heart. But there's far more available:

- **`Ctrl` + Down Arrow** (macOS) — shows every open window of the current frontmost app at once.
- **`Cmd-H`** — instantly hides the frontmost application.
- **In IntelliJ:** select any two files and press **`Cmd-D`** to see a diff between them — this even works on JARs and ZIPs.

**Many applications and operating systems also support Emacs-style key bindings**, especially useful for navigating text — code included:

| Shortcut | Action |
|---|---|
| `Ctrl-F` / `Ctrl-B` | Move forward/backward one character |
| `Meta-F` / `Meta-B` | Move forward/backward one whole word |
| `Ctrl-A` / `Ctrl-E` | Jump to the beginning/end of the current line |

A few minutes spent learning a handful of these bindings can save you real, compounding time over the course of a career.

> ### Emacs Versus vi
>
> If you genuinely want to start a heated debate in a project room, bring up Emacs versus vi — it makes "tabs vs. spaces" look tame by comparison. The old joke: "Emacs is a great operating system, lacking only a decent editor," while "Vim is a great editor, utterly lacking a decent window manager." Consider yourself warned.
>
> That said, learning basic vi is genuinely worth doing — in some situations, it may be the *only* editor available to you on a given machine. And Emacs key bindings, as covered above, can make you a genuine file-navigation savant regardless of which editor you ultimately prefer day to day.

**Even the ubiquitous copy/paste combo can be optimized.** A clipboard-history tool like **Pastebot** gives you access to everything you've recently copied, not just the single most recent item — genuinely useful when you need to copy several things from one application into another, rather than repeatedly flipping back and forth between windows. (Just be mindful with sensitive information, like passwords, sitting in that history.)

**Text expanders** are another strong productivity tool — standalone apps like TextExpander, or the built-in text-replacement features many operating systems already include, let you type a short abbreviation that automatically expands into something longer (a common code snippet, an email signature, a full address). Small savings, but they genuinely compound over time.

Your environment is genuinely rich with customization potential — don't settle for defaults. Your tools should conform to *you*, not the other way around. Time spent tuning your environment is a real, compounding investment in your own productivity.

## Strategic Automation

You spend all day writing code for other people — don't hesitate to write some for yourself too. A handful of lines of code can save you hours of repetitive work down the line, and gives you a genuinely good excuse to experiment with other languages and techniques along the way. Many developers have a love-hate relationship with regular expressions — but even a little regex knowledge goes a genuinely long way.

**A useful mental model from computer science: there are only three numbers — zero, one, and *n*.** Something you do zero times doesn't matter. Something you do exactly once, by definition, isn't worth automating. But the moment you do something *more than once*, assume you'll end up doing it many, many more times — that's your signal to automate it.

> **A caution:** automation itself can become a time sink. Sometimes writing the automation genuinely takes longer than just doing the task manually. If something really is a one-off you're unlikely to repeat, automating it isn't time well spent. It's often wiser to wait until you've done a task a handful of times *first*, before investing in automating it away — and even then, timebox the effort. Ultimately, use your own judgment here.

**Write helper scripts.** Thirty lines of Python can genuinely save you hours down the road. The underlying moral: automation is your friend — let the computer take on more of the repetitive load. And if you'd rather not write the script yourself, AI can genuinely help draft one for you — just make sure you actually read and test what it produces. The barrier to automating something away has never been lower.

## The Perpetual Pursuit of Productive Habits

The sheer number of available shortcuts, utilities, and commands is practically endless — trying to learn them all can feel overwhelming. Don't try to absorb everything in a month or two; treat it as an ongoing, incremental process. The real goal is small, steady improvement — aim to learn one new thing a week. It's a far more sustainable pace, and you'll actually retain what you learn.

### Collaborative Learning

Your coworkers are genuinely one of the best sources of tips and tricks available to you. Get in the habit of asking teammates about their own productivity practices. If a colleague uses a tool, command, or shortcut you haven't seen before, just ask — there's no reason to feel embarrassed about not already knowing it. The reverse holds too: if you notice a teammate doing something the hard way, take a moment to show them the easier path. Repeat it a few times together to help it actually stick, rather than mentioning it once and moving on.

If a colleague doesn't know how to get started with a tool you love, help them get set up — teaching it further cements your own understanding, and gives you a familiar, shared environment to work in together going forward.

Commit a few minutes each week to learning something new about your tools, and make a habit of sharing what you learn with others. Pair programming is a natural venue for this — if your pair uses something unfamiliar to you, ask them to walk you through it; if you know a shortcut they don't, teach it to them in return. A semi-regular lunch-and-learn is another great, low-pressure forum for people to share things they've picked up — don't assume everyone already knows what you know (they very likely don't). And by the same logic, don't skip a session on a tool you already feel confident with — there's usually still something new to pick up.

**This mindset extends to your programming language of choice, too.** Languages keep evolving — your own practice needs to evolve alongside them. When a new version ships, actually read the release notes and explore what's new. Teach others what you find, and stay alert for real opportunities to put new features into practice. Leading a study group works well here too — the classic "see one, do one, teach one" pattern is genuinely effective for making new knowledge stick.

## Personal Knowledge Management

Technology moves fast, and you'll be constantly flooded with information — podcasts, blog posts, videos, tutorials. It's genuinely worth having some kind of personal knowledge management strategy. It doesn't need to be elaborate or involve dedicated software — but you should have *somewhere* to stash interesting bits of information for later. You need a way to capture and organize the steady flow of information moving through your world. Few things are more frustrating than knowing you once had the exact answer to a problem, and having absolutely no memory of where you put it.

**Take notes.** You will run into that exact same problem again — and so will a teammate, eventually. Write it down; you genuinely won't remember it next time. Offload it from your own memory — external storage wins here, every time. It doesn't matter *how* you do it, or how formal the system is. A binder clip full of scrap paper works perfectly well too — though electronic notes are naturally easier to search later, and harder to accidentally lose, while sharing paper notes with colleagues takes a bit more effort.

Some developers keep a running note or document on their laptop. Others prefer a physical notebook (Field Notes, Moleskine) paired with a good pocket pen. Many lean toward a dedicated tool — Org Mode, Evernote, Notion. It's also worth thinking about how you'll manage the many web resources you'll stumble across day to day — syncing organized bookmarks across devices is one option; a dedicated bookmark manager like Pinboard is another.

> That said — there's no shortage of tools in this space, and while Notion is central to my own workflow, your mileage will genuinely vary. Use whatever actually works for *you*.

**Conferences are a great source of new skills** — but capturing what you learn for later study matters just as much as attending. Many conferences post session recordings afterward — take time to identify the talks that genuinely interest you, and curate your own playlist for easy replay or recall down the line. Some developers treat these talks like podcasts, queuing them up for a walk, a workout, or a commute — and playing them back above 1x speed to consume more content in less time.

**Whatever system you land on, make sure it's not locked to a single device — it should genuinely travel with you.** You never know when a flash of insight will strike for a work problem, or when a friend will recommend a great book or podcast — be ready to capture it wherever you happen to be.

## Wrapping Up

A genuinely good software engineer takes ownership of their toolkit. Just as you'd spend a few minutes adjusting the seat, mirrors, and steering wheel in a new car, your development environment should be shaped around *you* and your actual needs. Don't just accept the defaults — make your editor and operating system genuinely yours. Power users lean on power tools — keyboard shortcuts and the command line are real force multipliers, and you'd be surprised how much a small, free utility can improve your day. Don't be afraid to keep trying new things.

Keeping track of every shortcut and utility can feel like a lot — don't rely purely on memorization. Build a genuine knowledge management habit as your toolkit grows, and keep learning from — and teaching — your colleagues along the way.

## Putting It into Practice

- **Automate something small.** Pick a genuinely repetitive task, and see if you can script the toil away. If you already have some automation in place, consider expanding it to cover something a bit more significant. Share what you build with your colleagues.

- **Learn one shortcut a week.** Commit to picking up one new keyboard shortcut a week for the next few months. Spend 15 minutes some Friday afternoon browsing your favorite editor's documentation — what did you find that you genuinely didn't know before? Turn on your editor or terminal's "tip of the day" feature, if it has one — what surprised you?

- **Audit your knowledge management.** Is your current system actually working for you? If not, try something different — it doesn't need to be complex, or involve fancy tooling. It just needs to genuinely work for *you*.

- **Write it down.** The next time you learn a new command, jot it down somewhere. Lean on that external brain — it's there precisely so you don't have to rely on remembering everything yourself.
