# Chapter 1: Programmer to Engineer

> "Foundational skills, always tedious to learn, seem to be obsolete. And they might be, if there was a shortcut to being an expert. But the path to expertise requires a grounding in facts."
> — Ethan Mollick, *Co-Intelligence* (Portfolio, 2024)

Being a software engineer takes more than writing code. It means:

- Understanding what your customer is actually asking for
- Translating those needs into maintainable code
- Writing tests to confirm the software actually does what you think it does
- Building interfaces that work
- Thinking through architecture
- Working with data
- Getting code safely into production

If you want to grow your career as a software practitioner, you have to look past just writing code. You have to embrace the entire craft of engineering software.

Getting from *programmer* to *engineer* means mastering the fundamentals across the whole software development lifecycle (SDLC), working smart, and — just as importantly — being honest about what you don't yet know, and having a plan to close those gaps. This chapter gives you a starting point: the different paths people take into this field, the knowledge those paths often skip over, and some hard-earned advice to smooth the road ahead.

First, though — what does "software engineer" actually mean?

## An Engineer by Any Other Name

Software terminology gets reused and repurposed constantly, which leaves a lot of room for ambiguity. Ask five software professionals to define a term, and you'll likely get five different answers. (Fun fact: the word "computer" originally referred to a *person* who performed calculations by hand.) Today you'll hear terms like *programmer*, *coder*, *developer*, and *software engineer* — often used interchangeably, as if they're synonyms.

They aren't.

**Programmer or coder** usually implies someone focused narrowly on producing code. They may be highly skilled in a specific language or framework, but often without a full grasp of the broader development lifecycle. Many careers start here — but climbing further requires moving past just fixing bugs or bolting on features.

**Developer** usually implies a broader understanding of how software actually gets delivered. Developers typically know several languages and frameworks, have worked across multiple business domains, and — being generally more experienced — have started engaging with more of the SDLC. They often mentor or pair with less experienced teammates.

**Software engineer** means bringing real engineering discipline to the *entire* lifecycle. Writing good code is assumed at this level — what sets engineers apart is thinking seriously about scalability, reliability, efficiency, and security. It's not just knowing the rules of good design, but knowing when to bend them, and when to break them outright. Software engineers are the ones trusted with the most complex, most critical systems running in production today.

Boot camps and university programs mostly teach the *mechanics* of writing code — they produce developers and programmers. That's a fine starting point. But to excel, to get promoted, to land the most interesting work, you need to grow beyond that starting point.

There's more to software than writing syntactically correct programs. Real success requires understanding the *entire lifecycle* of a software product — not just a programming language. This book exists to help guide you through exactly that: showing you the things you may not even know you don't know.

## Fundamentals Matter

Professional athletes spend most of their practice time on the same basics they learned when they first started. Golfers drill stance, grip, and alignment. Basketball players drill layups, passing, and free throws — not the flashy plays you see on highlight reels. Fundamentals aren't exciting, but without them, no one reaches the top of their sport.

Software has its own fundamentals, and they deserve the same discipline. You'll spend the bulk of your career working inside *existing* code — learning to read someone else's code, and quickly getting your bearings in an unfamiliar codebase, is one of the most valuable skills you can build. An unfamiliar codebase can feel intimidating at first. But you have to get comfortable with ambiguity: even after months on a project, you won't understand every nuance of it — and you don't need to, in order to start making useful changes.

Writing code is one thing; writing code that makes life *easier* for whoever inherits it next is a different, deeper skill. And technology never stands still — you have to keep learning. A lot of developers get pulled toward chasing the newest language feature or framework, while neglecting the "boring" soft skills that actually drive career growth. Fundamentals don't generate buzz — but they're the real difference between stalling out and moving into work with greater scope and responsibility.

## The Many Paths to Becoming a Software Engineer

There's no single correct path into this field. Some people earn an undergraduate computer science degree. Others go through a boot camp. Others are entirely self-taught. Plenty come from adjacent fields — electrical engineering, for instance — and migrate into writing code later. Honestly, *how* you learned to build software doesn't matter much in the end.

You'll hear people argue their own path was "better," "faster," or "cheaper" than the alternatives. Every path has real trade-offs:

- **Self-taught:** no tuition costs, and — depending on your aptitude — potentially much faster than a formal program. But the internet's sheer volume of tutorials and videos can be overwhelming to navigate, it demands serious self-motivation, and you'll likely hit a fair amount of trial-and-error working through material of wildly varying quality.

- **University degree programs:** these are fundamentally designed to prepare you for *graduate* study in computer science — heavy on algorithms, language design, compiler theory, and operating systems. They don't necessarily prepare you for the day-to-day reality of building real-world applications. Projects tend to be small, span only a few months, and almost always start completely from scratch — free of the baggage that comes with an existing, living codebase.

- **Boot camps:** these compress very specific, practical knowledge into a short window — often just weeks — usually centered on whatever language or framework is currently in demand. That practicality is a real strength, but it comes with a trade-off: that kind of knowledge can go stale as the popularity of specific languages and frameworks rises and falls over time. (Interestingly, boot camps are sometimes even hosted at universities, occasionally taught by the very same instructors who teach the undergraduate courses — so the *teacher* isn't necessarily even different.)

**Time investment varies a lot too:**

| Path | Typical time commitment |
|---|---|
| Boot camp | ~600 hours, over several weeks |
| Associate's degree | ~850 hours, over 1–2 years |
| Bachelor's degree | ~1,400 hours, over 4–5 years |

Some argue a university degree provides an early career advantage — though this tends to even out over time. It's also worth remembering that "computer science degree" as we know it today is a fairly recent invention. Not that long ago, most programmers were trained mathematicians.

> **A brief history lesson:** Early computer science programs were often heavily weighted toward mathematics — some were practically math degrees with a bit of computer science mixed in, and many of the professors held math degrees themselves. That history still lingers today in the belief that strong math skills are a prerequisite for being a good software engineer. **They're not.** Writing code is fundamentally about *communication* — most of the job is communicating with other developers, not with the computer. Language aptitude matters far more than mathematical skill. Unsurprisingly, code is really about language, not numbers — a theme this book returns to in Chapter 2.

Ultimately, success in software comes down to problem-solving, tinkering, and creativity. If you have that mindset, you have what matters — full stop. However you built your skills, you don't owe anyone an apology for the path you took.

## What You Were Taught Versus What You Need to Know

Whatever path you took, you were taught *how to write code*. A language or two, generic placeholder variables like `foo` and `bar`, some debugging basics, some algorithm design. But a huge amount of genuinely important material rarely makes it into a curriculum or a self-study plan — usually because of limited time, limited scope, or a simple assumption that "you'll just pick that up somewhere."

What gets left out can seriously affect your long-term success: how to work with other people, how to *read* code (not just write it), how to write genuinely readable code, and how to work effectively inside legacy systems. This book covers all of that, and more, in the chapters ahead.

Most developers learn on **greenfield projects** — ones that start from a completely blank slate, with none of the accumulated technical debt of a real, existing codebase. Given the tight timelines of a semester or a boot camp, that makes sense pedagogically: you're usually starting fresh to practice one specific skill, working on something small and isolated, often entirely solo. That works fine as a teaching tool — but it doesn't look much like the real world.

As a working software engineer, you'll spend most of your time inside existing, legacy, or long-running applications — inheriting decisions made long before you arrived. And even on the rare occasion you *do* get a brand-new codebase, it starts accumulating its own technical debt within months. Real software projects run into the hundreds of thousands, sometimes millions, of lines of code, built by teams spread across the globe. You'll be working with people constantly — teammates, customers, stakeholders. Your code won't just sit in a repository waiting to be graded; it will run in production, where real people depend on it working correctly and delivering real business value. Put plainly: your first real exposure to this reality can be a genuine shock.

In school, most projects last a few months, if that. In the real world, projects essentially never truly "end" — though, like abandoned art, they can eventually just stop receiving attention. Code spends the vast majority of its life in **maintenance** — which is really just another way of saying software is made up of ongoing *products* with continuous investment, not discrete *projects* with a clean start and finish. As long as a piece of software keeps delivering business value, it keeps receiving investment.

## Embrace the Lazy Programmer Ethos

Despite the name, the "lazy programmer ethos" has nothing to do with watching cat videos or dodging your work. It's a philosophy centered on **efficiency**. A lot of new engineers instinctively rush straight into writing code, without pausing to actually think through the problem first. Being *strategically* lazy — deliberately taking time to think before acting — ultimately makes you more productive, and leads to better solutions.

> "Take the time it takes so it takes less time."
> — Pat Parelli, renowned horsemanship trainer (attributed)

**Beware the brute-force instinct.** It's fine as a starting point, but you should iterate from there. It helps to understand concepts like **Big O notation** (a way of describing how an algorithm's performance scales as input grows). You might write a working bubble sort, for example, and later learn it's far from the most efficient way to sort data. As an engineer, weigh the best case, worst case, and average case, and choose deliberately — not by default.

**You're probably not the first person to face this exact problem.** Spend a little time searching for an existing solution or library before building your own from scratch. Ten minutes of searching can sometimes save you days of work. If a task feels like it *should* have an easier path, there's a good chance one already exists.

### Story: The Capitalization Assignment

*Told by Nate.*

Early in my career, I was handed a simple task: capitalize the billing addresses in a database, to keep formatting consistent for the post office when bills got printed and mailed. My tech lead walked me through exactly what to change, including a homegrown capitalization function — written by someone long gone from the company — that I was supposed to call.

Looking at that function, something felt off. In my head, a capitalization function should need exactly one input: the string to capitalize. This one didn't. It took more than a dozen parameters. Understanding it fully would have taken hours.

It seemed to me like this was exactly the kind of problem the *programming language itself* should already solve. So I searched a little — and sure enough, the language had a built-in capitalization function. I used that instead.

The following week, in our team meeting, everyone went around sharing what they were working on. I mentioned the capitalization task. A colleague looked over and asked, "Oh, did you use Joe's capitalization function?"

I said no.

Every head in the room turned toward me. It was a genuine record-scratch moment. I was the least experienced developer in that room — and somehow, not one other person knew the language had a built-in way to do this. Maybe that language feature was added *after* the original function was written; I honestly don't know. But the lesson stuck: **if it feels like there should be an easier way, take the time to check.** Even a couple of hours spent investigating alternatives can save you days, sometimes weeks, of unnecessary effort.

## The Value of a Fresh Set of Eyes

> "The most dangerous phrase in the language is, 'We've always done it this way.'"
> — Grace Hopper, computer pioneer and naval officer

You may not have as many years — or as many lines of code — under your belt as your more senior colleagues, but as a newer developer, you carry something genuinely valuable: **a fresh set of eyes.** People new to a codebase, a team, or even the industry as a whole often see problems without the accumulated bias of history — unburdened by decisions that made sense once but have long since outlived their usefulness. That outside perspective can surface real innovation, spot inefficiencies others have stopped noticing, or challenge assumptions that quietly calcified into "just how it's done." New team members can also bring welcome energy to a project that's been running in place for a while.

### Story: That's How We've Always Done It

*Told by Nate.*

Years ago, I helped a client troubleshoot a performance problem in how their application processed "widgets" during an overnight batch job. When the company was small, they had only a handful of customers, each with just a few boxes of widgets — easily processed overnight. As the business grew, so did the customer base, and so did the volume of widgets. By the time I got involved, the overnight batch job wasn't finishing overnight anymore.

After reviewing the architecture with the team for a couple of hours, I asked one simple question: "Does processing widget A have anything to do with processing widget B?"

No — some aggregation happened at the very end, but each individual widget was processed completely independently.

So I made a simple proposal: what if you just processed the widgets **in parallel**? Deploy a set of worker processes, each grabbing the next available widget, processing it, and dropping the result into a queue to be aggregated afterward.

There was a long pause. Then: "Yes. Yes, that would work."

I'd love to say this came from some special insight — but honestly, it didn't. I just wasn't carrying around the most dangerous phrase you can utter on a team: *"That's how we've always done it."* Just because something has always been done a certain way doesn't mean it's the right way — and it definitely doesn't mean it's the *best* way. Whatever approach was chosen years earlier might have simply been the fastest option available at the time, or even an accident of circumstance. There's usually a better way waiting to be found — don't be afraid to ask questions, or to look for one.

Never underestimate the value of a different perspective, and don't be afraid to ask *why* things are the way they are. If something looks odd, out of place, or just doesn't make sense, ask a teammate to walk you through it. Sometimes an unconventional approach really does have a solid reason behind it — but more often, someone simply copied whatever pattern was already there when they arrived. As long as your questions come from genuine curiosity — not an attempt to embarrass whoever came before you — most engineers will engage with that conversation positively.

## Don't Solution Too Quickly

When you hit a problem, resist jumping straight to a fix. Instead, take the time to actually understand the *root cause*. Give yourself real, uninterrupted space to think — which, realistically, is in short supply. Your calendar is probably packed with meetings, your messaging app pings you constantly, and between daily standups, your product owner, your manager, your team lead, and your architect, you might be giving some kind of status update every couple of hours. Your to-do list never seems to shrink, and QA just found another batch of tricky bugs. Despite all of that pressure, making real time to think through a solution — instead of grabbing the first quick fix — genuinely matters.

**Quick fixes solve the immediate symptom, but often create bigger problems down the line.** This is part of why the industry has moved toward "shifting left" — catching problems as early as possible in the development timeline, rather than downstream. Bugs caught in production are, by a wide margin, the most expensive to fix: customers are already affected (which can cost the company real money), data may already be corrupted (and hard to reconcile), and the actual fix often spans multiple parts of the system, requiring complex refactoring and coordination across teams.

Shifting left isn't only about catching *bugs* earlier — it's about pulling everything of value earlier in the process generally:

- **Pair programming** turns code review from something that happens days or weeks after the work is done into something that happens in real time, as the code is written.
- **Continuous testing** catches problems close to the moment they're introduced, while the context is still fresh.
- **Continuous integration** eases the pain of conflicting changes by merging code early and often; paired with **continuous delivery**, it also surfaces environment-related surprises before they ever reach a customer.
- **Building security in from the start**, rather than bolting it on at the end, reduces the odds of a catastrophic vulnerability slipping through.

It's simply much cheaper to catch issues during testing. A bug caught soon after it's written is still fresh in the team's mind, saving real time re-deciphering old code. Test databases can be reset with minimal effort. The underlying issue might still be genuinely complex to fix — but at least there's less time pressure, since no real customers are affected yet. This is part of what gave rise to dedicated QA testing and QA engineers as a discipline. And it turns out catching issues even earlier — during development itself — is cheaper still, which is part of the motivation behind **test-driven development** (covered in Chapter 5).

**But what if the bug never gets written in the first place?** What if the problem gets solved back at the design stage?

> "Most of the big problems we have with software are problems of misconception. We do not have a good idea of what we are doing before we do it. And then, go, go, go, go and we do everything."
> — Rich Hickey, Clojure Conj 2010 keynote

**Problems of misconception** are everywhere in software — misunderstandings are one of the most common challenges in the field. Words like "client" and "customer" can seem interchangeable, right up until your product owner tells you an approach works fine for a client but not for a customer. They're related, but genuinely distinct — and that nuance actually matters. No amount of testing, and no type system, will ever catch a problem rooted purely in misunderstanding. A technical fix can't repair a gap in shared understanding — that requires actually closing the gap itself.

**The best way to deal with a misunderstanding-driven problem is to avoid creating it in the first place.** That means genuinely focusing on the *problem*, not the solution: Do you actually know what problem your application is solving? Do you understand its context and constraints? What related problems exist nearby? Give yourself real time to think through the problem space before writing anything.

Developers also tend to lean toward *over*engineering solutions. That's not automatically a disaster — there are always multiple valid ways to solve something — but never lose sight of the actual problem you're solving, and don't rush toward a solution before you fully understand it. What is the customer actually asking for — and more importantly, what do they actually *need*? Experience teaches you not to paint yourself into a corner by under-planning — but it's just as easy to swing too far the other way, building a pile of speculative code you'll eventually throw away, or worse, leave sitting around unused indefinitely.

It isn't always easy, but push toward the *why* — the real root cause behind a request (see "The Five Whys" below). Ask questions. Talk to your customers and your product owners directly. Often, a customer will ask for one specific thing while actually needing something else entirely — simply because they can't see past "that's how we've always done it." Sometimes a customer will hand you a very specific, literal solution — "we need to be able to export this table to a spreadsheet," for example. It's tempting to just build exactly that. But a seasoned engineer pauses first and asks what the underlying need actually is. There's frequently a real gap between what a customer explicitly *asks* for and what would actually solve their business problem.

> ### The Five Whys
>
> Originating from the Toyota Production System, the **Five Whys** is a problem-solving technique that digs toward a root cause by asking "Why?" five times in a row — each new "why" targeting the answer just given. This process peels back layer after layer, moving past a surface-level symptom toward the actual underlying issue.
>
> **Classic example:**
> - **Problem:** The robot stopped working.
> - *Why?* The circuit overloaded, causing a fuse to blow.
> - *Why?* There was insufficient lubrication on the bearings, so they locked up.
> - *Why?* The oil pump didn't circulate enough oil.
> - *Why?* The pump's intake is clogged with metal shavings.
> - *Why?* There's no filter on the pump.
>
> **A software example** *(told by Nate):* Years ago, I ran across a web page where every closing HTML tag sat on its own new line. The interpreter didn't care, but it was visually jarring — a closing tag starting nearly every line. I asked a colleague why anyone had written it this way.
>
> "Because of a bug in an old version of the web server we used to run."
> *OK — why are we still supporting that old version?*
> "We're not."
> *OK — why hasn't that bug been fixed, then?*
> "It has been."
> *OK then — why am I still seeing this pattern?*
> "...It was probably an old page."
>
> I mentioned that the page in question had actually been created just a few days earlier. At that point, we agreed: it was time to tell the team to stop following this outdated pattern, and we added a cleanup task to rework the remaining pages still using it.

In many real projects, your main source of requirements ends up being a "super user" of some older system. Their knowledge is genuinely valuable — but it's also inevitably shaped by whatever they're currently used to. Sometimes they won't fully understand *why* a given feature exists the way it does, and may quietly assume it's the *only* way that problem can be solved. In other words — consciously or not — they can steer you toward simply rebuilding the old system in a new technology, rather than solving the actual underlying problem.

Keep a broad perspective. Pick the right tool for the problem the customer *actually* has. A lot of engineers are overly optimistic by nature — stay realistic, and be ruthlessly pragmatic. Scalability genuinely matters, sure — but not every application needs to be built as if it will one day carry a third of all internet traffic.

> "Prediction is very difficult, especially if it's about the future."
> — Danish proverb

## Apply the Golden Rule to Software

Somewhere early in your education, you probably learned the Golden Rule: treat others the way you'd want to be treated. It's a principle embraced across cultures and continents, going back thousands of years, as a basic tenet of how to live well alongside other people. It turns out it isn't just a nice ethical slogan — it applies directly to your code too.

Think about the developer who will follow in your footsteps. (That developer might well end up being *you*, months or years from now, having forgotten every detail of what you built today.) What would that future developer want to find in this codebase? What would genuinely make their life easier? Code is, at its core, a communication tool. Yes, it instructs a computer to do something — but that's honestly the less important part of its job. Its real value lies in how clearly it speaks to the *next developer* who reads it. Write code meant to be read. Optimize for the human reading it, not just the compiler executing it.

If you want to stand out, follow the Golden Rule consistently: write clean code that the people who come after you can actually understand and maintain. Keep documentation updated, so no one wastes hours chasing an approach that's already been abandoned. Build diagrams that genuinely clarify things, instead of adding confusion. Make time to answer questions and help teammates. Put all of that together, and you become the kind of engineer people actively want on their team — because you make everyone around you better.

## Wrapping Up

A career in software engineering is about far more than staring at a screen and producing code. Becoming more than "just" a coder or programmer requires more than syntactically correct output — it demands a genuinely well-rounded skill set. It doesn't matter whether you learned this craft through a university degree, a boot camp, or entirely on your own through material you found online. What matters is how you bring all of those skills together.

What you were formally taught isn't everything you need for a successful career. This chapter covered some of the most common pitfalls to watch for — jumping to solutions too quickly, overengineering, and leaning on brute-force approaches by default. The sooner you internalize these lessons, the better positioned you'll be.

## Putting It into Practice

**Find a mentor.** This can be enormously valuable early in your career. Look around your organization and politely ask a more senior engineer if they'd be willing to grab coffee (your treat) and chat. Ask about their own experiences — what they've learned, what they wish they'd learned sooner, what's made them more valuable on a team, and what they think *you* should focus on learning next. You can also reach out to conference speakers or authors directly — many are genuinely happy to help people who take the initiative to ask.

**Pause before coding.** The next time you're handed a bug or a new feature, resist jumping straight into the editor. Make sure you actually understand the problem first. Spend 30 minutes researching — is there already a ready-made solution you could use instead of building your own? Try sketching out the problem domain and a few possible solutions before committing to one, and consider running your thinking past an experienced coworker.

**Map your own gaps.** Look through this book's table of contents. Where do you already feel strong? Where could you improve? Pick one area you'd like to grow in, make a concrete plan, and follow through on the "Putting It into Practice" exercises and additional resources in that chapter.

**Look under the hood.** Your framework, library, or cloud provider likely abstracts away a lot of your daily work — using something like Spring Data, for instance, saves you from writing countless raw SQL statements by hand. Embracing that productivity is genuinely a good thing — but you should still understand the layer *underneath* whatever abstraction you're using day to day. Take the time to pop the hood occasionally and look around underneath it; that knowledge pays off enormously the moment something doesn't behave the way you expected.
