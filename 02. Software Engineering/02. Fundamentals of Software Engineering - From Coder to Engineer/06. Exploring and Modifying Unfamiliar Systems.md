# Chapter 6: Exploring and Modifying Unfamiliar Systems

> "Some of the most valuable experience I gained was from supporting a legacy app. I highly recommend it, but I wouldn't wish it on anyone."
> — Dalia Abo Sheasha, software developer

Plenty of developers love the blank canvas of a greenfield project — but the honest reality of software engineering is that most of your work will happen inside *established* systems. Don't let that reality sour you on the profession. Working in an existing codebase has real upsides: most of the big infrastructure decisions are already made, letting you focus directly on the actual problem in front of you, instead of everything that goes into standing up a system from nothing.

Whether you're onboarding to a new project or just poking around an unfamiliar codebase, you'll need real skills for exploring and safely modifying systems you didn't build. This chapter builds directly on the code-reading fundamentals from Chapter 2, covering how to actually navigate and understand unfamiliar code, and — just as important — how to change it safely.

## Understanding Unfamiliar Codebases

Joining a new company, or even just a new project at your current one, can be genuinely stressful — often driven by the sheer unknown, or by unfamiliar languages, frameworks, and tools. But approached with a clear plan, this challenge becomes far more manageable, and your confidence grows accordingly.

This section covers three layers of that plan: understanding the big picture and overall goals of a project, following the actual execution flow of the code, and gradually building a mental model by breaking the system into digestible pieces. And an important reminder up front: it's genuinely okay not to understand everything right away. Even developers who've worked on a project for years don't know all of it.

### Start with the Big Picture

"You can't see the forest for the trees" — if you're laser-focused on tiny details (which exact version of a framework is in use), you can easily lose sight of the bigger picture (what this project is actually *for*). It's tempting to dive straight into code and start proving your value immediately — but a little upfront planning pays off.

#### Understanding the Project

Before you can even begin to understand the *code*, you need to understand the project's *purpose*. Start broad: why does this project exist at all? Who are its stakeholders? How important is it to the business?

A great first step: talk to someone with deep product knowledge — a product manager, ideally. Ask for time on their calendar, and come prepared to genuinely listen. Take detailed notes you can refer back to later; if it's a virtual meeting, ask to record it. Most importantly — **ask questions.** The only genuinely bad question is the one you didn't ask.

After that high-level conversation, it's time to go deeper technically. Reach out to engineers currently working on the project — and if you can track down someone who was there for its *original* development, treat that like striking gold. Ideally, find a tech lead or architect who can walk you through the reasoning behind key past decisions.

Also worth investigating: who actually uses this product? Look for any public information about the project from the customer's point of view. Some companies build formal **user personas** — fictional composite profiles representing real user types, their goals, and their pain points. These can give you invaluable context for *why* certain technical decisions were made the way they were.

#### Reviewing Available Documentation

After talking to stakeholders, comb through whatever documentation exists — including any **Architecture Decision Records (ADRs)**. With the big-picture context you've already gathered, much of this documentation should start clicking into place.

A big piece of this is the (often notorious) **onboarding documentation** — everything needed to get the project running on your own machine. Tools like Docker have made this considerably less painful over the years, but reviewing it can still take real effort.

While you're not there to *write* documentation yet, it's worth taking notes on what's missing or unclear as you go — that becomes a natural, high-value contribution you can circle back to later.

> ### Story: The Documentation Trap
>

### Understanding Architecture and Project Structure

Once you understand the project overall and have skimmed the docs, it's time to examine the codebase's actual architecture and organization — a crucial step *before* diving into individual files. A project's structure alone reveals a lot, and gives you a much more holistic sense of the codebase before you get lost in details.

Most projects follow some architectural pattern, whether by deliberate design or organic drift. Here are the most common ones you'll encounter:

**Package by layer** — organizes code horizontally, by *technical* responsibility. Top-level folders like `controllers`, `services`, `repositories`, and `models` group similar *kinds* of components together:

```
src/
├── controllers/
│   ├── UserController.java
│   └── ProductController.java
├── services/
│   ├── UserService.java
│   └── ProductService.java
├── repositories/
│   ├── UserRepository.java
│   └── ProductRepository.java
└── models/
    ├── User.java
    └── Product.java
```

**Package by feature** — organizes code vertically, around *business* capability instead. Top-level folders represent business domains — `users`, `products`, `orders` — with everything related to one feature grouped together:

```
src/
├── users/
│   ├── UserController.java
│   ├── UserService.java
│   ├── UserRepository.java
│   └── User.java
└── products/
    ├── ProductController.java
    ├── ProductService.java
    ├── ProductRepository.java
    └── Product.java
```

This makes it much easier to see an entire business workflow at a glance, rather than jumping between several technical layers scattered across the project.

**Hexagonal architecture** (also called "ports and adapters") — deliberately separates core business logic from everything external to it. Look for a central domain model surrounded by adapters connecting it to the outside world:

```
src/
├── domain/         // Business logic
│   ├── model/
│   └── service/
├── application/    // Use cases, orchestration
│   └── service/
├── ports/          // Interfaces for adapters
│   ├── input/
│   └── output/
└── adapters/       // Technical implementations
    ├── web/
    ├── persistence/
    └── messaging/
```

This pattern emphasizes keeping business rules genuinely isolated from technical implementation details — the `domain` folder shouldn't need to know or care whether data comes from a REST API or a message queue.

**Microservices architecture** — splits functionality across multiple independent services entirely, each with its own codebase and deployment:

```
microservices-system/
├── user-service/
│   ├── controllers/
│   ├── services/
│   ├── repositories/
│   ├── models/
│   └── Dockerfile
├── product-service/
│   ├── controllers/
│   ├── services/
│   ├── repositories/
│   ├── models/
│   └── Dockerfile
├── order-service/
│   ├── controllers/
│   ├── services/
│   ├── repositories/
│   ├── models/
│   └── Dockerfile
├── api-gateway/
│   ├── config/
│   ├── filters/
│   └── routes/
└── shared/
    ├── common-models/
    └── utils/
```

If you're working in a microservices environment, you need to understand *both* your specific service's internal architecture *and* how it fits into the broader ecosystem around it.

## Understand the Execution Flow

**Execution flow** is the actual sequential path a program follows at runtime — every decision, loop, and function call that determines exactly what code runs, and in what order.

With the big picture in hand, it's time to start looking at real code — not writing any yet, just tracing how it flows. This section covers finding application entry points, tracing requests and responses, and locating a system's external dependencies.

### Finding Application Entry Points

Every application has a set of "doors" — entry points — where execution actually begins: a main method, public APIs, web UIs, and more. These doorways are the natural starting point for understanding an application's execution flow.

**Example — a Spring Boot application's main entry point:**

```java
@SpringBootApplication
public class PetClinicApplication {
    public static void main(String[] args) {
        SpringApplication.run(PetClinicApplication.class, args);
    }
}
```

Once you've found an entry point like this, your IDE's debugger becomes genuinely invaluable — set a breakpoint on `run()`, and step through it to see exactly what the framework does behind the scenes.

You can also find entry points by locating public APIs — in this PetClinic example, its REST endpoints:

```java
@GetMapping("/pets/new")
public String initCreationForm(Owner owner, ModelMap model) {
    Pet pet = new Pet();
    owner.addPet(pet);
    return VIEWS_PETS_CREATE_OR_UPDATE_FORM;
}
```

This `@GetMapping` annotation tells you exactly which method runs when a request hits `/pets/new` — giving you a precise, concrete starting point to trace through with your debugger.

**Other common entry points worth knowing:**

| Entry point | What it is |
|---|---|
| **Main/bootstrap methods** | The traditional starting point of execution (e.g., Java's `main()`) |
| **Public APIs/controllers** | Endpoints exposing functionality to external systems |
| **Event handlers/listeners** | Code triggered by specific events |
| **Scheduled tasks/jobs** | Functionality that runs on a fixed schedule |
| **Lifecycle hooks** | Methods called during a component's creation, startup, or shutdown |
| **Plugin/extension points** | Interfaces designed for extending the application |
| **Message consumers** | Code responding to messages from a queue or broker |
| **Command-line argument processors** | Logic handling startup parameters |
| **Database triggers/stored procedures** | Server-side code responding to data changes |

The real skill is knowing which of these "doors" to open first for a given question you're trying to answer.

### Following the Data: Tracing Request Journeys

For web applications and APIs (REST, GraphQL, gRPC), tracing a request's actual journey through the system is one of the best ways to genuinely understand how the code works. Documentation and tests tell you what's *supposed* to happen — following an actual request tells you what *really* happens.

**Request tracing reveals:**

- The real execution path across multiple components and systems
- Every layer actually involved (controllers, services, repositories, and so on)
- Data transformations happening along the way
- Hidden business logic and validation rules
- How errors actually get handled in practice

Here's a practical toolkit for tracing requests, worth trying out to see what fits your own workflow.

#### Browser Developer Tools

For web applications, your browser's built-in dev tools are a great first stop for inspecting a request and its response.

Consider the PetClinic app's "add a new pet" form. Submitting it sends a request to:

```java
@PostMapping("/pets/new")
public String processCreationForm(Owner owner, @Valid Pet pet,
                                    BindingResult result,
                                    RedirectAttributes redirectAttributes) {
    if (StringUtils.hasText(pet.getName()) && pet.isNew() &&
        owner.getPet(pet.getName(), true) != null)
        result.rejectValue("name", "duplicate", "already exists");

    LocalDate currentDate = LocalDate.now();
    if (pet.getBirthDate() != null && pet.getBirthDate().isAfter(currentDate)) {
        result.rejectValue("birthDate", "typeMismatch.birthDate");
    }

    if (result.hasErrors()) {
        return VIEWS_PETS_CREATE_OR_UPDATE_FORM;
    }

    owner.addPet(pet);
    this.owners.save(owner);
    redirectAttributes.addFlashAttribute("message", "New Pet has been Added");
    return "redirect:/owners/{ownerId}";
}
```

Opening your browser's dev tools and checking the **Network** tab while submitting the form lets you inspect the actual `POST` request to `/pets/new` — its headers, the exact payload sent, and the server's response.

#### API Testing Tools

For a simple `GET` request, pasting a URL directly into your browser works fine. For anything else — `POST`, `PUT`, `PATCH`, `DELETE` — you need a dedicated API testing tool: **Postman**, **Insomnia**, or **Bruno** are all solid, widely used options (many also exist as IDE plugins).

These tools let you construct a request directly — headers, authentication, request body, and all — and inspect the response, entirely bypassing the browser UI. This is particularly useful for testing:

- **Required fields and data types** — which fields are mandatory, and in what format (strings, numbers, arrays, etc.)
- **Input validation behavior** — how the API handles invalid data, missing fields, malformed JSON, empty strings, or nulls
- **Authentication and authorization** — confirming protected endpoints properly reject unauthorized requests, and accept valid ones
- **Response structure and status codes** — what comes back on success versus various failure modes (400, 401, 404, 500, etc.)
- **Error message quality** — whether error responses actually help with debugging
- **Performance characteristics** — response times, and any endpoints that seem unusually slow

#### Logging

Logs give you insight into a system's internal behavior — and in production, they're often your *only* available window into what actually happened during a request. Look for log statements like:

```java
log.info("Finding owner with id: {}", ownerId);
```

**Example — an endpoint listing all pets for a given owner:**

```java
@GetMapping("/owners/{id}/pets")
public List<Pet> findAllPets(@PathVariable("id") int ownerId) {
    log.info("Finding owner with id: {}", ownerId);
    Optional<Owner> owner = ownerRepository.findById(ownerId);
    if (owner.isPresent()) {
        var pets = owner.get().getPets();
        log.info("Found {} pets for owner", pets.size());
        return pets;
    }
    return null;
}
```

Calling this endpoint with a valid owner ID produces two log lines — with the `{}` placeholders replaced by the actual values passed in (e.g., `Finding owner with id: 3`, then `Found 2 pets for owner`). Watching logs like this in real time as you exercise the application is a simple, effective way to confirm exactly what path the code actually took.

#### Debugging

Your IDE's debugger is invaluable for stepping through genuinely complex logic. Revisit the pet-creation validation from earlier:

```java
@PostMapping("/pets/new")
public String processCreationForm(Owner owner, @Valid Pet pet,
                                    BindingResult result,
                                    RedirectAttributes redirectAttributes) {
    // Set breakpoint here
    if (StringUtils.hasText(pet.getName()) && pet.isNew()
        && owner.getPet(pet.getName(), true) != null)
        result.rejectValue("name", "duplicate", "already exists");
    // Additional validation...
}
```

Suppose you know the app is rejecting a new pet's name — but you don't know *why*. This `if` condition has three separate parts, all of which must be true simultaneously for the "duplicate name" error to trigger. Setting a breakpoint right here, and stepping through it, lets you directly inspect:

- **What each individual condition evaluates to** — does `StringUtils.hasText(pet.getName())` return `true`? Does `pet.isNew()`?
- **The actual state of your objects** — what pets does this owner already have on file? What's the exact name being checked?
- **What method calls actually return** — what does `owner.getPet(pet.getName(), true)` genuinely come back with?

Debugging like this reveals not just *what path* the code takes, but *why* it takes that path — especially valuable when actual behavior doesn't match your expectations. If the logs say a pet name already exists, but you're confident that's wrong, the debugger lets you verify that claim directly, rather than guessing.

### Locating External Dependencies

Very few real applications run in true isolation — most depend on external services: other APIs, databases, caches, and more. Understanding execution flow means mapping out all of these external dependencies too.

**A good starting point: configuration files.** In a Spring Boot app, `application.properties` or `application.yaml` typically declares exactly what a service depends on:

```properties
# database
spring.datasource.url=jdbc:postgresql://db-server:5432/petclinic
spring.datasource.driver-class-name=org.postgresql.Driver

# external service
payment.service.endpoint=https://payments.example.com/api/v2

# email host
email.service.host=smtp.company.com
```

This one snippet immediately tells you the app depends on a PostgreSQL database, an external payment service, and an email host — three concrete dependencies worth investigating further.

For containerized applications, check the Docker Compose file or Kubernetes manifests to see linked services directly:

```yaml
# In docker-compose.yml
services:
  app:
    image: petclinic:latest
    depends_on:
      - mysql
      - redis
      - kafka
```

Here, `depends_on` tells you this app relies on MySQL, Redis, and Kafka — three more real dependencies to account for as you build your mental model of the system.

## Locating Internal Frameworks and Libraries

One of the trickiest challenges in exploring existing code is running into a **homegrown internal framework** — a custom, in-house solution built to solve a specific problem. These might have been the right call when they were built, but they often create real friction for anyone new trying to explore or modify the codebase.

**Why do teams build internal frameworks instead of reaching for something external?** A few common reasons:

- The team needed functionality that no existing library offered.
- They had specific security or performance requirements an off-the-shelf option couldn't meet.
- The total cost of adopting an external solution was too high.
- A quick fix for a small problem gradually evolved into something much bigger.
- The team simply wasn't aware a suitable existing solution already existed.

> "Meta work is more interesting than real work."
> — Neal Ford, director and software architect at Thoughtworks; author and speaker

Unfortunately, these homegrown solutions often suffer from poor documentation, no access to whoever originally built them, and inconsistent ongoing maintenance.

### Identifying Internal Frameworks

Internal frameworks can be harder to spot than external dependencies, since they're woven directly into the codebase itself. A few telltale signs:

- **Package naming patterns** — company-specific prefixes or generic names, like `com.yourcompany.framework`, `utils.common.core`, or `internal.shared`.
- **Custom base classes** — many classes extending suspiciously generic-sounding base classes like `BaseService`, `AbstractController`, or `CommonEntity`.
- **Custom annotations** — non-standard annotations not part of any well-known framework:

```java
@Entity
@CompanyTable(name = "users", audit = true)
public class User extends BaseCompanyEntity {
    @CompanyField(encrypted = true)
    private String email;
}
```

Here, `@Entity` is a standard JPA annotation you'd recognize immediately — but `@CompanyTable` and `@CompanyField` are clearly custom, company-specific additions layered on top, and `BaseCompanyEntity` is very likely a custom internal base class too.

- **Overly broad utility classes** — things named `AppUtils`, `BusinessHelper`, or `SystemManager`, quietly handling a wide, unrelated grab-bag of responsibilities.
- **Heavy custom configuration** — extensive YAML/XML files or configuration classes that don't resemble any standard library's typical patterns.

### Working with Internal Frameworks

A few practices that help you explore internal frameworks *and* leave them better than you found them:

- **Find real usage examples first.** Search the codebase for how other developers actually use the framework — real, working examples are often far more instructive than any documentation, if it even exists.
- **Look for tests.** Framework code sometimes has surprisingly solid test coverage, precisely because it needs to work correctly across many different scenarios. Tests reveal intended behavior and known edge cases.
- **Document as you learn.** Jot down notes on what the framework does and how to use it — even rough, informal notes genuinely help the next developer.
- **Check version control history.** `git blame` and old commit messages can reveal *why* something was originally built, and how it's evolved since.
- **Evaluate the trade-offs honestly.** Does this internal framework still earn its keep, or has it quietly become a maintenance burden? Sometimes replacing a homegrown solution with a standard library really is the right long-term call.
- **Make incremental improvements.** Apply the **scout rule** (covered later in this chapter) as you go — improving naming, adding a little documentation, or strengthening test coverage each time you touch this code.

## Build Mental Models Incrementally

A **mental model** is your own internal picture of how a system actually works — and building a genuinely solid one takes time and deliberate effort. The single best way to build one is incrementally: rather than trying to grasp an entire e-commerce application at once, pick a single path — the checkout process, say — and build outward from there. Don't let tooling or perfectionism get in your way, either; a rough sketch on a scratch pad is often all you need.

> **A note on AI:** text-based diagramming tools pair nicely with generative AI these days. You can describe a system or process in plain language and ask an AI tool to generate diagram syntax (like Mermaid), then refine that initial draft by hand to match what you're actually seeing in the code.

### Breaking Down Complex Systems

Trying to understand an entire large codebase all at once is overwhelming and inefficient. Instead, focus on breaking it into manageable pieces — and application entry points, covered earlier, are a natural place to start.

**Example — an e-commerce app broken into distinct execution flows:**

- **New customer flow** — a user signs up, becomes a customer, and receives a promotional coupon.
- **Checkout flow** — what happens when a user completes a purchase.
- **Product review flow** — a customer leaves feedback on a purchased product.

Each of these is a manageable, bite-sized piece you can genuinely wrap your head around, one at a time. Once you've traced how a single new review actually gets recorded, and seen the code behind each step, you can start building a real mental model of that one process.

Everyone learns a bit differently — you might prefer building your model around *code structure* rather than *business process*. If the app uses a package-by-feature layout, you could build a model per feature instead. Here's everything related to a shopping cart, for example:

```
src/
├── cart/
│   ├── CartController.java
│   ├── CartService.java
│   ├── CartRepository.java
│   ├── Cart.java
│   ├── CartItem.java
│   └── dto/
│       ├── CartDTO.java
│       └── CartItemDTO.java
```

### Visualizing Your Mental Models

Visualizing relationships in the code helps make a mental model concrete. Chapter 4 covered software modeling and several diagram types in depth — and your visualizations here don't need to be polished; a rough sketch works just as well as a formal UML diagram, whatever level of detail actually helps you.

**A few diagram types worth reaching for, and what each is good for:**

| Diagram type | Best for |
|---|---|
| **Flowcharts** | Sequential processes and decision points |
| **Entity–relationship diagrams** | Clarifying data structures and their relationships |
| **Sequence diagrams** | Showing how components interact over time |
| **Component diagrams** | High-level architecture and dependencies |
| **Mind maps** | Organizing related concepts hierarchically |

**Start simple, and expand as your understanding grows.** For example, sketching the e-commerce checkout flow might start with just the major moving pieces, expressed here in **Mermaid** syntax (a popular Markdown-based diagramming format):

```
graph TD
    A[Shopping Cart] --> B[Checkout Form]
    B --> C[Payment Processing]
    C --> D[Order Confirmation]
```

This produces a simple, four-box flowchart — cart leads to checkout form, which leads to payment processing, which leads to order confirmation. As your understanding deepens, you can expand this into something far more detailed, capturing specific services, database calls, and external integrations:

```
sequenceDiagram
    participant U as User
    participant FE as Frontend
    participant API as API Layer
    participant CS as CartService
    participant PS as PaymentService
    participant OS as OrderService
    participant DB as Database
    participant PP as Payment Provider

    U->>FE: Click "Checkout"
    FE->>API: POST /checkout
    API->>CS: validateCart()
    CS->>DB: getCartItems()
    DB-->>CS: cartItems
    CS-->>API: validation result
    API->>PS: processPayment()
    PS->>PP: authorizeCharge()
    PP-->>PS: authorization
    PS-->>API: payment result
    API->>OS: createOrder()
    OS->>DB: saveOrder()
    DB-->>OS: orderConfirmation
    OS-->>API: order details
    API-->>FE: success response
    FE-->>U: Display confirmation
```

**Reading this diagram:** each `->>` arrow represents a call from one participant to another (solid arrow, "I'm asking you to do something"), while `-->>` represents a response flowing back (dashed arrow, "here's the result"). Reading top to bottom, you can trace the *entire* checkout journey — from the user's click, through the frontend and API layer, into cart validation, payment authorization with an external provider, order creation, and finally back up to a confirmation shown to the user. This single diagram captures a genuinely complex, multi-system interaction in a form that's far easier to hold in your head than the equivalent scattered across a dozen files.

### Gradually Expanding Your Scope

Once individual components make sense, widen your focus step by step:

1. Start with single methods, in isolation.
2. Move to classes and their direct, immediate dependencies.
3. Continue to feature-level flows spanning multiple components.
4. Finally, tackle cross-cutting concerns — security, transaction handling, and similar concerns that touch many parts of the system at once.

This incremental approach avoids cognitive overload while still building toward a genuinely comprehensive understanding. Investing this time pays off directly in better decisions when you eventually start modifying the codebase — and remember, this is an ongoing process; even experienced team members keep refining their own understanding as the system continues to evolve.

## A Sample Process

Over time, you'll develop your own intuition for working with unfamiliar code — but a concrete process helps in the meantime. Christopher Judd teaches the following method in his boot camps:

1. Clone the project from source control.
2. Review the README, coding standards, architecture docs, and any other supporting documentation.
3. Take notes as you go (architecture, build commands, common SQL, industry terms, etc.) — and don't hesitate to share these notes with your teammates.
4. Review the build scripts.
5. Review the project's dependencies.
6. Review the project structure (packages, namespaces, modules, artifacts, etc.).
7. Review the CI/CD pipelines.
8. Install whatever project dependencies you need locally (build tools, runtimes, languages).
9. **From your IDE:**
   a. Run and debug the application.
   b. Add breakpoints on interesting methods, and connect them back to the running application.
   c. Run and debug the unit tests.
   d. Add breakpoints on interesting test-related methods too.
10. **From the command line:**
    a. Build the project artifacts.
    b. Run the unit tests.
    c. Start any required containers (a datastore, for example).
    d. Run the application locally, and confirm ports and URLs behave as expected.

Understanding an existing codebase is inherently iterative — you won't fully grasp everything on your first pass, and that's genuinely normal. Every issue you tackle and every feature you build adds another layer of understanding.

## Making Changes Safely

With a solid process for *understanding* an existing codebase, it's time to cover how to actually *modify* one safely. Existing systems have usually been through countless iterations already — you can't charge in swinging wildly and burning all your energy in round one. "Move fast and break things" has its place in some contexts — this generally isn't one of them.

### Refactoring Safely

Existing codebases often present genuine opportunities to improve readability, performance, and more through refactoring — but this needs a plan, and restraint against trying to do too much at once.

#### Rely on Existing Tests

Tests aren't just useful for understanding a codebase (as covered in Chapter 5) — they're what makes refactoring genuinely *safe*. A comprehensive test suite is your safety net: make a small change, run the tests, and confirm nothing broke elsewhere.

**Example — refactoring `calculateTotal` to separate subtotal calculation from discount application:**

```java
// Before refactoring
public double calculateTotal(Order order) {
    double total = 0;
    for (OrderItem item : order.getItems()) {
        total += item.getPrice() * item.getQuantity();
    }
    if (order.hasDiscount()) {
        total = total * (1 - order.getDiscountRate());
    }
    return total;
}
```

```java
// After refactoring
public double calculateTotal(Order order) {
    double subtotal = calculateSubtotal(order);
    return applyDiscount(subtotal, order);
}

private double calculateSubtotal(Order order) {
    return order.getItems().stream()
            .mapToDouble(item -> item.getPrice() * item.getQuantity())
            .sum();
}

private double applyDiscount(double amount, Order order) {
    return order.hasDiscount()
            ? amount * (1 - order.getDiscountRate())
            : amount;
}
```

**What changed:** the original method mixed two distinct responsibilities — computing a subtotal, and conditionally applying a discount — into one block. The refactored version splits these into two small, independently understandable (and testable) private methods, while `calculateTotal` itself becomes a simple two-line summary of what actually happens. **Crucially: don't modify the tests during this kind of refactor** — the goal is to improve *structure* while preserving *behavior* exactly. Run the associated tests after each incremental step to confirm the observable behavior hasn't shifted at all.

#### Add Tests Before Refactoring

If a codebase lacks adequate coverage for the area you're about to touch, that's your cue to add tests *before* refactoring anything — an approach sometimes called **test-driven refactoring**:

1. Write tests that document the *current* behavior, whatever it happens to be.
2. Confirm these tests pass against the *current* implementation.
3. Refactor the code.
4. Confirm the same tests still pass against the *new* implementation.

**Example — a test documenting existing discount behavior:**

```java
@Test
public void calculateTotalAppliesDiscountWhenOrderHasDiscount() {
    // Given
    OrderItem item1 = new OrderItem("Product1", 10.0, 2);
    OrderItem item2 = new OrderItem("Product2", 15.0, 1);
    Order order = new Order(Arrays.asList(item1, item2), true, 0.1);

    // When
    double total = orderService.calculateTotal(order);

    // Then
    assertEquals(31.5, total, 0.001); // (10*2 + 15*1) * 0.9 = 31.5
}
```

**Walking through the math in the comment:** two units of Product1 at $10 each, plus one unit of Product2 at $15, comes to a $35 subtotal — then a 10% discount (multiplying by `0.9`) brings the final total to $31.50, exactly what the assertion checks. Writing a test like this *before* touching the implementation gives you a concrete, verified baseline of current behavior — and real confidence that your eventual refactor hasn't silently changed it.

> **Important:** when adding tests to existing code, your first goal is to *document* current behavior — not to *change* it. Even if you're confident you've spotted an actual bug, or a clearly better approach, write tests confirming the *current* behavior first. Bug fixes and further refactoring can come afterward, once that safety net is genuinely in place.

### The Scout Rule

The **scout rule** — "always leave the code better than you found it" — borrows from the Boy Scouts of America's camping philosophy: leave a campsite cleaner than you found it.

Applied to code, this means making small, incremental quality improvements every time you touch a file:

- Adding missing documentation
- Improving unclear variable or method names
- Breaking an overly complex method into smaller pieces
- Removing dead code
- Fixing minor bugs you happen to notice along the way

**Example — applying the scout rule to a poorly named, undocumented method:**

```java
// Before applying the Scout Rule
public void prcs(List l) {
    for (int i = 0; i < l.size(); i++) {
        Object o = l.get(i);
        // Process object
        // ...
    }
}
```

```java
// After applying the Scout Rule
/**
 * Processes a list of customer records and updates their status in the database.
 *
 * @param customers The list of customers to process
 */
public void processCustomers(List<Customer> customers) {
    for (Customer customer : customers) {
        // Process customer
        // ...
    }
}
```

**What changed, concretely:** the cryptic method name `prcs` became `processCustomers`; the untyped `List` (and manual index-based loop, pulling out an untyped `Object`) became a properly typed `List<Customer>`, iterated with a clean enhanced for-loop over actual `Customer` objects; and a documentation comment now explains the method's purpose and parameter. None of this changes what the method actually *does* — but it makes the code dramatically easier for the next person (quite possibly you) to understand at a glance.

**A necessary caveat: balance cleanup against the actual task at hand.** In a large codebase, it's entirely possible to spend all your available time polishing code, only to realize the actual bug you set out to fix is still sitting there untouched. Not everything that *could* be improved should be improved *right now*.

**Good candidates for refactoring in the moment:**

- The code you're already working in is genuinely hard to understand.
- You need to add a feature to this exact area anyway.
- You're already fixing a bug here.
- The code is causing real performance problems.
- Multiple developers work in this area frequently.

**Good candidates for deferring refactoring:**

- The code works fine and rarely needs to change.
- You're under real deadline pressure.
- The risk of breaking something outweighs the benefit of cleaning it up.
- The refactor would require sweeping changes well beyond your current task.
- You don't have adequate test coverage to do it safely.

### Small, Reversible Changes

Making incremental, easily reversible changes is the safest way to modify existing code without destabilizing the whole system — a skill that takes continual practice to build. It's tempting to tackle a big, complex problem all at once (and product owners sometimes push for exactly that) — but your actual goal should be smaller modifications that don't put the whole system at risk.

#### Change Management Strategies

A **change management strategy** is a structured approach to proposing, reviewing, implementing, and tracking changes across a team — and when it's built around small, reversible changes, it should prioritize both safety and visibility.

**Practical strategies worth adopting:**

- **Make changes visible.** Document what changed, and briefly why — this doesn't need to be exhaustive, just clear.
- **Build in verification.** Confirm changes work as expected via automated tests, code review, and static analysis.
- **Plan for rollback.** Murphy's Law applies here as much as anywhere — always have a way to revert. Small changes are naturally easier to roll back cleanly than large ones.
- **Monitor effects.** Watch for unintended consequences after a change ships — this is exactly where good observability into your running application really pays off.

#### Small, Testable Increments

Once you're genuinely in the habit of working in small, independently testable increments, modifying existing code becomes noticeably less stressful. The benefits compound:

- Reduces risk by limiting the *scope* of any single change
- Makes testing more focused and effective
- Makes code review far more manageable
- Makes troubleshooting easier if something does go wrong

This sometimes means genuinely pushing back on feature requests that are too large, breaking them into smaller, independently shippable pieces instead. It's genuinely better to ship several small, successful changes than one large change that quietly introduces a bug.

**Example:** suppose you're asked to change how orders get processed. This method is already doing far too much on its own — a clear candidate for careful, incremental refactoring:

```java
// Instead of changing this entire method at once...
public void processOrder(Order order) {
    // Validate order
    // Calculate totals
    // Apply discounts
    // Calculate shipping
    // Apply taxes
    // Process payment
    // Update inventory
    // Send confirmation
}
```

**Rather than rewriting this whole method in one pass, break it into separate, individually testable extractions:**

```java
// 1. First, extract the validation logic
private boolean validateOrder(Order order) {
    // Validation logic here
    return isValid;
}

// 2. Next, extract the totals calculation
private double calculateOrderTotal(Order order) {
    // Total calculation logic here
    return total;
}

// 3. Continue with other extractions, testing after each change
```

Each extraction is its own small, self-contained, independently testable step — validate it, confirm the tests still pass, then move to the next piece, rather than attempting the entire rewrite in one large, hard-to-verify leap.

### Version Control Best Practices

Most projects you join will already have version control set up — but you'll occasionally encounter code that lacks solid practices around it, or need to establish good habits from scratch:

- **Legacy codebases** — older projects with version control technically in place, but no real branching strategy, inconsistent commit messages, massive unmerged branches, or generated files and dependencies that never should have been committed in the first place.
- **Personal or small-scale projects** — it's tempting to skip good version control habits when it's "just you," but building them early prevents real technical debt later, and makes it much easier when the project grows or new people join.
- **Open source contributions** — forking and contributing means understanding and respecting the project's own conventions, while still properly maintaining your own fork.
- **Contractor or consultant work** — you may need to actively establish or improve version control practices that simply weren't a priority before you arrived.

Whatever the scenario, tools like Git are essential for making changes safely — used well, they give you a genuine safety net for experimenting, tracking history, and collaborating, without fear of losing work or breaking something for everyone else.

#### Commit Strategically

Your commit history should tell a coherent story of how the code actually evolved:

- **Make atomic commits.** Each commit should represent one self-contained logical change — easier to understand, review, and revert if needed.
- **Write meaningful commit messages** — explaining both *what* changed, and *why*. Your future self (and every future teammate) will thank you for the context:

  ```
  # Poor commit message
  Fix bug

  # Better commit message
  Fix order calculation when a discount code is applied twice

  The system was double-applying discount codes when customers
  entered them in both mobile and desktop sessions. This fix
  ensures the discount is only applied once per order.
  ```

  Notice how the better message doesn't just say *what* changed — it explains the actual bug's root cause, making it immediately useful to anyone reading the history later, without having to dig through the diff to understand *why* this change was necessary.

- **Commit frequently.** Don't accumulate dozens of unrelated changes before committing — small, frequent, logical commits make it far easier to pinpoint exactly when and where a problem was introduced.

#### Branch Wisely

Branching conventions vary by team and project, but a few principles hold broadly:

- **Create feature branches** to isolate your work until it's genuinely ready for integration — this keeps the main branch stable, and gives you room to freely experiment.
- **Keep branches short-lived.** The longer a branch lives, the further it drifts from the main codebase, making eventual integration progressively harder. Aim to merge within days, not weeks.
- **Rebase before merging**, when appropriate, to work against the most current version of the codebase. **Important:** rebasing rewrites commit history — use it carefully, and generally avoid it on branches other people are actively sharing.

#### Pull Requests and Code Reviews

A **pull request (PR)** is a formal proposal to merge your changes from one branch into another, opening them up for review, discussion, and automated checks before integration. Keep PRs focused and genuinely manageable — a 1,000-line PR touching 20 files is extremely difficult to review well. Aim instead for smaller, focused PRs addressing a single concern.

In a PR description, clearly explain the problem you're solving and how your change addresses it, including links to any relevant tickets or requirements. And remember: **code review is about improving the solution, not critiquing the person who wrote it.** Stay open to suggestions, and genuinely willing to iterate on your own approach based on feedback.

Modifying an existing codebase doesn't require timidity — but it does reward discipline. Refactor safely, backed by tests. Follow the scout rule. Favor small, reversible changes over large, risky ones.

## Wrapping Up

Working effectively with existing code is one of the fundamental skills that separates a *coder* from a genuine *software engineer*. This chapter covered how to understand an unfamiliar codebase — starting with the big picture, working through documentation, and grasping the project's architecture — how to trace actual execution flow through entry points, requests, and external dependencies — how to build a mental model incrementally, breaking down complexity and visualizing relationships as you go — and finally, how to make changes safely through careful refactoring, the scout rule, and solid version control practices.

These aren't optional extras — they're essential to your growth as an engineer. Building something new from scratch is genuinely fun, but the honest reality is that most of your career will involve working inside code someone else wrote. Mastering these techniques means you can confidently contribute to *any* codebase you encounter, regardless of its size or complexity.

## Putting It into Practice

1. **Explore without coding.** Find an open source project that interests you, and spend 30 minutes exploring its codebase without writing a single line of code — focus purely on understanding its structure and organization.

2. **Trace a request end to end.** Pick a web application (PetClinic works well) and trace a complete user journey — from a UI interaction, through the API layer, all the way to a database change — using browser dev tools, logging, and debugging together, in sequence.

3. **Refactor for readability.** Find a genuinely hard-to-understand method in an open source project, and refactor it to improve clarity, without changing its actual behavior.

4. **Add tests to legacy code.** Find a project with thin test coverage, and practice adding tests that document its *existing* behavior.

5. **Build a mental model diagram.** Pick one real business process from a codebase you know, and create a visual representation of it — a hand sketch, flowchart, sequence diagram, or component diagram — showing how the code actually flows. Validate it with a teammate afterward.

6. **Make a complex change incrementally.** Tackle a real, complex change to an existing codebase using small, reversible commits, and get a peer's feedback specifically on your commit strategy.

7. **Apply the scout rule.** In a project you're actively working on, identify three small improvements you can make to genuinely leave the code better than you found it.

8. **Practice safe dependency changes.** Identify an external dependency in a project, and research how to safely upgrade or replace it — including a real impact analysis and a rollback plan.
