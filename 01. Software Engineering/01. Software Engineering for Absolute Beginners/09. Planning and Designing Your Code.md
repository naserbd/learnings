# Planning and Designing Your Code

Knowing your tools is half the battle — but tools alone don't make software great. They make the *process* of building software great. Technically, you could still build everything covered in this book using Notepad and FTP: run MySQL locally instead of in Docker, skip Git in favor of manually saved files like `main.py.2020-01-15`, and run your tests by hand instead of automating them.

But there's one thing you genuinely can't skip: **designing your code properly.**

This chapter covers two things:

1. A basic **Software Development Lifecycle (SDLC)** — the overall flow a project moves through, from planning to production.
2. **Design techniques** — first at a high level (planning a portion of your system), then at a more granular level (how you structure individual classes and objects).

These build on each other: the lifecycle gives you the process, and the design techniques give you the tools to actually execute the design phase well.

## The Software Development Lifecycle (SDLC)

### Why Bother With a Formal Lifecycle?

An SDLC breaks software creation into distinct phases, each with a clear purpose. The work from one phase feeds directly into the next: phase one's output becomes phase two's foundation, phase two's output becomes phase three's foundation, and so on. Within a given phase, you stick to that phase's task — no development work happens outside the development phase, for instance.

Occasionally you'll need to revisit an earlier phase (misunderstandings and misjudgments happen) — but revisiting should be the exception, not the norm.

**The real payoff of following a lifecycle:** it reduces risk and cost by giving structure to an otherwise chaotic process. It tells you where you stand, helps you estimate a realistic finish line, and — importantly — helps guard against a very human problem: **scope creep**, where a project's requirements keep quietly expanding beyond what was originally agreed. Left unchecked, scope creep inflates both timeline and cost.

### A Cautionary Story About Scope Creep

Years ago, the author took on a small contract: a simple, template-based emailing system to send deals and specials to a client's contacts. It sounded straightforward, so the quote was based on a rough phone conversation — no formal requirements were pinned down.

Over time, the client kept adding features, without any adjustment to the timeline or the quote. What started as a week of part-time evening work stretched into two months. Eventually, the client handed over a list of many thousands of contacts and asked for an importer to email them directly. When asked where the list came from, the client admitted he'd found it online — he wasn't asking for a mailing tool, he was asking for a spam tool.

The relationship ended badly. The client felt he wasn't the one doing anything wrong (he was the one sending the emails, after all) — but the author felt like an enabler, and understandably didn't want to be involved. The result: two months of unpaid work, entirely avoidable, caused by never formally pinning down the requirements and never adjusting the quote as the scope grew.

**The takeaway:** this risk exists on projects of every size, from a solo contract to a large corporate initiative. Nailing down requirements up front — and revisiting the quote/timeline whenever scope changes — isn't bureaucratic overhead. It's protection.

### The Seven Phases of a Typical SDLC

A quick but important framing point: "lifecycle" is the operative word. The process never truly ends. Even the final phase — supporting the software — continues for as long as the software exists, and every new feature request during that phase effectively kicks off its own miniature version of this whole cycle. Eventually, as the surrounding technology moves on (language versions drop old compatibility, dependencies get abandoned), even actively maintained software becomes obsolete. That's a natural, if slightly bittersweet, end point for any project.

Here are the seven phases:

**Phase 1: Planning**
Scheduling, team organization, cost analysis, and feasibility analysis. Interestingly, the software engineering team itself isn't always involved at this stage.

**Phase 2: Requirements**
Gathering requirements from stakeholders and breaking them into small, well-defined **units of work**, added to a **backlog**. A good unit of work should be small enough to complete in a short, predictable window — often just a day or two (e.g. "an endpoint that creates a user").

**Phase 3: Design**
Requirements get transformed into concrete specifications — UML diagrams, written documents, or other formats. There's no fixed rule for *how* these specifications should look, as long as they're clear and unambiguous. (This phase is the main focus of the rest of this chapter.)

**Phase 4: Development**
The actual building begins, guided by the units of work from Phase 2 and the specifications from Phase 3. This phase often produces its own, more granular diagrams as developers work out implementation details. Development runs in fixed-length iterations — commonly two weeks — called **sprints**, with each developer assigned an achievable amount of work per sprint.

**Phase 5: Testing (an ongoing concern, not a single phase)**
Testing doesn't wait until the end. Inside each sprint, code gets tested continuously — through unit tests and peer review. A dedicated testing phase still exists before a major release, but no sprint should ever ship untested code. Once a sprint's work is tested and reviewed, relevant stakeholders can perform **user acceptance testing (UAT)**.

**Phase 6: Deployment**
Large, single deployments are typical mainly for brand-new projects. Once a system reaches reasonable maturity, releases tend to become smaller and more frequent — every two weeks is common, and some teams deploy as soon as an individual unit of work is done and tested.

**Phase 7: Support and Maintenance**
Ongoing bug fixes, upgrades, and new feature requests. Each new request effectively spins up its own smaller SDLC, feeding back into this same cycle.

### How These Phases Actually Play Out

It's easy for a newcomer to feel overwhelmed by seven distinct phases — in practice, it's less chaotic than it sounds:

- You'll often only be directly involved in Phases 2 through 6 (Phase 1 may happen entirely without engineering involvement, and if work comes pre-loaded from the backlog, even Phase 2 might already be done).
- Phase 4 (development) typically repeats many times, as batches of work get built, tested, and shipped.
- Everything eventually folds into Phase 7, which becomes the software's ongoing, default state.

**In practice, completing and deploying even a single unit of work is a miniature SDLC of its own** — and the same discipline still applies at that small scale:

- Don't start coding without a design.
- Don't deploy without thorough testing.
- Don't consider your mini-cycle "done" until the work is actually deployed.

**Why deployment specifically matters so much:** an undeployed branch can't be merged into the shared main branch other developers are building on top of. If you let it sit, it drifts out of sync — and even if you diligently keep it updated, every other developer's work in the meantime simply doesn't include what you built. Left long enough, this becomes a real headache to untangle.

### There's No One "Correct" SDLC

Plenty of variations on the SDLC exist across companies, and that's fine — there's no universal template that fits every organization perfectly. The same principle from earlier chapters applies here too: just as object calisthenics and coding standards should only be applied where they genuinely make sense, the same goes for your SDLC. If two phases can reasonably collapse into one for your team's needs, don't keep them separate purely for the sake of "following the rules." Optimize the process to fit your actual situation — don't include steps you don't need.

## Modelling

### Where Modelling Fits Into the SDLC

Modelling generally happens in two places: the **design phase** (Phase 3) and the **development phase** (Phase 4) — and in some companies, these two phases are effectively merged into one. Neither approach is wrong; it depends on what fits your team.

For a brand-new project, it's often worth creating complete models and diagrams before any code is written. But months later, as new features get added, those original diagrams may no longer be sufficient on their own — they'll need updates, or entirely new diagrams.

**How you handle this generally depends on the size of the task:**

- **Small tasks** (a couple of days' work) → modelling happens informally, inside the sprint itself (Phase 4).
- **Large or complex tasks** → it's worth stepping back through Phases 2 and 3 properly: re-gathering requirements, creating fresh units of work, and producing real design documentation before development begins.

### Why Bother Creating Diagrams and Models at All?

You'll rarely diagram an *entire* system in one go — instead, you'll build several small, focused diagrams that together describe the whole. This keeps things readable and manageable.

It's tempting to skip modelling entirely and just start coding directly from a verbal description of the requirements. Sometimes this works out fine — especially for developers confident they already understand the problem deeply. But the risk is real: code built this way can end up working *technically*, while fitting awkwardly into the rest of the system, or blocking future expansion without a costly refactor.

**Modelling gives you two concrete benefits:**

1. **A chance to verify your design before committing to it in code** — forcing you to actually think through how components will interact, before that thinking gets baked into hard-to-change code.
2. **Documentation for the future** — for the next developer who joins the project, or for *you*, looking back at your own decisions 12 months from now, trying to remember why you built something a particular way.

## High-Level Models and Diagrams

High-level diagrams describe a system (or part of one) without getting into actual class design — they show the big picture: how data flows, and what paths execution can take. This section covers four types, all using **UML** (Unified Modelling Language) notation:

- Activity diagrams
- Use case diagrams
- Swimlanes
- Database diagrams (ERDs)

### Activity Diagrams

Activity diagrams are simple to create and probably the most commonly used diagram type, thanks to their small, powerful set of shapes. They can represent branching logic, decisions, repeated steps, and even concurrent (simultaneous) execution paths.

*(A quick note on terminology: at this high level, it's best to avoid programming-specific words like "if statement" or "loop" — though conceptually, that's often exactly what these symbols end up representing once you get to code.)*

**Symbols used:**

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Black dot | Start of the flow |
| Ellipse | An action being taken |
| Diamond | A decision point |
| Horizontal black bar | Splitting or joining concurrent (simultaneous) activities |
| Arrow | Links one step to the next |
| Arrow pointing backward | Represents a repeated step (iteration) |
| Black dot inside a circle | End of the flow |

**Example: modelling a card payment flow.**

To complete a payment: the user selects a card, enters a PIN (retrying if it's wrong), and the system needs to confirm both sufficient account balance *and* that the daily spending limit isn't exceeded — checked concurrently. If either check fails, the user can pick a different card; otherwise, the payment is processed and the flow ends.

Walking through this as an activity diagram: start (black dot) → user selects a card → user enters a PIN → if incorrect, loop back to PIN entry → if correct, run the balance check and spend-limit check concurrently (shown with the horizontal split/join bars) → if funds are insufficient, allow selecting another card → otherwise, process the payment → end (black dot inside a circle).

This is a simplified version of a real transaction flow, but it demonstrates the idea well. As with most diagrams, you wouldn't diagram an entire payment system this way — you'd break it into several diagrams, each covering a focused piece.

### Use Case Diagrams

A use case diagram models the actions an **actor** — not necessarily a human; it could be an external automated system — can take within your system. It focuses on *what* can happen, not *how* it's technically achieved, which makes it especially useful for pinning down requirements and communicating with non-technical stakeholders.

**A key rule of thumb: stay focused on one specific flow.** If you're diagramming "send an email," don't also diagram "log in" — that's a separate use case entirely. Mixing unrelated flows into one diagram (like combining "upload a payment file" with "log in" and "reset password") muddies what should be a clean, focused picture.

**Example, described in words first:** *"As a financial manager, I need to upload a CSV file of payments, confirm the entries, run a fraud check, and — if everything looks fine — flag the entries as payable. From there, it gets authorized, and the accountant releases the funds."*

Turning that into a use case diagram (actors: **Financial Manager**, **Accountant**; actions: upload CSV → confirm entries → run fraud check → flag as payable → authorize → release funds) makes the flow far easier to absorb at a glance than the paragraph above — though having both the diagram *and* the written description together is ideal.

**What to avoid:** cramming unrelated actions like "log in" or "reset password" into a use case that's really about processing payments. Those are their own, separate use cases.

### Swimlanes

Swimlane diagrams show a process broken into parallel "lanes," each belonging to a distinct entity — a person, a system, or a subsystem — making it easy to see exactly who (or what) is responsible for each step, and how the pieces hand off to one another. They're particularly good at revealing *inefficiencies* in an existing process, which is valuable when redesigning a system for the better.

**Example: the finance manager's CSV upload process, as swimlanes.**

- **Finance Manager lane:** uploads the CSV file to an FTP server.
- **System lane:** a scheduled job (**cron**) checks periodically for new files. If none are found, nothing changes. If a file is found, it gets imported, and the manager is notified.
- **Finance Manager lane:** performs fraud checks on the imported data.
- **Accountant lane:** gets notified, and releases the funds — marking the end of the process.

Reading this left-to-right (or top-to-bottom, depending on layout) makes the handoffs between people and systems immediately clear — something a plain written description struggles to convey as efficiently.

### Database Diagrams (ERDs)

Database diagrams — also called **Entity Relationship Diagrams (ERDs)** — model the relationships between your data tables. A few notation styles exist (Chen notation, crow's foot notation), but this book uses **UML notation**, which tends to be the most immediately readable — you can look at the connecting line between two tables and understand the relationship without needing to recall what a specific symbol means.

**The basic shape:** each table is drawn as a box, listing its name, primary key, other columns, and any foreign keys. Boxes are connected by lines representing relationships — for example, a user might have one address, but many phone numbers.

**Reading relationship notation:**

| Notation | Meaning |
|---|---|
| A plain line | One-to-one relationship |
| `1` on the line | A *mandatory* one-to-one relationship |
| `0..1` | Zero-or-one relationship |
| `1..*` | One-or-many relationship |

**Example — a one-to-zero-or-many relationship:** a `User` connects to `Address` with a `1..*`-style notation on the `Address` end, meaning a user may have zero or many addresses.

**Example — a many-to-many relationship:** a `User` connects to `Country`, where a user can be linked to zero or more countries, and a country can be linked to zero or more users — both ends of the relationship allow multiples.

The good news: **modelling** a database this way is genuinely the easy part. As covered in the databases chapter, **normalizing** it properly is where the real thinking happens.

## Low-Level Models

Now we shift from the big picture down to the actual building blocks: how you design individual classes so your code scales well and stays understandable. This section covers:

- Value objects vs. entity objects
- Cohesion and coupling
- The SOLID principles
- DRY (Don't Repeat Yourself)
- Composition

### Types of Objects: Value Objects vs. Entity Objects

**Entity objects** have their own identity within the system — something like a `User`, typically represented as a row in a database.

**Value objects** don't have an identity of their own — they exist *as part of* an entity, but still carry their own internal rules, methods, and attributes. A classic example: a `Password`. It doesn't make sense as a standalone "thing" in your system, but it clearly has its own behavior (validating complexity, checking for reuse) that doesn't really belong inside `User` itself.

An important convention: **value objects generally shouldn't be modified once created** (even though nothing technically stops you) — doing so risks subtle bugs. Entity objects, by contrast, can have their attributes updated freely.

**Applying this idea:** instead of storing a vehicle's mileage as a plain number directly on a `Vehicle` class — and making `Vehicle` responsible for figuring out whether a service is due — pull that responsibility out into dedicated value objects.

```python
class Mileage:
    def __init__(self, previous_miles: int, current_miles: int):
        if current_miles < 0 or current_miles < previous_miles or previous_miles < 0:
            raise Exception('Incorrect miles entered.')
        self.current_miles = current_miles
        self.previous_miles = previous_miles

    def miles_current(self):
        return self.current_miles

    def miles_previous(self):
        return self.previous_miles


class Service:
    SERVICE_MILES = 1000

    def __init__(self, mileage: Mileage):
        self.mileage = mileage

    def must_service(self):
        return self.mileage.miles_current() - self.mileage.miles_previous() > self.SERVICE_MILES


class Vehicle:
    def __init__(self, vehicle_type: str, numberplate: str, mileage: Mileage):
        self.vehicle_type = vehicle_type
        self.numberplate = numberplate
        self.mileage = mileage

    def time_for_service(self):
        service = Service(self.mileage)
        return service.must_service()


mileage = Mileage(1000, 2900)
vehicle = Vehicle('Ford', 'CAM123456', mileage)
print(vehicle.time_for_service())
```

**What's happening here:** `Vehicle` no longer needs to know *how* to calculate mileage differences or service intervals — it delegates that entirely to `Mileage` and `Service`. This keeps each class focused and reusable. (As you'll see in the next section, though, this particular version still has a design problem worth fixing.)

### Cohesion and Coupling

These two concepts describe how interlinked your classes are, and how focused each one is on doing just one thing.

#### Coupling

**Coupling** measures how dependent your classes are on the specific, concrete presence of other classes. If a class needs 15 other classes just to function, that's often a sign it's doing too much — or that responsibility has been scattered awkwardly across too many small pieces.

- **Loose coupling** — low dependency between classes. Generally good.
- **Tight coupling** — high dependency between classes. Generally something to minimize.

**Looking back at the `Vehicle` example above:** `time_for_service()` directly creates a `Service` object itself — which means `Vehicle` is now responsible not just for its own job, but for knowing how to construct a `Service`. On top of that, `Service` only exists to lean on `Mileage`'s methods — without `Mileage`, `Service` is useless. That's three classes, all tightly bound together.

**A better-decoupled version:**

```python
class AbstractService:
    SERVICE_MILES = 0
    TYPE = ''

    def __init__(self, previous_miles, current_miles):
        if current_miles < 0 or current_miles < previous_miles or previous_miles < 0:
            raise Exception('Incorrect miles entered.')
        self.previous_miles = previous_miles
        self.current_miles = current_miles

    def must_service(self):
        print('Calculating ' + self.TYPE + ' service mileage')
        return self.current_miles - self.previous_miles > self.SERVICE_MILES


class Service(AbstractService):
    SERVICE_MILES = 1000
    TYPE = 'normal'


class MajorService(AbstractService):
    SERVICE_MILES = 10000
    TYPE = 'major'


class Vehicle:
    def __init__(self, vehicle_type: str, numberplate: str):
        self.vehicle_type = vehicle_type
        self.numberplate = numberplate

    def time_for_service(self, serviceObject: AbstractService):
        return serviceObject.must_service()


vehicle = Vehicle('Ford', 'CAM123456')
print(vehicle.time_for_service(MajorService(1000, 20000)))
print(vehicle.time_for_service(Service(400, 453)))
```

**What changed, and why it matters:**

- `Mileage` is gone entirely — `Vehicle` was never actually interested in mileage itself, only in whether a service was due, so that responsibility moves fully into the service classes.
- A new `AbstractService` parent class defines the shared shape (`must_service()`, `SERVICE_MILES`, `TYPE`) that `Service` and `MajorService` both build on, each only needing to override the two class attributes that differ.
- `Vehicle.time_for_service()` now simply *accepts* a service object as a parameter, rather than constructing one itself. `Vehicle` is handed whatever service instance the calling code decides to create — it no longer needs to know how to build one.
- Adding a brand-new service type (say, a `MinorService`) later requires zero changes to `Vehicle` at all — just a new subclass of `AbstractService`.

**Worth remembering:** coupling never fully disappears — some dependency between classes is inevitable and necessary. The goal isn't zero coupling; it's keeping it as low as reasonably possible.

#### Cohesion

**Cohesion** describes how tightly focused a single class's own responsibilities are. A class representing a shopping basket shouldn't also handle user login — those are two separate concerns, deserving two separate classes (`ShoppingBasket` and `User`).

A useful gut-check: does every function in your class actually use the class's own attributes? If a function doesn't touch anything belonging to the class it's in, that's a sign it might belong somewhere else entirely.

**Worth actively avoiding:** the old "helper" class pattern — a grab-bag object stuffed with unrelated utility functions (string trimming, searching, flattening arrays, and so on). This pattern has thankfully become less common, and for good reason: it's usually a symptom of insufficient upfront design, not a legitimate organizing principle.

### SOLID

**SOLID** is an acronym for five principles that, applied well, lead to better-structured software:

- **S**ingle Responsibility Principle
- **O**pen/Closed Principle
- **L**iskov Substitution Principle
- **I**nterface Segregation Principle
- **D**ependency Inversion Principle

#### Single Responsibility Principle

This is essentially the same idea as cohesion: a function or class should have exactly **one reason to change**. If a class only does one clearly defined job, this naturally follows.

#### Open/Closed Principle

A class should be **open to extension, but closed to modification** — meaning you should be able to add new behavior without editing the class's existing, already-working code.

**Here's what *violating* this principle looks like** — cramming a new service type directly into the existing `Service` class:

```python
class Service:
    SERVICE_MILES = 1000
    MAJOR_SERVICE_MILES = 10000

    def must_service(self, serviceType):
        print('Calculating service mileage')
        if serviceType == 'normal':
            return self.current_miles - self.previous_miles > self.SERVICE_MILES
        return self.current_miles - self.previous_miles > self.MAJOR_SERVICE_MILES
```

*(Note: the original version of this example contained a typo — `return return` — which doesn't run. The corrected version above shows what it should look like.)*

This might look like less code overall, and it does work — but every time a new service type comes along (say, a "minor" service), you're forced to reach back into this same function and modify it further:

```python
class Service:
    MINOR_SERVICE_MILES = 500
    NORMAL_SERVICE_MILES = 1000
    MAJOR_SERVICE_MILES = 10000

    def must_service(self, serviceType):
        print('Calculating service mileage')
        if serviceType == 'normal':
            return self.current_miles - self.previous_miles > self.NORMAL_SERVICE_MILES
        elif serviceType == 'minor':
            return self.current_miles - self.previous_miles > self.MINOR_SERVICE_MILES
        else:
            return self.current_miles - self.previous_miles > self.MAJOR_SERVICE_MILES
```

*(Again, the original example had syntax errors — `elseif` instead of Python's `elif`, missing colons, and more `return return` typos — corrected here.)*

Every new service type makes this one function bigger and more tangled — exactly what the open/closed principle warns against: the function keeps needing **modification**.

**The fix — matching the decoupled `AbstractService` design from earlier:**

```python
class MinorService(AbstractService):
    SERVICE_MILES = 500
    TYPE = 'minor'
```

Adding a new service type now means writing a small, self-contained new subclass — the existing `AbstractService`, `Service`, and `MajorService` code is never touched. That's the principle in action: **open** for this kind of extension, **closed** against needing modification.

#### Liskov Substitution Principle

If class `B` extends class `A`, you should be able to swap a `B` object in anywhere an `A` object is expected, without the system breaking. "Breaking" here doesn't mean the *output* differs (that's expected, since subclasses can behave differently) — it means the program actually malfunctions or takes a completely wrong path because of the swap.

**Here's a violation of this principle:**

```python
class MinorService(AbstractService):
    SERVICE_MILES = 100
    TYPE = 'major'

    def must_service(self):
        print('Calculating ' + self.TYPE + ' service mileage')
        return str(self.current_miles - self.previous_miles > self.SERVICE_MILES)


if vehicle.time_for_service(MinorService(1000, 20000)) == True:
    print('You are due for a minor service')
```

*(The original example also had a stray unclosed quote in the `print()` call — fixed above.)*

**The problem:** every other `AbstractService` subclass returns a **boolean** (`True`/`False`) from `must_service()`. This one returns a **string** (`"True"` or `"False"`) instead, because of the `str(...)` wrapper. That breaks the `if vehicle.time_for_service(...) == True:` check silently — a string can never equal the boolean `True` in Python, so this check will always evaluate to `False`, even when a service genuinely is due. Swapping `MinorService` in for another service type has changed how the *system* behaves, not just what value comes back — that's the substitution principle being violated.

**How to prevent this:** make the expected contract explicit. Adding a return type hint to the parent method documents exactly what every subclass is expected to return:

```python
def must_service(self) -> bool:
    print('Calculating ' + self.TYPE + ' service mileage')
    return self.current_miles - self.previous_miles > self.SERVICE_MILES
```

This won't force correctness at runtime in Python, but it makes the expectation unmistakable to any developer extending the class — a strong signal that returning a string instead of a boolean is a mistake.

#### Interface Segregation Principle

No class should be forced to implement methods it doesn't actually need. This is enforced through **interfaces** — much like parent classes, but containing no actual logic, only a contract (a required set of method names and return types) that any implementing class must fulfill.

**A violation of this principle:**

```python
import abc

"""
This is our interface
"""
class ActionsInterface(abc.ABC):
    @abc.abstractmethod
    def eat(self) -> str:
        pass

    @abc.abstractmethod
    def tree(self) -> str:
        pass


"""
This is the class implementing the interface
"""
class Human(ActionsInterface):
    def eat(self) -> str:
        return 'I am eating'


human = Human()
print(human.eat())
```

*(Uses Python's `abc` — Abstract Base Class — module to define the interface. `pass` inside a method body just means "do nothing here.")*

**The problem:** a `Human` can meaningfully `eat()`, but "tree" isn't a sensible action for a human to implement at all — yet `ActionsInterface` forces every implementing class to define both methods. As written, this code won't even run, since `Human` never implements the required `tree()` method.

**The fix — split into smaller, more focused interfaces:**

```python
import abc

"""
This is our actions interface
"""
class ActionsInterface(abc.ABC):
    @abc.abstractmethod
    def eat(self) -> str:
        pass


"""
This is our botany interface
"""
class BotanyInterface(abc.ABC):
    @abc.abstractmethod
    def grow(self) -> str:
        pass


"""
This is the class implementing the interface
"""
class Human(ActionsInterface):
    def eat(self) -> str:
        return 'I am eating'


human = Human()
print(human.eat())
```

*(Note: the original book example accidentally reused `eat()` as the method name inside `BotanyInterface` too — renamed here to `grow()`, since a botany-related interface wouldn't logically share the same method as the actions interface.)*

**The takeaway:** favor several small, focused interfaces over one large interface bundling unrelated responsibilities together. A class should only ever need to implement what's actually relevant to it.

#### Dependency Inversion Principle

Higher-level classes (the ones orchestrating behavior) shouldn't depend on the concrete details of lower-level classes — they should depend on **abstractions** instead. You've already seen this principle in action:

```python
class Vehicle:
    def __init__(self, vehicle_type: str, numberplate: str):
        self.vehicle_type = vehicle_type
        self.numberplate = numberplate

    def time_for_service(self, serviceObject: AbstractService):
        return serviceObject.must_service()
```

`time_for_service()` doesn't know or care whether it's been handed a `Service`, `MajorService`, or `MinorService` — it only cares that whatever it receives is a subclass of `AbstractService`, and therefore has a `must_service()` method it can call. This is sometimes called **duck typing**: if it has the right method, it's good enough to use, regardless of its specific type.

This decouples `Vehicle` from the implementation details of any specific service type, making the whole system easier to extend and reuse.

### DRY — Don't Repeat Yourself

**DRY** is simple to state: every piece of knowledge in your system should exist in exactly **one** authoritative place. When the same fact or logic is duplicated across multiple locations, you risk one copy drifting out of sync with the others — leading a future developer to unknowingly use stale, incorrect information.

**A real example:** South Africa's VAT (sales tax) rate changed at one point while the author was working on a financial system built heavily around VAT calculations. Because the rate had been defined as a single constant, referenced everywhere it was needed, updating it required changing exactly **one line of code** — even though it was used in hundreds of places throughout the system.

### Composition

**Composition** is often framed as the opposite of inheritance — not better or worse, just a different tool, each suited to different situations. Instead of a class *inheriting* the internal details of other classes, composition means a class is *built from* other objects, referencing them directly.

```python
class DB:
    def connect(self):
        print('DB connect class')


class LogFiles:
    def write(self):
        print('LogFiles connect class')


# Poor design: inheriting from DB and LogFiles purely to "gain access"
# to their functionality
class InheritanceAccess(LogFiles, DB):
    def __init__(self):
        print('Inheritance')


# Better design: composition — this class HAS a DB and a LogFiles,
# rather than incorrectly claiming to BE one
class CompositionAccess():
    def __init__(self, db, log_files):
        print('Composition')


inheritance = InheritanceAccess()
composition = CompositionAccess(DB, LogFiles)
```

**What's wrong with the inheritance version:** `InheritanceAccess` technically becomes "a `DB`" and "a `LogFiles`" (an **is-a** relationship) purely because it wants to *use* database and logging functionality. That's a semantically wrong relationship — an access-control class isn't fundamentally a type of database connection or a type of log writer — and this pattern makes you far more likely to violate the Liskov substitution principle down the line, since the "parent" classes weren't really designed to be inherited from in the first place.

**Why composition is the better fit here:** `CompositionAccess` instead **has a** `DB` and **has a** `LogFiles` — it depends on them, without pretending to *be* one of them. This is both more accurate to the real relationship, and easier to change later (swapping in a different database or logging implementation doesn't require touching an inheritance chain).

## Summary

Sticking to the ideas in this chapter — a sensible SDLC, proper modelling, and disciplined object design — will put you in a strong position. None of it clicks immediately; recognizing a SOLID violation on sight, or knowing exactly what belongs in a diagram, takes real practice and plenty of mistakes along the way.

The best approach is simply consistency: take every piece of code you write seriously, and treat design and planning as a genuine, ongoing habit — not a box to check once and forget. Over time, that habit sharpens your instincts, until spotting a design mistake in your own code (or in someone else's, during review) starts to feel less like effortful analysis and more like second nature.
