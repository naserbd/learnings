# Chapter 13: Mastering Soft Skills in the Tech World

> "When dealing with people, remember you are not dealing with creatures of logic, but creatures of emotion."
> — Dale Carnegie, *How to Win Friends & Influence People*

Every skill you learn has a shelf life — worth remembering when deciding how to spend your limited time. Technology never sits still: APIs evolve and get replaced, yesterday's best practice gets quietly obsoleted by a new language feature. If it feels like something becomes irrelevant right around the time you finally understand it, you're not imagining things — and you're far from alone in that experience.

However you found your way into software engineering, you probably focused heavily on technical skills at first — understandably, since it's hard to write code without understanding the language you're writing it in. Early career growth tends to center on expanding that technical toolkit: more frameworks, cloud provider proficiency, staying current with your language of choice.

**But one set of skills will genuinely last your entire career: the soft skills many engineers tend to overlook.** Learning to work with others and communicate clearly matters just as much to your success as mastering the next language or framework. Human beings simply don't change nearly as fast as technology does — it takes millennia to meaningfully "update" how people think and interact. That's exactly why soft skills never really go out of date. Learning to communicate effectively, influence and work with others, and manage your own time pays off across your *entire* career, not just this quarter's sprint.

Time invested in a skill that pays dividends for 30, 40, even 50 years is a genuinely excellent return. You still need to keep your technical toolkit current, of course — but to truly set yourself apart, don't neglect the more evergreen skills sitting right alongside it. It can feel like a lot at first, but developing strong soft skills mostly comes down to building a handful of good habits. Let's get into it.

## Collaborative Communication

Some people enter tech expecting it to spare them the messier parts of human interaction. The stereotype of the introverted, solitary developer traces back to the earliest days of computing, when projects were smaller and often driven by a single person (go far enough back, and "computer" simply meant a person doing calculations by hand). Companies weren't yet running armies of engineers across multimillion-line codebases. Today, thriving in this career genuinely requires mastering the art of communication.

That doesn't mean you need to become an extrovert — plenty of accomplished conference speakers are themselves introverts. It may take more deliberate effort for some people than others, but software is fundamentally a team sport. Given the sheer scale of modern applications, the "lone wolf" developer era is over — no one person can hold an entire modern codebase in their head anymore; it genuinely takes a team, working together.

**Even how teams physically work has evolved.** Instead of isolated cubicles, many teams now work together in shared project rooms — great for collaboration, but genuinely exhausting for introverts. Some developers deliberately block out solo time on their calendar specifically to recharge after heavy collaborative stretches. That might mean a real conversation with your manager — but don't hesitate to advocate clearly for what you actually need to do your best work.

Software is a collaborative endeavor, requiring a real range of techniques to work effectively alongside a team. As Kent Beck once put it: "Software design is a human process…done by humans for humans." It might not come as naturally as picking up a new language — but mastering technical communication is a genuinely learnable skill. Communication involves far more than the words leaving your mouth: it means picking the *right channel*, navigating what we'll call "enterprise operator," and learning to communicate effectively up, down, and across your organization.

### Communication Channels

You won't just communicate through a programming language — you have no shortage of channels to choose from: warm or cold, personal or impersonal, high-touch or low-touch. Some leave a durable written record; others offer a bit more plausible deniability. The real challenge is choosing the right method for the right moment — easier said than done.

**A few real, common scenarios where the *record* of a communication genuinely matters:** a critical security vulnerability that forces you to upgrade a key component — making sure everyone genuinely understands the problem and the fix can be the difference between a smooth patch and a crippling breach.

**Choosing a channel also means genuinely knowing your audience.** Some people live in their inbox; others haven't opened email in months. Some organizations run entirely on a chat tool; others insist everything important happens face-to-face in a meeting. Understanding how information actually flows in your specific environment will guide which channel makes sense, situation by situation.

#### Messaging

Regardless of your company's stance on remote work, you'll spend real time in a corporate messaging tool — Slack, Microsoft Teams, Google Chat. These tools let you send quick, asynchronous messages to individuals or groups, often bundling in files, code snippets, images, or GIFs. Messaging tends to be informal, and genuinely excellent for quick questions or a heads-up on an issue. Its asynchronous nature is one of its biggest strengths — you can ping a teammate knowing they'll respond whenever it's actually convenient for them.

Depending on how flat your org chart is, messaging is generally appropriate for your immediate team — whether it extends comfortably to senior leadership varies quite a bit by culture. Even when your whole team sits in the same room, messaging someone right next to you is genuinely common — sometimes to keep noise down, sometimes to avoid interrupting deep focus. It's also a handy way to offload a small detail from your short-term memory — jotting down "this function needs refactoring in the next commit" before you forget it entirely.

**Because messaging is asynchronous, don't expect an immediate response.** Setting team norms around response expectations can help — but stay mindful of people's time. Even a "quick question" can genuinely derail someone's whole morning; pay attention to notification settings, focus-time blocks, and calendar entries before firing off a message.

Soft skills, again, mostly boil down to good habits. If you're swamped and someone messages you a question, try to respond promptly anyway — even if it's just "swamped today, can chat later this week." Apply the Golden Rule from Chapter 1: treating others the way you'd want to be treated is one of the simplest, most reliably effective things you can do for your own career. Promotions rarely come down to a pure formula — they often reflect how you're genuinely perceived across the organization. What people say about you when you're *not* in the room matters, and often decides the outcome.

**Assume anything you type into a corporate messaging tool is logged, retained, and potentially retrievable later** — regardless of your company's official retention policy. It's generally not the venue for workshopping a sarcastic joke about your standup material. And remember: tone genuinely doesn't always survive translation into text. Something written as a joke can easily land as a literal, serious statement to the person reading it — it may sound completely different once it's outside your own head. Err on the side of caution.

#### Meetings

Surveys consistently show developers spend a significant chunk of their week in meetings — and unsurprisingly, "too many meetings" is a perennial top-five answer whenever you ask engineers what they dislike most about the job. That said, a meeting is genuinely sometimes the fastest path to resolving something, or communicating a new initiative clearly.

Meetings are synchronous and ephemeral (though often recorded, with slides shared afterward). They range enormously in purpose — a morning standup, a 1:1 with your manager, an all-hands covering quarterly results. They can be the fastest route to a decision — though people can still walk away with genuinely different understandings of what was actually decided.

> ### Story: An Hour Can Cost You Thousands
>
> *Told by Nate.*
>
> For part of my career, I sat in a standing two-hour meeting every Thursday morning. A glance at the standard attendee list showed more than 200 names — all senior technologists across the company. A rough back-of-envelope calculation shows this meeting routinely cost the organization thousands of dollars a week in salaried time.
>
> But it was almost certainly worth it. The meeting covered environment changes — application deployments, new infrastructure coming online, and so on. Keeping everyone genuinely informed minimized disruptions, and let people flag a potential impact before it became a real problem.
>
> It also helped enormously that this meeting was genuinely well-run — the organizer circulated a detailed agenda every single week, letting people step in and out as their own agenda items came up rather than sitting through the entire two hours regardless. **A costly meeting isn't automatically a wasteful one.**

At some point, you'll inevitably end up double- or triple-booked. Sometimes a meeting invite goes out to an overly broad distribution list, or your role means you get looped in by default, without anyone specifically requiring your input. Your time genuinely has value — it's completely reasonable to be deliberate about which meetings you actually attend. Don't hesitate to ask the organizer directly what your role is expected to be, or consult your manager for guidance if you're unsure.

**An agenda is a bare minimum courtesy for any meeting** — and if one's missing, simply asking what you're actually there to contribute can clarify things fast. What's the actual goal here? Do you need to be present the *entire* time? Would this genuinely have worked as an email instead?

**Eventually, you'll find yourself in "that" meeting** — the one with the CTO, a major client, or some other genuinely important person. Often a sign of real career growth, but these can be intimidating. Don't hesitate to ask the organizer questions beforehand — if it's a recurring meeting, ask regular attendees for context on how it typically flows. Is it a genuine collaboration session where input is wanted, or is it mostly about receiving marching orders? Is spontaneous input welcome, or is there an unspoken "wait to be called on" convention? Senior leaders sometimes carry unwritten expectations that aren't obvious from the outside — without a bit of inside context, you can inadvertently leave a poor impression on exactly the person who'll weigh in on your next promotion.

Recruiting an ally — your manager, or a senior technical colleague — genuinely helps here, especially the first time you're interacting directly with leadership. Sometimes just having one familiar, friendly face in the room is enough to steady your nerves. That ally can also help you read the room afterward if your remarks didn't quite land the way you hoped, and offer honest feedback for next time.

**Do your homework before the meeting.** If there are materials to review, actually block time to read them beforehand. Whether you're in person or on a video call, pay real attention to nonverbal cues — furrowed brows, crossed arms, genuine smiles. Try to identify the real power structure of the room: who's the actual decision maker? Are they even present, or are they external to the meeting entirely?

**Virtual meetings have their own etiquette.** Depending on your team's culture, there may be unspoken expectations around cameras or a tidy visible background. Invest in a genuinely good headset, and test any new one before jumping into a call with your CTO — many headsets let you tune microphone sensitivity, so dial it in ahead of time. And mute yourself when you're not speaking, especially while eating — few things are as distracting as hearing someone finish their lunch mid-meeting.

**Back-channel messaging during a meeting can genuinely help** — a clarifying question, or a comment injected without interrupting the speaker, is common practice. Sometimes it's even how you assert a point in a meeting where speaking up directly feels harder. And know exactly who's actually in the room — is it purely internal IT staff? Are customers present? Senior leadership? Never assume; modern video conferencing has mostly eliminated the truly silent, anonymous lurker, but it's still worth double-checking the attendee list.

**You'll also be the one running meetings, eventually.** Practice good meeting hygiene: have an agenda, keep attendance limited to those who genuinely need to be there, and ask a colleague to take notes if you can. The default 60-minute calendar block isn't a law of physics — meetings famously expand to fill whatever container you give them (how many times have you heard "this shouldn't take the whole hour" — and then it did?). Constraints can be genuinely freeing: booking 30 minutes forces real focus. And if you booked an hour but wrapped everything in 30 minutes, give people their time back rather than padding the remainder with filler.

> **Most calendar tools let you change the default meeting length.** Try something a little unconventional — 50 minutes instead of 60 for longer meetings, 25 instead of 30 for shorter ones. Consider starting meetings 5–10 minutes past the usual top or bottom of the hour, giving people real travel time between back-to-back calls. This sharpens everyone's focus, and gives attendees a genuine chance to grab coffee or use the restroom before the next thing starts. Some organizations change these defaults org-wide — but there's nothing stopping you from doing it locally, just for the meetings you personally organize.

**Be respectful of everyone's time by starting on schedule.** Meetings will inevitably run long sometimes, or someone will need a quick break — but do your best to keep things on track. Be mindful of lead time too — if you're asking someone to review a complex design, give them genuine time to actually look it over first. If you're booking meeting rooms across different office locations, check in with local attendees about what's actually convenient nearby — calendar systems aren't always accurate about physical resources.

As the organizer, actively work to keep things on schedule — gently curtail tangents, and push genuinely deep discussions to a dedicated follow-up rather than letting them consume the whole room's time. Make a point of circling back to quieter attendees, giving everyone a real opportunity to contribute. Meetings may never become your favorite part of the job — but unless you're a team of exactly one, they're simply unavoidable. That doesn't mean they have to be pure misery.

### Presentations

Ask people what they genuinely fear, and public speaking comes up constantly. Plenty of people hate presenting — but growing your career will eventually require delivering talks of some kind. You don't need to become a fixture on the conference circuit, but you do need genuine comfort presenting to different audiences.

Presentations take many forms, and aren't always hour-long affairs. Keep a short, one-minute "elevator pitch" ready to go — you never know when an unplanned, brief moment with a senior leader will present itself. Shorter talks are often genuinely *harder*, not easier — don't bury the actual point; focus tightly on the single key thing you need to land.

You might be asked for a short, 5–10 minute update in front of an audience beyond your immediate project team — an engineering-wide status update, a talk to the architecture team, or a briefing for the CTO's direct reports. Shaping your message to genuinely resonate with a given audience is a core skill here — you may need to adjust content substantially to meet people exactly where they are.

**There's genuinely no shortcut to becoming a better presenter — you improve by actually presenting, repeatedly.** Consider volunteering to speak at a local user group or meetup — plenty of speakers you admire today started in exactly that kind of low-stakes local setting. Committing to give a talk is also a strong forcing function for learning something new — the deadline itself pushes you to genuinely understand the material well enough to explain it.

**Practice is essential.** Run through your talk with your pet, if nothing else, just to get a real sense of timing — and recruiting an actual test audience gives you genuinely useful feedback. Presentations, like code, evolve with iteration — you'll often stumble onto a valuable insight you weren't even looking for. Sometimes the *real* value of a talk turns out to live somewhere unexpected — one of this book's authors built a talk comparing React and Angular, and after delivering it three or four times, realized it could be generalized into a much broader talk about comparing *any* two competing technologies.

Plenty of excellent resources exist to sharpen your speaking skills specifically. Many cities (and some large companies) have Toastmasters chapters — some even focused specifically on technical presentations. The curriculum teaches genuinely useful technique, but having a genuinely safe space to actually *practice* the craft is the real value.

### Code as a Communication Medium

As a software engineer, code itself is one of the primary ways you communicate with other developers. Good communication skill genuinely extends to how clearly and effectively you write code — code is the ultimate source of truth on any project, and conveying your intent through it clearly matters enormously.

As covered in Chapter 3, write code with the human reader firmly in mind. Good naming, concise structure, and avoiding needless cleverness all directly serve whoever inherits this code after you. Optimize for the human reading your code.

> "Any fool can write code that a computer can understand. Good programmers write code that humans can understand."
> — Martin Fowler, British software developer, author, and international public speaker on software development

Nearly every other document on a software project goes stale shortly after it's written. Code doesn't — it's the actual source of truth, the single most up-to-date documentation you genuinely have. From well-written tests to disciplined coding practices, take real care to make sure your code communicates clearly on its own terms.

Communication is a genuinely massive topic, and this only scratches the surface. As your career progresses, you'll gradually learn and master different corners of this whole spectrum — and being a genuinely effective communicator will serve you throughout. It may not always feel natural, but the investment consistently pays off.

## Enterprise Operator

You've probably played the game "Telephone" — sitting in a circle, one person whispers a phrase to their neighbor, who whispers it to the next person, and so on, until the final person announces (often very different) message out loud.

Unsurprisingly, the message drifts as it passes hand to hand — someone mumbles, a listener unconsciously interprets rather than repeats verbatim, someone substitutes an unfamiliar phrase for something more familiar to them. **The exact same thing happens inside organizations, with shocking regularity.** Someone misspeaks in a meeting; someone else hears selectively what they wanted to hear. At some point, despite your best communication skills, you'll find yourself caught in a real-world game of "enterprise operator" — you can't fully prevent it, but you can be genuinely prepared for it.

This tends to show up most often around company-wide standards. Don't be surprised to hear something like "Alice said we're out of compliance," or "I heard Foo is now the corporate standard." Is there actually a real standard? Does it genuinely apply to your specific application? Don't hesitate to ask for the receipts.

**What do you actually do when you find yourself inside this exact scenario?**

1. **Stay level-headed, and recognize it's happening.** There's often real noise, and some people in a genuinely frustrated mood.
2. **Trace where the message actually broke down.** Did someone hear only part of the original story? Pinpointing exactly where things went sideways tells you precisely where to focus your correction.
3. **Identify who's actually involved.** Is there a stakeholder you weren't previously aware of who now needs to be looped in directly? Reach out to them proactively.
4. **Run a real retrospective afterward.** Was there anything genuinely different you could have done to prevent this in the first place?

> ### Story: Dealing with a Hostile Room
>
> *Told by Nate.*
>
> Years ago, I helped establish a best practice around client-rendered user interfaces — genuinely common across the industry at the time. With smartphones, tablets, and other form factors proliferating, applications could no longer be built for one fixed screen resolution — instead, they evolved into backend services acting as JSON "pumps," with the UI adapted client-by-client. Not a particularly controversial idea, but it still required real teaching and evangelizing to get our whole portfolio genuinely aligned on it.
>
> A few months later, a chief architect from another division asked me to present the practice to his team, since they had "some concerns." I ran through my usual presentation. When I opened the floor for questions, one person was visibly frustrated — insisting I was asking them to completely redesign their application around "Windows MVC," and that this was a wildly unreasonable ask.
>
> That genuinely surprised me — I'd never mentioned any specific implementation technology at all. I asked him to walk me through their actual architecture, and it turned out they were already roughly 90% of the way to already complying with the practice.
>
> Digging further, it became clear: one of their business partners had heard *a different* presentation of mine, one that mentioned *JavaScript* MVC libraries. Not knowing what that actually meant, they'd mentally substituted the more familiar "Windows MVC" — and had been genuinely worried the whole team was violating a real standard, based on a completely garbled telephone-game version of my original talk. Once we cleared that specific misunderstanding up, the rest of the meeting turned genuinely positive.

### Know Your Audience

As a software engineer, you'll spend most of your communicating time with other technical people — fellow engineers, architects, testers, product managers. But as your career progresses, you'll increasingly need to communicate technical ideas comfortably with far less technical stakeholders. Learning to translate cleanly from "developer" into "business" is a genuinely important, practicable skill.

**Outside the project room, deep command of technical jargon rarely impresses anyone.** Learn from sales engineers here — their entire job depends on describing genuinely complex technical products in a way that resonates with whoever's actually in front of them, tailoring the message to that specific audience every time. Some vendors are genuinely excellent at "speaking exec" — tying a piece of technology directly back to a concrete business outcome, rather than describing it purely on its own technical merits. That's a skill worth deliberately learning. Decision makers are rarely persuaded by "it's a cool new technology" alone — learn to articulate the actual *business value* it delivers instead.

**How you communicate matters enormously, beyond just the content.** It might feel satisfying to tell someone up your management chain to "just read the manual" — but a cynical, dismissive style rarely correlates with a strong career trajectory, compared to a genuinely less confrontational approach.

Understanding — and being able to clearly articulate — the actual business value of your work matters too. Do you genuinely understand the domain you're working in? Do you know what your business partners actually care most about?

## Practicing Influence

> "Leadership is influence, nothing more, nothing less."
> — John Maxwell, American author and speaker

**Influence** is the art of getting someone to genuinely want what you want — ideally, with them believing it was their own idea all along. As covered in Chapter 9, too many people assume they can simply issue a directive — but unless you're the founder or CEO, you generally can't just order people around. Instead, you have to master the subtler art of genuine influence. From choosing a framework for your next project, to what snacks show up in the break room, opportunities to practice this are genuinely everywhere. So how do you get what you want when you're not the ultimate decision maker?

Every organization has genuinely influential people — not always the ones the org chart suggests. If you're unsure who they are, notice who consistently lands the genuinely interesting assignments. The best managers recognize the limits of their own expertise, and cultivate a trusted circle of experts they lean on for advice. You may not have a direct line to the CTO — but you very likely can work through someone genuinely in their orbit. Influencing the influencers is a real, effective strategy.

### Understanding and Articulating Value

Start by framing the benefits of your preferred option in terms that genuinely resonate with the specific person you're trying to influence — find real common ground, and shape your message around your organization's actual current priorities. Is the decision maker fixated on time-to-market? Developer efficiency? Cutting costs? Don't fight the grain — show clearly how your desired outcome directly serves *their* mission. If you know they specifically want less drama around releases, align your pitch around exactly that.

By the same logic, understand the information ecosystem this person actually lives in. What sources do they genuinely trust? Some leaders lean heavily on a specific analyst firm, or are especially receptive to a particular technology vendor's content. Cite those exact sources when it genuinely helps your case.

### Strategic Approaches to Influence

It's genuinely hard to convince someone to change their mind if they start out disagreeing with you. Two broad approaches exist: **the hammer** (simply ordering someone to do something) and **the ninja** (helping them arrive at your desired conclusion as though it were genuinely their own idea). If you've ever been in a close relationship with another human being, you already know the hammer rarely, genuinely works. Subtlety and gentle nudging get you far further.

Some people resort to sheer volume — yelling louder to bend others to their will. That approach nearly always backfires. Engage in a genuine conversation instead. Don't underestimate the real difficulty of changing someone's mind — it takes real patience and persistence. Start small, demonstrate a genuine early success, and build outward from there.

Your own passion can easily read as aggression to someone else — so take real time to actually listen to whoever you're trying to influence. What are they genuinely saying? What are their real concerns? What language actually resonates with them? Sometimes it's as simple as adjusting your own phrasing to match their expectations, rather than changing your underlying argument at all.

**Your reputation matters just as much as the strength of your ideas.** It speaks for you in rooms you're not actually in — do you have a genuine sense of what yours currently is? If you're unsure, don't hesitate to ask someone directly. You might not love the honest answer — but it's the only real way to actually change it.

Don't be afraid to recruit genuine allies. Reach out to others who already share your view, and work together toward the same goal — there's real strength in numbers, and a second set of eyes may spot an angle you'd genuinely missed.

**Who actually delivers a message can matter as much as the message itself.** Sometimes your preferred outcome needs to come from someone other than you.

> ### Story: The Messenger Matters
>
> One of this book's authors once joined a new company and decided to "make his mark" by introducing test-driven development (TDD). He prepared and delivered a genuinely solid talk on TDD — how it would reduce defects, and why it really wasn't as intimidating as people assumed. It landed with noticeably less warmth than he'd hoped.
>
> A friend on the team — call him Steve — was genuinely eager to bring in more testing, so the author worked closely with Steve's team specifically. Over a few months, that particular corner of the codebase visibly, measurably improved. Seeing the real impact firsthand, Steve then delivered essentially the *same* TDD talk again — almost word for word.
>
> This time, the reception was completely different — near-universal enthusiasm, and genuine momentum to extend the same approach across the rest of the codebase.
>
> **What actually changed?** Everyone already knew and trusted Steve — he had real tenure at the company. The original author was still "the new guy" — why would anyone assume he genuinely knew better? **The messenger genuinely matters.** Who does the actual decision maker naturally turn to for advice? Is that person already aligned with your view? Don't hesitate to work through someone with more established clout inside your organization. It's less personally satisfying than standing in the spotlight yourself — but it can be dramatically more effective.

How you frame things for your specific stakeholders matters just as much. Know which messaging genuinely resonates in *your* organization's culture. Some companies fixate on speed-to-market above nearly everything; others prioritize cost savings above all else. Shape your pitch accordingly — in a startup, emphasize how your approach gets features to customers faster; in a more established company facing real budget constraints, lead with how much it saves.

Wielding genuine influence isn't something most engineers are ever formally taught — but learning to do it well genuinely helps you get things done, and meaningfully advances your career. A core piece of influencing effectively is understanding and actively managing your stakeholders — covered next.

## Stakeholder Management

You need to genuinely know who your stakeholders actually are, and understand them, to communicate with them effectively. Every project has multiple interested parties — teammates, management, and the eventual real users of whatever you're building. Some stakeholders are genuinely obvious — if you're working on the CTO's top initiative, they're clearly, directly involved. Others are far less obvious.

Sometimes there are people not *directly* involved who can nonetheless genuinely help or hurt your project. You can often sense these secondary players lurking in comments like "the VP will never go for that." Sometimes just the *idea* of these people is disruptive enough on its own — negotiating around someone who isn't actually in the room is genuinely difficult, and sometimes the only real fix is getting them physically or virtually present to break the logjam.

**Not every stakeholder deserves the same amount of your time and energy** — this genuinely varies based on their level of interest, and their actual power within the organization. Picture this as a simple two-by-two grid — **power** on one axis, **interest** on the other:

- **Low interest, low power** — genuinely won't be your focus.
- **High power, low interest** — keep them genuinely informed, but respect their time. Give them the information they need to feel comfortable about the project, delivered in whatever format actually suits them, and let them opt into more detail if and when they want it. They can sometimes pivot quickly from low interest to high — transparency is the key move here.
- **High interest, high power** — the people who can genuinely make or break your project's success. Never underestimate their voice when promotions and bonuses are being decided. They can be challenging to work with — packed calendars are a real obstacle — but they can't be ignored, and they'll occasionally surface at the last moment and reshuffle everything. Be genuinely proactive with this group — you may need to adapt your own working style to fit theirs. Have a direct conversation about how you can best work together. And unlike the command line, silence is *not* golden here — keep them genuinely informed, whether through regular demos, concise summaries, or dedicated 1:1 check-ins.
- **High interest, low power** — ignore this group at real peril. They can be genuinely, surprisingly helpful — their enthusiasm is often contagious, and they'll frequently become some of your strongest internal advocates, volunteering to help without even being asked. They may also have real, useful connections to the high-power, high-interest people you're also trying to reach. Whether it's testing a new feature, letting you shadow their workflow, or simply being available for an interview about their needs — this group is genuinely invaluable. Ignore them, and they can quietly work against you instead; keep them genuinely engaged, and things tend to go far more smoothly.

Despite all the zeros and ones underneath, software is ultimately about people. Genuinely understanding the web of stakeholders surrounding your project can be the actual difference between a failed initiative and a real success.

## Time Management

Managing your time is genuinely vital — time is a nonrenewable resource, it doesn't scale, and there's no way to simply create more of it. Demand on your schedule never really lets up: meetings, code reviews, an urgent production issue, whatever status report your organization requires. You must be the active guardian of your own time — don't hesitate to block out genuinely uninterrupted time for your most important work, and give yourself real room to actually think.

**Know your own personal rhythm too** — are you sharper first thing in the morning, or in the early afternoon? Wherever reasonably possible, structure your schedule around when you're genuinely at your best, saving more routine, low-focus tasks for when you know your energy naturally dips. You'll need real flexibility, of course — but working *with* your own rhythm, rather than fighting it, tends to make you both happier and more genuinely productive.

**Giving yourself real space to think can be the actual difference between finishing a feature cleanly and fixing a defect that never should have shipped.** Rich Hickey's well-known talk on "hammock-driven development" emphasizes exactly this — the real value of genuinely uninterrupted thinking time. It's obvious that fixing a bug already in production is the most expensive way to deal with it (exactly why automated tests and QA processes exist) — but the *cheapest* fix, by far, is never introducing the bug at all.

> "Most of the big problems we have with software are problems of misconception. We do not have a good idea of what we are doing before we do it. And then, go, go, go, go and we do everything."
> — Rich Hickey, creator of the Clojure programming language

Problems of *misconception* are genuinely everywhere in software. Terms get overloaded, seemingly-synonymous words turn out to carry critical, meaningful differences. Blocking out real time gives you the space to genuinely think through the consequences of a design before you've committed to it in code. Take the time it takes, so it takes less time overall. It's also worth recognizing that different roles genuinely operate on different kinds of schedules — there's a real, structural difference between people who spend their day *making* (software engineers) and people who spend it *managing*.

### Maker's Schedule

Software is closer to a genuine craft than a pure science — it demands what Paul Graham calls a **maker's schedule**. A *manager's* schedule is essentially a chain of interchangeable hour-long meeting blocks — literally any given hour is functionally identical to any other, which is exactly why managers naturally default to a "find an open slot on my calendar" approach to scheduling. That works genuinely well for reaching consensus on a decision, or hashing out a budget proposal. It works far worse for actually building software.

**As a software engineer, what unit of time do you genuinely think in?** A full day? A half-day? Your working day isn't naturally a series of clean one-hour blocks. A single 30-minute meeting dropped at 10 a.m. can quietly wreck an entire morning, by chopping your remaining time into fragments too small for anything meaningful. There's little point even *starting* something genuinely hard, knowing you'll have to abandon it and context-switch for a poorly timed meeting anyway. You must be the guardian of your own schedule here. That's the essence of a maker's schedule.

Software fundamentally requires loading an entire problem into your head — building a genuine mental model of the whole system. Think about debugging: setting breakpoints, re-running the program, re-triggering the issue, carefully tracing through internal state — and then, *bang*, someone interrupts you. The careful mental stack you'd built collapses instantly. How long does it genuinely take to rebuild that exact same state of concentration afterward?

**All isn't lost, though — you can genuinely protect your own focus.** Use technology deliberately in your favor, and don't hesitate to actually turn off interruptions. Close email and messaging apps outright if the notification pull is too strong to resist otherwise. Tune notification settings, and lean on "Do Not Disturb" modes when you genuinely need deep focus.

**Consider restructuring your week itself.** Some organizations set aside specific meeting-free days or blocks, deliberately protecting real focus time for engineers. Clustering your own meetings into one or two specific days concentrates the disruption, freeing up larger, genuinely uninterrupted stretches elsewhere in the week. If your company doesn't already do something like this, propose it as a small, low-risk experiment.

### Staying on Task

Part of genuinely managing your time is staying focused — which is remarkably hard in practice. Between the relentless flow of email and instant messages, on top of the internet's endless supply of distraction, staying genuinely on task is a real, constant struggle. Sometimes that internal tug-of-war is actually a signal the current task can genuinely wait — but it's also worth deliberately bringing more structure to how you work through your to-do list. A clear plan reduces cognitive load, and gradually turns real focus into an actual habit, rather than a constant, exhausting act of willpower.

**Consider the Pomodoro technique** — essentially Agile methodology applied at a very small, personal scale. Pick a single task, set a timer for 25 minutes, and work exclusively on that task. If something else crosses your mind mid-session, jot it down quickly and return immediately to the task at hand. When the timer goes off, deliberately switch contexts and take a short break. Repeat. After roughly four cycles, reward yourself with a longer break.

Other approaches, like the Focus Course, offer additional tools for setting and actually achieving personal goals — and habit-formation techniques (popularized by writers like James Clear) can be genuinely powerful here too. Don't hesitate to consume some genuinely high-quality material *outside* the usual computer-science corner of the internet — a lot of the best thinking on focus and productivity comes from entirely unrelated fields.

There will always feel like too little time in the day to get through everything on your plate. Learning to genuinely, proactively manage your own time helps you focus on what actually matters, and makes you meaningfully more productive in the process.

## Wrapping Up

Soft skills probably weren't the first thing you learned on the road to becoming a software engineer. But effective communicators consistently tend to get promoted — deploying the right technique at the right moment genuinely makes the difference in achieving your goals. Managing your time well helps you stay grounded while everything around you feels chaotic. And however passionate you are about the newest technology, you genuinely can't afford to neglect the soft skills that will keep paying off across your entire career.

## Putting It into Practice

- **Practice presenting.** Volunteer to give a talk to your teammates, or at a local user group.
- **Protect your focus.** Proactively block real time on your calendar for genuine, uninterrupted work and thinking.
- **Run a better meeting.** The next time you schedule one, be more deliberate — include a real agenda, invite only who genuinely needs to be there, and try a length other than the default 60 minutes.
- **Try the Pomodoro technique** for a couple of weeks. Did it genuinely improve your focus and output?
- **Pause before you send.** Before firing off yet another email, consider whether a different channel would genuinely serve the message better.
- **Practice influence deliberately.** Try introducing a new idea, tool, or technique to your team, using the strategies covered in this chapter.
